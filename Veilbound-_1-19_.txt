Veilbound


by moomlucy


From the TG Storytime Archive





Chapter 1





Chapter 1


The sound of metal hitting metal echoed through the stone arena. Bright sunlight poured over the training ground where six of us moved through drills. I stood at the edge, gripping and releasing the shaft of my practice spear. Prince Kaelen and Therrin were at the center, locked in a fast exchange of blows. They fought like they were born to it. I just tried to keep up.



"You’re slowing, your highness," Therrin said with a smirk.



Kaelen dodged and smiled. "And you’re too easy to read."



Marek rolled his eyes and flicked a small flame between his fingers. "Children, please. I could end both your fights before you finish your jokes."



"Only if you don’t trip over your pride first," Joval added, already aiming an arrow that buzzed with wind magic.



We laughed. Even I couldn’t help but smile. Moments like these made the weight we carried feel a little lighter.



Kaelen called us back to focus, and we fell into our routines. Marek showed how his fire could weaken a weapon’s grip. Joval curved his arrow with a breeze. They worked out ideas, pairing skills for the best results. I just kept my footing steady and my eyes sharp.



Not yet. I was still seventeen, and I could still manifest. It was a disappointment for my parents so far, but they hadn't given up hope. Not completely.



"We should be preparing for more than practice," I said quietly. My own voice caught me off guard.



Therrin lowered his sword. "You mean Veylan?"



The air went still.



Kaelen nodded. "Yes. The empire sees the peace pact as broken."



Twenty years ago, Elaria and Veylan had signed a fragile peace treaty sealed by a royal promise: when the time came, Elaria would offer a princess to marry into the imperial line. That princess had been Kaelen's sister. Her life was more than symbolic—it was the living bond between the two nations. But last spring, she died from a strange, wasting illness no healer could stop. Some whispered it was a curse, others a slow poison. Whatever the truth, her death shattered the one thread holding the peace. Without her, the empire claimed the pact was void. And they were ready to act on it.



"They want war," Marek muttered.



The pact was the only thing that had kept them from attacking us outright. Now that it was broken, they saw nothing in their way. Elaria had suffered too much already — first from years of war, then from the famine that followed. The people were tired, the fields thin, and the coffers nearly empty. And now, with the princess gone, we had nothing left to offer them. Only delay. Only hope.



Kaelen looked tired as he put away his sword. He turned to face us. "We can't. Not this time. Our kingdom is too weak. The last war drained us. We rely on food from other lands. Our walls are still broken, and our leaders argue about gold while our soldiers train with dull blades."



All of us boys had been too young to fight in that war. It had started small, with border raids against a southern kingdom, but it grew fast into a full campaign. By the time it ended, thousands were dead and Elaria was left shattered. We had trained, watched, and waited from the sidelines, wishing we could have helped. Wishing we could have proved ourselves.



"So what do we do?" Joval asked.



Kaelen looked toward the palace. "We don't know yet. My father and his mages are still speaking with the Council."



My heart pounded. I gripped my spear tighter.



He said it quietly, but the weight of it still hit us. The others froze. I felt something colder. I understood.



"They're trying to find a way to hold the pact together," Kaelen said. "But we all know time is running out."



He looked at me. Not as a prince to a guard. Not even as a friend. But like someone who knew I would follow, no matter where this led.



The silence that followed felt heavier than armor.



For the first time that morning, I wished the sound of swords would return.



Because I knew that silence meant everything was about to change.



A few days later, as we walked with Kaelen through the castle yard, the morning sun caught on the stone paths and freshly watered hedges. That was when we saw Princess Alerya, the wife of Crown Prince Edren, passing by. She had come from a northern kingdom, one of Elaria's oldest allies. Beside her walked my sister, Sereyna, along with three other noble companions. Sereyna was engaged to the second prince, a match arranged last winter. She spoke of him often in letters, but I’d never seen them together. Now, she walked with her head high, as if she'd already become part of the royal family.



The last of them—I knew her name was Elsin—lingered for a moment, her gaze drifting toward us. No, toward me. She smiled, just a little, like she knew she had caught my eye.



I looked away too fast. My face was warm. I felt... ridiculous. Strange and excited all at once. Like something inside me had caught fire and didn’t know whether to burn or sing.



As we started to head toward the practice yard, Crown Prince Edren stepped into our path. He greeted us with a smile, then placed a hand on Kaelen's shoulder.



"Walk with me," he said.



Kaelen nodded. As the third prince and youngest child, he was last in line for the throne, but he admired his two older brothers deeply. Edren, the eldest, was more than ten years older and already had a son of his own—Kaelen’s nephew, whom he adored.



As Kaelen followed the crown prince, Edren glanced back at us.



"The rest of you are to come as well," he said. Something about his voice made my stomach twist.



We weren’t often summoned. Even though my father was one of the king’s most trusted generals, and we were distant cousins to the royal line, private meetings with the king and queen were rare.



When we arrived, the door guards opened the great doors without a word. Inside the high chamber, all our fathers were already there, standing in quiet tension. The king and queen sat at the head of the room. Around them stood the royal mages—and two others dressed in silver-trimmed robes I didn’t recognize, until I saw the insignia.



They were from the High Council.



The king stood to speak. His voice was steady, but I could hear the weight behind it. He explained again what we all already knew but hadn’t dared to say aloud: the peace pact with Veylan was broken. With the princess gone, there was nothing left to hold it together.



He spoke of the risk plainly. Without the pact, war was not just likely—it was expected. And Elaria could not survive another war. Not now. Not after all we had lost.



He looked at each of us in turn, his gaze heavy, and said, "This kingdom has no more time to wait."



Then he continued, explaining that the Council could no longer deny Veylan's demands. Next week would mark one full year since Princess Elmira's funeral. After that day, the pact would be officially annulled, and with it, any authority the Council had to stop an invasion. Once that line was crossed, there would be nothing to prevent Veylan from declaring war.



The room fell silent. No one moved. No one even breathed.



"Unless..." the king said.



Everyone leaned forward except the mages.



"We have a new princess."



Shock rippled through the chamber like lightning. No one spoke. Eyes widened. Mouths opened, but no words came. For a moment, even thought itself seemed to pause.





Chapter 2





Chapter 2


Shock rippled through the chamber like lightning. No one spoke. Eyes widened. Mouths opened, but no words came. For a moment, even thought itself seemed to pause.



"Princess?" someone whispered.



"How? From where?" another asked aloud.



A few lords exchanged glances, confused and murmuring. One of them, Lord Haren, stepped forward. "With respect, Your Majesty... the empire will never accept this unless the princess is of your own blood. That was the condition."



King Thalen did not look offended. If anything, he seemed tired. He raised a hand for silence, and the room obeyed.



"You are correct," he said. "Which is why the Council offered us an answer. A dangerous one."



His gaze swept over us. I could feel it settle on Kaelen, even if the king wasn’t looking directly at him.



"The Council has reviewed our case," he continued, "and they agree that the pact must be fulfilled to avoid war. But they also determined that we still have a living royal who can fulfill it—one born with the right affinity."



A few gasps escaped.



The king went on, voice low but firm. "They proposed the use of a rare and sacred rite: Soulshape."



Now the mages stirred.



"It is a transformation spell," the king said. "An ancient one. Forbidden in most uses, but not in this case. Not for peace."



One of the Council mages stepped forward, noticing the tension and curiosity on several younger lords’ faces—and the skepticism in their fathers'.



"Soulshape is not a common spell," she explained calmly. "It is both an affinity and a rite. Those born with the Soulshape affinity—called Soulshapers—can manipulate the bond between soul and body. But their powers are strictly controlled. Minor uses are permitted—changing one’s face slightly, softening features, reshaping a scar—if there is consent and Council approval. But larger transformations are forbidden."



She looked across the chamber, letting the weight of her words settle.



"Transforming from one form of life to another—from man to woman, from human to beast, even deep changes of the soul itself—these are considered major acts. Dangerous, irreversible. And even if permitted, they are never done alone. A full transformation of gender, for example, requires over a hundred Soulshapers working in perfect unity. Without them, the spell would collapse, and the cost would be death."



Her tone grew solemn.



"It touches everything—body, voice, instinct, and even essense. That is why it is feared. That is why it is sacred."



She stepped back.



He paused, as if choosing every word with care.



"The ritual, if approved, would change body and spirit alike. It is no illusion. It is real, permanent, and dangerous. It has not been done in over a century."



A noble near the back scoffed, his face red with frustration. "No one survives that! There's a reason it was forbidden! This isn't magic—it's madness. To bend the soul like that... it's not natural."



"Some survive," said one of the robed Council mages, stepping forward. His voice was steady. "Soulshape is forbidden under most circumstances—but unlike dark magic, it is not absolutely outlawed. When done with full consent, for a noble cause, and with Council oversight, it may be permitted. That is why we are here."

He glanced around the room, then added, "When the soul and the magic are aligned, survival is possible. That is why testing is required. That is why we must be sure."



I had heard of dark magic, but it was forbidden even to speak of it. Every now and then, in our magic and history classes, there were vague references—always brief, always shadowed with warning. We never studied it directly. Its affinities were kept from us. Even research was banned. Some said the Council alone kept records, locked away and hidden from every school in the realm.



Rare affinities like Soulshape, on the other hand, were studied in more detail. It was seen as dangerous, yes, but not corrupt. It was rare and powerful—and just barely allowed.



King Thalen nodded. "And we are sure. The one who would undergo the ritual... has already been tested. Privately. The Council confirmed their affinity aligns with the transformation."



He paused again. Then added, "This is not a deception. It is not a trick. It is the only path left."



No one dared to ask the question. Not yet. But all of us knew it was coming.



Who?



Who had been chosen to become the new princess?



It had to be one of the princes, of course. There were only three. But which one? The crown prince was clearly not the choice—he was married, a father, and already bound to the empire’s expectations. That left only two. Prince Eryndor, the second prince? Or the youngest, Kaelen?



One of the Council mages spoke up again, perhaps sensing the silent question. "For the ritual to succeed, the chosen must either possess a neutral affinity—or a feminine one. If the candidate has already manifested a masculine affinity, the transformation would be lethal. The soul and the form must be in harmony, or the Veil will reject the change."



That sealed it. No one said a word, but everyone knew. The second prince bore Ironmind—strong, unbending, unmistakably masculine. He had never declared it publicly, but anyone who had seen him in a duel understood. He was like stone—calm, immovable, and undefeated. The second prince could never survive the ritual.



That left only one name in the room.



And all eyes slowly turned toward Kaelen.



Kaelen was my best friend. He had no known affinity—or at least, none ever spoken of. Royals kept their affinities secret. Even nobles often did, unless they were forced to declare them. Either he hadn’t yet manifested, like me, or he had and had chosen silence. But if he had already shown a masculine affinity, there would be no choice left. The ritual would be impossible.



The mage confirmed it next. "Among the three princes, only one has a neutral affinity. Prince Kaelen."



Gasps spread through the room.



"Even more," the mage continued, "Prince Kaelen is himself a Soulshaper."



A stunned hush followed. Of course. That explained the secrecy. A royal Soulshaper, even a well-intentioned one, could easily become a scandal. The power to reshape a body—or soul—was not something the court would trust lightly.



I turned to look at Kaelen.



Even he looked surprised. Whatever he had hidden since his affinity had awakened, it was no longer his secret. And now far too many people knew.



The mage added, "The success of such a transformation greatly increases if the candidate is a Soulshaper. It has to do with how much Veilflow of that kind the body and spirit can endure. A Soulshaper’s essence is more adaptable to the strain, more in tune with the ritual’s demand. It gives us a fighting chance."



I looked at the prince. He wasn’t saying anything. Maybe he couldn’t. His face held steady, but I knew him better than anyone. His eyes were distant, his hands too still. He was in shock. Frozen. As if the ground beneath him had shifted and he hadn’t yet decided whether to fall or stand still.



And somewhere in my chest, I felt a weight beginning to build.



Then it happened. Despite trying to stay upright, to show strength, Kaelen's legs buckled slightly. He staggered—not quite falling, but clearly unsteady. Two of our comrades, Joval and Marek, standing closest, rushed forward and helped him into a nearby chair.



Queen Avarielle stood quickly. Her voice broke the silence. "What if it doesn’t work? I don’t want my son to die."



The Council mage stepped forward again, folding his hands calmly. "We cannot say the risk is zero," he said. "But we can reduce it. There is one way to strengthen the prince’s chances. He must be joined by a Veilbound."



A hush fell across the chamber. The elders' faces turned pale. They knew the word.



Lord Rennev, father of Therrin—one of my closest friends—stepped forward sharply. "That is not acceptable," he said. "I would never allow it."



I looked at my own father, General Maeren. He said nothing, but the lines in his jaw told me he didn’t like it either.



The mage, noticing the fear and murmuring among us younger ones, spoke gently. "A Veilbound is someone who is magically bonded to another, bound in spirit. When near each other, they can share Veilflow, stabilize one another. It is rare. Sacred."



He turned his eyes back to us. "To ensure the Veil does not overwhelm the prince during the ritual, he must have a Veilbound. One of you will become that anchor."



A cold silence settled over us.



We all understood the weight of what he meant.



And dread crept in, silent as frost.





Chapter 3





Chapter 3


We were taken to the examination hall early the next morning. A cold room made of polished stone, lit by daylight pouring through tall windows. Eight of us stood in silence before two mages from the Council. We were to be tested—evaluated, they said—to determine who might be eligible for the role of Veilbound.



One of us, Serin, was dismissed immediately. He was his family's only heir, and the law forbade heirs of noble blood from taking part in any rite that risked death. His face was pale, but his relief was clear.



That left seven.



One of the Council mages, a woman in deep blue robes, stepped forward. Her voice was calm and measured. "Have any of you already manifested your affinity?"



Three of the boys raised their hands. Without hesitation, each named their affinity—Joval had Embercraft, Marek wielded Windsway, and the third, Deren, had Stonecall. All elemental. All masculine. All disqualified.



"Masculine affinities are incompatible with the Veilbound role," the mage said simply. "You are dismissed."



They left without protest. Joval and Marek, my friends, gave me a final look—some mix of apology and relief—as they turned away with Deren and disappeared through the side door.



Now there were four of us.



Therrin, son of Lord Rennev. Cale, calm and deliberate. Nerran, always eager to prove himself. And me.



We waited in silence as the mages prepared the next phase.



They tested us to make sure none of us had already manifested without knowing. Sometimes affinities reveal themselves quietly, unnoticed. The mages brought out polished stones—one blue, one emerald, and several others of varying hues. When touched by someone with an active affinity, the stones were said to shimmer, glow, or hum softly. We each placed our hands on them, one by one.



None reacted. No light. No sound.



That confirmed it: none of us had manifested yet.



There were other examinations after that—ritual focus, physical endurance, even spiritual alignment. All of us passed. But passing only made it worse. This was the dilemma none of us could speak out loud. We all wanted to be loyal. Some of us, I believe, would have given our lives for Elaria without question. And yet this—this choice—felt different. Why was it so difficult? Why did it feel like stepping forward meant stepping away from everything we were?



Then the mages escorted us to the main hall. It was full—lords, the king and queen, court mages, and even a few foreign observers. I caught sight of my friends—Joval, Marek, Deren. They looked relieved. I was happy for them. They didn’t have to endure this.



The mages stood tall before the gathering and announced that the four of us were candidates to become Veilbound. They didn’t say our names. They didn’t have to. Everyone saw us walk in together.



Lord Rennev was the first to speak, loud and indignant as always. "This is madness. My son is not meant for this."



The king rose, his gaze steady. "Would you give your life for this kingdom?"



Lord Rennev faltered. "Your Majesty, I—"



But King Thalen raised his hand. "I understand. This is more than asking for your life. That, I do understand. But is it more than the lives of tens of thousands of innocent people in a stupid war?"



Silence fell.



He was right. None of us could argue with that.



The king looked out over the room. "This will not be humiliation. It will be honor. You will be cherished by the people."



I was still listening when my eyes turned to Kaelen.



He sat still, his shoulders heavy, his face blank. I knew him. He had no chance to run from this. He wouldn't try. But he looked... defeated. Queen Avarielle sat beside him, her hand on his, but it didn’t help. This was too much. Too heavy. And I understood why. No matter how strong you are, a sacrifice like this still breaks something inside you.



Then King Thalen stood again. "To have a fair selection," he said, "there will be a tournament. All of you are fighters—good ones. So let the fight decide."



A quiet murmur stirred the room.



"You will be paired and you will fight. The losers of the first round will fight again. And the one who loses twice... will become the Veilbound."



He gave us a moment to let it sink in.



"The rest of you," he added, "will accompany the prince and his companion as their special guards."



My heart was beating so hard I could barely hear.



Then the king called for cards.



A steward brought a tray with four simple cards, freshly shuffled.



We stepped forward. I didn’t want to be paired against Therrin—not because he was stronger, though he was, and I had never won against him—but because he was my friend. I didn’t want the two of us to eliminate each other. But then again... if he lost and I lost, we’d be facing each other in the final match. I just hoped that didn’t happen.



Therrin drew first. A red circle.



I stepped up next, trying not to shake. I drew a blue circle.



Good. Not against Therrin.



Cale and Nerran each took the remaining cards. Cale also had a red circle—he would face Therrin. Nerran held the second blue circle, which meant he would face me.



Our opponents were set. Therrin versus Cale. Nerran versus me.





Chapter 4





Chapter 4


The duels were held the next day. There wasn’t much time to prepare. The Council didn’t want this to drag on. The ritual had to begin soon.



The first fight was between Therrin and Cale.



They were given time to select their weapons and armor. Cale chose a long-handled spear with a narrow blade and light leather armor for speed. Therrin, unsurprisingly, picked a sword. He always did. He wore heavier mail than Cale—more protection, less movement—but I knew from experience, he didn’t need to move much to win.



We stood along the edge of the stone ring, watching. Everyone silent.



In truth, I wanted Therrin to win. Not just because he was my friend, though that mattered. The sooner he was out, the better—for both of us. I didn’t want to face him in the second round. But there was another reason, one I didn’t like to admit: I didn’t like Cale. I didn’t know why. There was something about him that felt... off. I’d trained with him, spoken with him, but I never got the sense he was fighting for the same reasons we were.



The bell rang.



Cale moved first—fast, darting in and jabbing low with his spear. Therrin stepped back smoothly, turned the blade aside, and countered with a hard strike toward Cale’s side. Cale dodged, but not fast enough. The tip of Therrin’s sword caught the edge of his armor and tore the leather clean across.



They circled again.



Therrin pressed forward. Cale danced around him, using reach to his advantage. He was clever. But Therrin was solid. Patient. When Cale lunged again, Therrin let the spear pass, closed the gap, and brought the pommel of his sword up into Cale’s ribs.



Cale staggered.



Therrin didn’t give him the chance to recover. He swept Cale’s legs out from under him, dropped a knee to his chest, and pressed the blade lightly to his throat.



The bell rang again.



Cale coughed, then nodded. "Yield."



The match was over sooner than everyone expected.



Therrin had won.



The second fight was mine.



Nerran stood across from me in the center of the ring. He looked eager, focused, maybe even a little overconfident. He always had something to prove. I didn’t. I just wanted to survive.



We were both given the same choices: weapon and armor. Nerran took a broad-bladed axe and strapped on heavy vambraces. I chose twin short swords—lighter, faster, familiar. My armor was simpler than his, more suited for speed than brute force.



The crowd around us quieted.



The bell rang.



He came at me like a storm—straightforward, hard, fast. His axe cleaved the air with raw strength, and I barely got my blades up in time to deflect the first hit. The force of it sent a jolt through my arms.



I stepped back. Sidestepped. Let him overreach. He was powerful, yes, but power meant nothing if it couldn’t land.



He swung again, wide and reckless. I ducked under it and sliced a shallow cut along his side. The crowd murmured, but I didn’t pause to look.



Nerran roared and charged. I dodged. Let him waste his strength.



But he adapted quickly. On his third pass, he feinted left and hooked his foot behind my ankle. I hit the ground hard, and he was on me in a second.



I rolled, bringing a blade up to keep him back. It cut his arm—not deep, but enough.



We both scrambled up. Breathing hard now.



The fight became slower, tighter. More measured. Each of us circling, searching.



I parried another blow and kicked him in the knee. He staggered. I struck again—low, then high. He blocked, barely. But I could see it—he was wearing me down.



I misjudged the next swing. His axe caught my side, even through the armor. Pain flared. I dropped one blade.



He rushed forward, and I tried to step aside, but my balance faltered. He caught me in the chest with the flat of his weapon and knocked me to the ground.



The bell rang. My blade was gone. His axe hovered over me.



"Yield," I said.



I closed my eyes for a second, chest heaving. Nerran offered me a hand, and I took it.



The match was over.



I had lost.



There was a strange look in Nerran’s eyes as he helped me up. As if he wanted to say sorry but didn’t know how—or maybe wasn’t allowed to. It wasn’t smugness. It wasn’t pride. Just a quiet apology, sitting unspoken between us.

-----------------------------



Then came the last fight.



Cale and me.



From watching his match with Therrin, I could tell he wasn’t in top form. I’d never trained with him seriously, not more than a few light rounds. We weren’t close. But I remembered the way he moved in the last fight—cautious, slow. I had a chance.



And yet... the pressure was worse than anything I had felt before. My chest was tight with dread. This wasn’t just a duel. This was the end of the path for one of us.



My father stood by the edge of the training hall. General Maeren. His face was stone, but I could feel his support. Beside him stood my younger brother. He never came to things like this. He rarely showed interest in what I did. His presence unsettled me more than it comforted.



My mother and sister had wanted to attend, but my father said no. He told them it was a private matter. He wasn’t wrong. These duels were kept from the court and public. Only a handful of people even knew they were happening. The risk of sabotage—especially from Veylan—was too great. Other kingdoms might even encourage it. War between Elaria and Veylan would serve many interests.



I tried not to dwell on that.



Therrin gave me a few final words before I stepped in. "Don't overthink it," he said. "He's fast, but you're sharper when you're calm. Fight your fight, not his."



We entered the ring.



This time, I chose a different weapon—one I hadn’t trained with as much, but something I hoped would give me an edge: a curved blade and a buckler. Cale stuck with what he knew: his spear.



The bell rang.



We moved slowly at first. Testing. Feeling. My heartbeat was loud in my ears.



Cale lunged. I caught the spear on my shield and knocked it aside, countering with a quick slash. He stepped back.



Again. Thrust. Block. Step.



The fight dragged. Longer than either of us expected. Neither one of us gaining the upper hand for long.



I could feel the edge slowly shifting in my favor. He was slower than the first round. I landed a solid hit to his shoulder. Another to his thigh.



Then a hard strike with the flat of my blade against his ribs. He sat down hard in the ring, one hand clutched to his side.



I thought it was over.



But then something changed.



He looked up at me—sweat dripping down his face, eyes hollow—and then suddenly sprang forward with renewed speed.



His attacks came faster. Sharper. I blocked and dodged, but I was on the defensive.



He pushed me back. Again and again. I stumbled.



My arms ached. My shield arm went numb. He struck low, then high, a feint that nearly disarmed me.



He was winning now.



But I refused to lose.



I closed my eyes for half a breath. Focused. Dug deep.



And when I opened them, I waited. Deflected. Stepped inside. Let him exhaust himself.



His breathing became ragged. His steps uncertain.



He raised his spear for another lunge—and then collapsed.



Just fell.



I rushed to him, but the fight was over. The bell rang. He didn’t rise.



I had won.



I didn’t believe it. I had won.



I was about to win, then almost lost, but in the end... I had won.





Chapter 5





Chapter 5


The fight was over, but the result was not yet settled.



No sooner had Cale hit the ground than his father and one of the Council mages rushed into the ring. They knelt beside him, checking his pulse, brushing back his hair. The mage laid a hand on Cale’s chest and closed his eyes. A long pause.



"He lives," the mage said at last. "But he’s out of Veil."



Veil?



I blinked. What?



Had he used magic? In the ring? During a sanctioned duel?



My mind raced. He hadn’t manifested. We had all been tested. All four of us. The stones had shown nothing.



Whispers began almost instantly.



How did he channel the Veil?



Had he hidden an affinity all this time?



Was he tested falsely?



I should have been focused, but all I felt was a flood of relief. I had escaped. I had won. I would not become the Veilbound. I was sorry for Kaelen, of course. He had no choice in this. But I had been given a way out. And I had taken it.



Then guilt followed.



I was closer to Kaelen than any of the others. I had trained with him, bled beside him, seen the way he carried himself when no one else was watching. Cale hated him. He made no secret of it. How could he be the one to stand beside Kaelen in the hardest thing he’d ever do?



I would stand at a distance, I told myself. I would protect him from afar. I would keep them both safe.



When we returned to the royal court, Cale was already seated and breathing steadily. A fresh bandage across his ribs.



But then the attention shifted.



Everyone was looking at me.



At first, I thought it was some mistake. Then I saw the mage step forward again.



"During the final match," he said, "Cale manifested an affinity. Windsway.



"It happened during the moment of his recovery—when he surged with speed. That surge was Veilflow. The manifestation was confirmed."



The murmurs grew louder. Even the nobles who had remained quiet throughout the duels now leaned in.



"As such," the mage continued, "he is disqualified from candidacy. The Veilbound must remain unmanifested until the ritual."



He turned his eyes to me.



"Our Veilbound... will be Rynn Maeren."



The room fell into silence.



My heart, which had calmed for a breath, now roared again in my ears.



I was chosen.



---



Despite everything, I was chosen.



Since the announcement



I didn’t want to talk to anyone. I hated everybody.



The king—for his decision. The mages—for their stupid suggestion. My father—for having no power to stop it. The prince—for no reason I could name. My brother—for inheriting everything I was meant to. My mother and sister—because I was about to become like them. And my friends—for escaping a fate I couldn’t.



But you know who I hated the most? Cale. Because he cheated. He tapped into the Veil when none of us were supposed to. He manifested during combat—against the rules, against what was fair. And I had beaten him, cleanly. I was faster, smarter, calmer. I deserved to win. I deserved to walk away free. I had earned my escape, and yet it was taken from me in a single breath.



And worse—I hated myself. For not manifesting earlier. For not being enough. I felt sick. I felt like disappearing.



But then something happened.



As I lay there, eyes closed, drowning in thoughts I didn’t want, I felt something stir inside me. A small light. A flicker. Just something to focus on. I reached toward it, desperate for calm, any calm at all.



It met me.

Together, Kaelira and I walked down the corridor toward the grand hall. The sound of our skirts brushing the stone floor echoed strangely in the silence between us—layers of silk whispering like unfamiliar voices. It was an odd, fluttering sound, one that would've been laughable a few weeks ago. But now, it barely registered. Among all the other strange new things, it was perhaps the least of our worries.



When we arrived, the guards opened the great doors.



Inside, the room was alive with motion. Council mages, generals, diplomats, and knights circled the massive central table, its surface covered in a map of the realm. Colored markers showed routes, escorts, campsites, and risks.



But the moment they saw us, they stopped.



A hush fell like a veil. Dozens of eyes turned. And I held tighter to Kaelira’s hand.



To everyone’s surprise, the king stepped forward to greet his daughter. I dropped into a deep curtsy, the motion tugging at my tightly laced bodice. From the corner of my eye, I could see my former companions—those who had once trained beside me—standing tall at the side of the map like statues in armor. Why is it that I must curtsey in this delicate dress while they stand like warriors, dressed in heavy fabric and steel, part of the plan rather than merely escorted by it?



The king stopped before Kaelira, looking her over from head to toe. I could feel her stiffen beside me, mortified under his gaze.



Then he nodded solemnly. "You are so beautiful, my daughter. You are the jewel of this kingdom. And you so much resemble your late sister."



I had never thought of that. The king’s bond with the late princess had been unshakable. Her death had shattered him for months. Now he had a daughter again, and I saw it—in his eyes, a shine just short of tears. He wanted to say more, I could feel it. But not here. Not in front of everyone. How do I know that? Is this what Stillmark does—let me feel emotions beneath the surface?



I sensed Kaelira’s inner conflict. She wanted to reach for him, to hug her father. Something inside her yearned for it. But she was afraid—afraid of how it would look, how it would feel, after being a prince.



After a long pause, the king gently reached out, took her hand, and drew her forward. Then, with quiet dignity, he leaned in and kissed her forehead.



Lightly.



Fatherly.



The king then escorted his daughter to the side of the map table. As they passed, everyone bowed or curtsied to the princess—everyone except for the queen and the two princes, who offered her quiet, approving smiles.



I tagged along, a step behind, trying to make myself small.



Then the king turned and addressed the hall. "General Maeren," he said, nodding toward my father, "the floor is yours. Share the plan."



My father stepped forward, straight-backed and unshaken, as he always had been.



"There are two columns in this escort," he began, gesturing to the map. "The primary will carry the princess and her Veilbound—Lady Rinya—along the eastern trade road. It is the better-paved, more visible route. We will not hide. The convoy must be seen."



He placed markers—wooden sigils carved in the shapes of our royal crest—along the winding path.



"The core of the procession will include four carriages: one for the princess and Lady Rinya, one for noble support, and two for provisions and healers. Surrounding them will be an honor guard comprised of the seven knights selected by the crown." He looked toward the young men who had once been my companions. "Their role is protection, but also presentation. They are to be visible. Veylan watches even now."



He moved his hand to the edge of the map. "Prince Eryndor will command the rear vanguard. His unit—seasoned knights—will monitor the terrain, scout ahead, and intervene if the front column is blocked."



There was a pause as he moved a second set of pieces.



"A secondary party, three days behind, will carry false banners. A distraction—should any saboteurs attempt to intercept us, they may strike at shadows."



He looked up at the gathered room. "The greatest threat will be during the border crossing. There, we are subject to Veylan’s terrain—and Veylan’s politics. The Council insists we make the crossing during daylight, with visible ceremonial presence, to avoid misinterpretation."



He tapped one final marker near the Veylan border. "If anything happens, this is the place. Our magic defenses will be strongest then, with three Veilcast mages traveling in rotation."



A murmur passed through the court.



Then the king nodded slowly. "Thank you, General. That will be all for now."



Silence returned. And all eyes turned again to Kaelira.



And then, to me.



I wasn’t sure why. Perhaps because I stood beside her. Or because I was part of this story now, not as a knight or a noble’s son, but as something they didn’t know how to define.





Chapter 17





Chapter 17


We packed.



Or rather, the maids packed.



They moved with quiet, ruthless precision—folding, sorting, wrapping. So many gowns. So many delicate things layered in tissue and lavender sachets. I had never owned so many clothes in my life. As the son of a general, I had always been provided for. I had training garments, formal attire, and a few well-cut jackets. But nothing like this. This was not attire. This was a wardrobe. A life packed in silk.



Kaelira must have had even more. I was sure of it. She came from royalty, after all.



My hands were useless. I tried to help, but only got in the way. Eventually, I sat on the cushioned bench by the window, letting it all wash over me.



Then came the goodbyes.



My mother was already crying. She tried to hide it at first—held her head high, smiled, made herself graceful for court—but it didn’t last. Her hands trembled as they touched my arms, smoothing invisible wrinkles in my sleeves. Her voice cracked when she tried to say we’d be safe. But she didn’t lie. She was crying, and she was afraid. Both her daughters were walking into danger.



My father approached Serenya first. He held her close, whispered something into her ear that I couldn’t hear. But then he pulled back, placed both hands on her shoulders, and said with gentle sternness, "Take care of your sister."



That was new.



I had always been the one who took care of her.



Now it was reversed. Now I was the one needing protection. I wasn’t a knight. I wasn’t strong anymore.



He came to me.



He did not clasp my hand like a soldier. He didn’t clap me on the shoulder like a son. He hugged me.



Like a daughter.



And kissed my forehead.



When he pulled away, he looked me square in the eyes. There was no shame in his gaze. Only something ancient and full of knowing.



"Remember who you are," he said quietly. "Not what they call you, or what silk they dress you in. You were born with a soul that listens before it speaks. That steadies others without effort. That weeps where others would shout. That is Stillmark. And now I see; it always was."



I couldn’t speak.



There was a lump in my throat and a heat behind my eyes. I only nodded, clinging to those words like a thread through everything I was still unraveling.



He pressed his forehead lightly to mine.



And then he was gone.



We were leaving.



At the side of the great gate stood the king and queen, speaking quietly with Kaelira. Both princes were present, though only one—Prince Eryndor—was dressed for travel, already in his riding cloak and ceremonial armor.



I watched as the king addressed Kaelira gently, delicately—just as my father had treated me. There was something in the way he looked at her, as if he were trying to memorize her face. If what I sensed yesterday was true, then it must be devastating for him to lose a daughter so soon after discovering her again.



Across the yard stood the seven knights. My old companions. They were already mounted, armor gleaming, banners rolled behind their saddles. Mirna had mentioned that the seven of them had become minor celebrities simply for being chosen to escort us. She said girls across the city whispered their names like they were knights from a ballad.



It sounded ridiculous. Who would swoon over someone like Joval—or Deren, of all people? Cale, maybe. And Therrin... yes, he was handsome. I caught myself and shook the thought away. What was I doing?



They were ready. Alongside them stood Prince Eryndor’s knights—more seasoned, grimmer, already reviewing the route ahead.



Then came the final moment.



Kaelira and I approached the carriage. Though Serenya had her own, she chose to begin the journey with us. Prince Eryndor offered his hand and helped each of us inside with practiced grace.



As I climbed the small steps into the carriage, I glanced back. My eyes met Cale’s—he was smirking, that same insufferable, teasing grin he always had. I looked past him to Therrin, who simply nodded to me. A knight’s nod.



To a lady.



It hurt more than I expected.





Chapter 18





Chapter 18


The carriage rocked gently as it began to roll, wheels groaning beneath the polished wood and velvet lining. We had departed.



Inside, it was just the three of us—Kaelira, Serenya, and me. We sat close, wrapped in layered silence for a time, the reality of movement settling in. Then, as the rumble of hooves and cartwheels surrounded us, conversation stirred.



"Did you notice the changes?" Serenya asked, peeking through the curtain. "There were last-minute additions."



"I counted four mages," Kaelira said softly. "Miranda, of course. And I saw a younger one—Valeen, I think. Then there’s that quiet one in the blue robes—Halren? And someone new... what was his name?"



"Mage Tarsen," Serenya filled in. "He’s from the Western Reach. Specializes in veil shielding."



Kaelira nodded. "That makes four."



"And the knights," I added. "Tavren and Mollian are here—but I also spotted Sir Alric of the Iron Wall. And Sir Cael from the Onyx Guard."



"Cael’s the one with the scar across his jaw?" Kaelira asked.



"That’s him," I confirmed. "I heard he fought off three assassins in the Ice Rebellion."



Serenya smiled. "You’ve been paying attention."



I said nothing to that.



"There are two ladies of the court as well," Serenya added. "Lady Maerel, of course—and Lady Elsin."



I turned at that. "Lady Elsin? She’s coming?"



"Yes," Kaelira said, already grinning. "You didn’t see her?"



"No—I didn’t know she was joining."



"You had a crush on her," Kaelira said, too quickly, too delighted.



I groaned. "Please don’t tell her that. Ever. That’s sad. I mean... she was—she is beautiful. But I can’t... I mean, I wouldn’t... I can’t have her now. In any way."



Kaelira’s smile softened. She didn’t mock me further.



"Why is she coming?" I asked.



"The decoy caravan," Serenya said. "They’re splitting some of the court between the real procession and the decoy to sell the illusion. Someone had to go."



I turned to the window, brushing the curtain aside just enough to peer through.



Outside, Marek rode close to the carriage, his posture stiff, eyes scanning the road ahead. Even in motion, he looked like a man carved from stone—serious, silent, and utterly focused on his duty.



He was one of us.



He still was.



And yet, everything had changed.



A while later, the caravan came to a gentle halt. The sudden stillness made us lean forward slightly from the momentum. We had been riding for several hours, and the sun now hovered just past its peak.



Serenya peered through the curtain. "Why are we stopping?"



A voice replied from just outside the carriage. "Just a short delay," Therrin called back. "The scouts noticed a stretch of bad terrain ahead—marshy earth from recent rain. The wheelmen want to inspect it before we pass. We’ll move again shortly. If all goes well, we’ll reach a village just before the border by nightfall. We’ll sleep there."



"I could sleep now," Kaelira muttered, stretching her back against the seat.



We waited, the sounds of murmuring guards and shifting horses filtering in through the thin carriage walls.



A while after, we heard Therrin dismount and approach the carriage door. He cleared his throat softly.



"Your grace," he said formally, "we are to remain here for at least half an hour. I’ve asked the maids and cooks to prepare a small camp for you nearby. There’s a river just down through the woods and a lovely pool beside it. We thought it might be a pleasant place to rest."



I could only imagine how Kaelira felt hearing Therrin speak that way. We had once been playmates. Then comrades. But now, his tone was so formal, his posture too cautious. It was disappointing—proof that things would never be the same between us and them.



"Down the woods? In these dresses?" Serenya asked, raising an eyebrow. Even in our travel attire, taking careful steps over flat stone was difficult enough. The idea of picking our way down into the trees in layers of cloth seemed absurd.



"We will help you, my lady," Therrin said. "Nerran found a path with a gradual slope. It should be manageable. The maids have already set up a shaded area with seating and refreshments."



I looked at Kaelira. She nodded.



We had been sitting for hours. We could use the air, the space, and maybe a moment of peace.



Therrin helped us out one by one. First the princess, then Serenya, and then me. When it came to my turn, I took his offered hand and stepped gracefully down from the carriage. At first, it was just a courteous gesture—but once on the ground, I realized how much he towered over me. I had lost so much height, so much of my former presence. It struck deep.



Prince Eryndor had unmounted as well. Though he had been riding in the shadows, during the stop he had rejoined his knights. Now he stepped forward and approached Kaelira and Serenya, offering his arms—one for each of them—to escort them through the trees to the blanket spread near the river.



Therrin stepped up beside me and offered his arm.



He smiled—perhaps awkwardly, but also genuinely.



I hesitated at first. But I was afraid the hem of my dress would catch, that I’d trip on a root or slip on a stone. The woods were uneven, the path soft. And I was not as sure-footed as I used to be. I accepted his arm.



It wasn’t far. But it felt like an eternity.My heart raced. Embarrassment? Maybe.



As we reached the blanket, I saw the maids had indeed prepared everything. A small spread of refreshments—fruits, water, chilled tea—rested on silver trays atop folded linen. We each chose a spot to sit.



Serenya sat with her legs tucked slightly to the side, graceful but relaxed. Kaelira followed suit, lowering herself carefully and smoothing her skirts as she did so. I watched them, then tried to copy Serenya’s posture—back straight, knees angled modestly, ankles crossed just so. The dress all but demanded it. Any other way of sitting felt impossible.



Prince Eryndor did not join us. He returned to his knights, who stood in the shade of the woods. I tried to listen in on their conversation, but their voices were too far, lost beneath the wind and the rustling of leaves.



Therrin, meanwhile, had drifted off toward the river’s edge, where Nerran stood skipping stones. I caught a glimpse of the two of them laughing. A quiet nod, a shared glance, and a grin. They were talking about something light—too light for this journey.



Something about it stung. What were they saying? A joke? About me? How ridiculous I looked, perhaps. I tried not to let the feeling fester, but it pulsed under my skin like a bruise I didn’t know how to tend.

**Chapter 19 – Among Ladies**

Not long after we settled at the riverside, the two other noblewomen joined us—Lady Maerel and Lady Elsin. Their arrival was quiet, but unmistakable. Maerel moved with stately command, every step measured, every gesture precise. Elsin, by contrast, floated like a swan—serene, graceful, and utterly aware of the eyes she drew.

"I thought we’d find you all in silence," Lady Elsin said with a soft smile, easing onto the blanket beside Kaelira. "But you look quite comfortable," Elsin added.

She looked radiant in a delicate gown of pearl and pale rose, her hair coiled like sculpture. I found myself suddenly shy at her arrival. Once, I’d had an eye for her—a distant admiration. And now she sat near me, more alike to me than I ever imagined. She was graceful, poised, and dressed as finely as I was. Delicate, like I now seemed to be. How bizarre. How strange.

"Trying to be," Kaelira replied. Her voice carried that careful politeness we’d both been practicing.

"It’s lovely out here," Lady Maerel added, lowering herself onto the far end of the spread. "Almost makes you forget the reason we travel."

They brought with them the scent of sandalwood and polished leather, and with them came the tide of conversation.

Serenya was the first to dive in, picking up a thread about fabric dyes that I couldn’t even follow—something about imported saffron yielding better saturation than Elarian poppy. Lady Elsin responded instantly, her voice light with amusement, commenting on how fickle last year’s dye trends were and how she refused to wear citrus tones ever again. Maerel nodded, adding that bold colors had their place, but elegance lived in restraint.

From there, the conversation flowed effortlessly into the intricacies of gown design. Serenya described a new corset stitching she’d seen in Everis, while Elsin offered details about a dress that unraveled into a shawl with a hidden ribbon pull. I didn’t even know that was possible.

"Layered lace is back," Elsin said. "Apparently Everisian ladies are wearing it on gloves now."

"It’s impractical," Maerel replied, sipping her tea. "But very flattering."

"Did you see the gown Lady Theryne wore during the autumn banquet?" Serenya added. "Three layers of satin and not a single wrinkle."

"That was witchcraft," Elsin replied with a wink. "Or seventeen maids."

"And the neckline," Maerel said. "It was barely there."

"Barely there," Serenya repeated with mock scandal. "Half the hall was staring."

"Half the hall was staring at the sapphires sewn across the hem," Elsin corrected. "That dress probably cost more than a cavalry horse."

They laughed, soft and unhurried. Even Maerel, who carried herself like a carved statue, allowed a quiet chuckle.

They kept going, layering opinions on silk textures and the politics of color during court presentations. It was a world I had never paid attention to. And somehow, they made it sound like war councils—just with more ribbon.

Kaelira and I exchanged a glance.

We were lost.

I tried to listen. I truly did. But they spoke with such ease, such casual elegance, as if these were matters of diplomacy and not ribbons and slippers.

Kaelira leaned toward me. "I don’t even know what half these stitches are."

"I didn’t know people had opinions about neckline geometry," I whispered back.

Elsin must have overheard, because she laughed, soft and airy. "You’ll learn. Trust me, even when you hate it, you start to notice."

Maerel raised a brow. "Some things are not meant to be loved. Only mastered."

"Like swords," I offered dryly.

"Exactly," Maerel said without missing a beat. "But prettier."

They continued talking, and we listened. And maybe that was enough for now.

I didn’t speak much. Kaelira even less. We were still figuring out the new rhythm of our lives. But watching them—three women unafraid of their femininity, unapologetic in their confidence, it stirred something inside me.

In time, we could belong there too.

Amidst the gentle laughter and glint of sunlight off polished silk, Prince Eryndor and Therrin approached. Their presence was quiet, respectful. Therrin bowed slightly, and Eryndor offered a polite smile.

"My ladies," Eryndor said, "I’m sorry to interrupt your privacy, but the scouts have signaled. It’s time to continue our journey."

Hearing him speak like he’d intruded upon a secret meeting finally settled in me. Of course, he had no idea. This was what women talked about in their circles, their safe gatherings. I had never known before. Now I did. I had been part of it. And the ones who had once been my closest friends—those boys—weren’t.

The men offered their hands as we stood. Eryndor helped Kaelira and Serenya with practiced grace. One of the prince's knights stepped forward to assist Lady Maerel. Therrin turned to Lady Elsin and me. He gave me a quiet, sincere smile as he helped me to my feet.

As I stood, the skirt of my dress moved gracefully around me, the light fabric swaying with a whisper as it adjusted perfectly to my slender frame, gravity guiding it with gentle elegance. I hesitated only a second before taking it.

Climbing was easier than sliding down. So we didn’t need much help. Despite that, Therrin stayed close to me in case I needed support. He would have offered his arm, but like all the other ladies in front of me, I needed to lift the hem of my dress slightly with both hands to prevent it from catching underfoot. Therrin, like the other knights and the prince, tried to assist us by holding a protected arm a few inches behind our backs.

As we made our way back to the carriages, Serenya spoke up.

"Would it not be better if we all rode together in the princess’s carriage? There’s room, and the space is more comfortable."

Eryndor nodded thoughtfully. "I see no harm in it."

Therrin, always cautious, added, "It might appear strange if the second carriage is empty."

"No one will know," Eryndor countered. "The windows are curtained."

And so it was decided. Lady Elsin and Lady Maerel would join us.

Serenya looked pleased. I could tell she was relieved. Our cabin had been quiet, too quiet. Kaelira and I barely spoke. Now she’d have companions to lighten the mood.

But for me, the thought felt heavy. More talk I wouldn’t understand. More reminders of how far I still was from their world.

As we approached the carriage, Lady Elsin lingered beside me. She leaned in, voice low enough for only me to hear.

"Don’t worry," she whispered. "I’m quite sure we’ll have fun."

And maybe, just maybe, we wouldn’t always feel like strangers at our own campfire.
