# Veilbound Writing Style Guide

Use this file when writing new chapters or checking old chapters for consistency.

## Core Voice

The story is written in close first person, usually through Rinya. The narration should feel intimate, emotional, and observant. Rinya notices court manners, body language, power, fear, clothing, and the loss of old freedoms.

The style should stay:

- Direct and readable.
- Emotional without becoming melodramatic.
- Focused on lived experience: body, role, fear, duty, shame, loyalty, attraction, and social pressure.
- Grounded in political fantasy rather than modern casual language.
- Clear about transformation as real and permanent, not disguise.

## Current Style Notes

The current chapters use a reflective, internal style. This works well for Rinya because she is constantly comparing her old life with her new one.

Strengths already present:

- Strong emotional interiority.
- Clear sense of gendered social restriction after transformation.
- Good contrast between courtly softness and martial life.
- Good use of the Veilbond to show shared fear and comfort.
- Effective political pressure around Elaria, Veylan, and the marriage pact.

Patterns to watch:

- Too many sentences use the same shape: `Name, descriptive phrase, said...`
- Too many dialogue tags rely on `voice`, `tone`, `expression`, `gaze`, or `eyes`.
- Too many lines explain an emotion immediately after the reader can already infer it.
- Some scenes use repeated soft adverbs: `softly`, `quietly`, `gently`, `firmly`.
- The manuscript currently uses many em dashes. Do not add more.

## No Em Dashes

Do not use em dashes in new writing.

Avoid this:

```text
She turned away - or rather, tried to - before anyone could see her tears.
```

Use one of these instead:

```text
She turned away, or rather, tried to, before anyone could see her tears.
```

```text
She turned away. Or rather, tried to. Before anyone could see her tears.
```

```text
She turned away; or rather, she tried to, before anyone could see her tears.
```

Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead. A simple sentence is usually best.

Also avoid spaced hyphens as a replacement unless absolutely needed. Do not turn every em dash into ` - `. Rewrite the sentence instead.

## Dialogue Style

Dialogue should feel natural and varied. Keep some descriptive tags, but use them rarely, around 10 percent of the time, when the description adds something important.

Avoid overusing:

```text
Lady Maerel, composed and watchful, said, "We should go."
```

```text
Therrin said, his voice low and firm, "Stay behind me."
```

Better options:

```text
Lady Maerel folded her hands. "We should go."
```

```text
"Stay behind me," Therrin said.
```

```text
Therrin stepped in front of me. "Stay behind me."
```

```text
Lady Maerel did not smile. "We should go."
```

Good dialogue rhythm should mix:

- Plain tags: `he said`, `she asked`.
- Action before speech.
- Action after speech.
- No tag when the speaker is clear.
- Interior reaction after the line.

## Dialogue Tag Rules

Use `said` and `asked` most often.

Use tags like `murmured`, `whispered`, `snapped`, or `called` only when the sound matters.

Avoid stacking tags with explanation:

```text
"No," she said quietly, her voice trembling with fear.
```

Better:

```text
"No." Her hands trembled in her lap.
```

Or:

```text
"No," she whispered.
```

If the action already shows the emotion, do not explain it again.

## Variation Bank

When a scene has several lines of dialogue, rotate between different sentence shapes. Do not let every line follow the same pattern.

Use this mix:

- Dialogue first: `"We should go," she said.`
- Action first: `She set the cup aside. "We should go."`
- Reaction first: `The words settled cold in my stomach. "We should go."`
- No tag: `"We should go."`
- Interior response after speech: `"We should go." I wished she had not sounded so certain.`
- Setting interruption: `Outside, the wheels groaned over stone. "We should go."`
- Physical detail instead of tag: `Her fingers tightened around the glove. "We should go."`

Try not to use more than one or two descriptive dialogue tags in a full exchange. Most lines can stand with action, context, or no tag.

## Ways To Say The Same Thing

Use these alternates to avoid repeating the same phrases.

### Instead of "she said softly"

```text
"I know." Her words barely crossed the space between us.
```

```text
"I know." She did not look up.
```

```text
"I know." The answer was small, but steady.
```

```text
"I know," she whispered.
```

Keep the plain version sometimes:

```text
"I know," she said.
```

### Instead of "his voice was firm"

```text
He did not raise his voice. "No one leaves this room."
```

```text
"No one leaves this room." No one argued.
```

```text
His hand came down on the table. "No one leaves this room."
```

```text
"No one leaves this room," he said.
```

### Instead of "her expression tightened"

```text
Her fingers pressed into the silk of her skirt.
```

```text
The smile left her face.
```

```text
She became very still.
```

```text
For a moment, she looked carved from the same stone as the throne.
```

### Instead of "his gaze shifted"

```text
He looked away first.
```

```text
His attention moved to the guards at the door.
```

```text
He no longer looked at me.
```

```text
His eyes found Kaelira.
```

Use `gaze` sometimes, but not as the default word for looking.

### Instead of "I felt small"

```text
The room made me aware of every inch I had lost.
```

```text
Beside him, I seemed made of silk and nerves.
```

```text
I hated how easily the world could make my body feel like a weakness.
```

```text
For the first time, the distance between my old life and this one felt physical.
```

### Instead of "I was afraid"

```text
Fear moved through me before I could name it.
```

```text
My hands forgot how to be still.
```

```text
The thought made the room colder.
```

```text
I wanted to step back, but there was nowhere to go.
```

### Instead of "the silence was heavy"

```text
No one spoke.
```

```text
The court waited.
```

```text
Even the servants seemed to stop breathing.
```

```text
The pause lasted long enough to become an answer.
```

### Instead of "she looked beautiful"

```text
The gown made her impossible to overlook.
```

```text
Every candle seemed to find her first.
```

```text
She looked like the court had been built around her.
```

```text
Beauty was not the right word. It was too simple.
```

Use direct `beautiful` when Rinya is overwhelmed or emotionally unguarded. Use more specific description when the scene is formal.

### Instead of "the Veilbond stirred"

```text
Through the bond, her fear brushed mine.
```

```text
The Veil between us tightened.
```

```text
Her panic reached me before her hand did.
```

```text
A quiet pressure moved through the bond, asking me to breathe with her.
```

```text
For a heartbeat, I could not tell which fear was mine.
```

Do not overexplain the bond every time. Sometimes one sensory line is enough.

## Sentence Shape Rotation

When revising a paragraph, look at the first few words of each sentence. If several sentences begin the same way, change one.

Common repeated starts to watch:

- `I felt...`
- `I looked...`
- `She looked...`
- `He said...`
- `There was...`
- `For a moment...`
- `The silence...`

Possible rotations:

```text
I felt the heat rise in my face.
```

```text
Heat rose in my face.
```

```text
My face warmed before I could stop it.
```

```text
The room seemed to notice my blush before I did.
```

Another set:

```text
There was fear in her eyes.
```

```text
Fear brightened her eyes.
```

```text
She tried to hide it, but fear had already reached her eyes.
```

```text
Through the bond, I felt the fear she kept from her face.
```

## Scene Type Guidance

Court scenes:

- Use restraint.
- Let danger show through pauses, protocol, glances, seating, and who speaks first.
- Keep descriptions precise.
- Avoid too many soft adverbs. Court language should often be controlled rather than soft.

Travel scenes:

- Use physical detail: wheels, dust, horses, cramped carriage space, clothing, weather.
- Keep dialogue shorter.
- Let discomfort and danger interrupt reflection.

Private scenes:

- Let the prose slow down.
- Use touch, breath, hair, clothing, and the Veilbond carefully.
- Keep emotional repetition only when the character is spiraling.

Action scenes:

- Use shorter sentences.
- Name positions clearly.
- Avoid long emotional explanation in the middle of movement.
- Let fear appear through choices and mistakes.

## Revision Targets

When improving old chapters, do not rewrite everything. Aim for these targets:

- Keep about 10 percent of descriptive dialogue tags.
- Remove the rest if they repeat nearby patterns.
- Replace half of repeated `voice`, `tone`, `gaze`, and `expression` phrases with action or direct speech.
- Leave emotional tags when they mark a turning point.
- Keep Rinya's introspection, but reduce repeated statements of the same feeling.
- Remove all em dashes by rewriting the sentence.

## Body Language

Body language is important in this story, but it should not become repetitive.

Use body language when it shows:

- Court restraint.
- Fear being hidden.
- Attraction or embarrassment.
- Power imbalance.
- The difference between old masculine training and new feminine expectations.
- The Veilbond affecting Rinya and Kaelira.

Avoid repeating the same signals too often:

- Eyes lowering.
- Gaze shifting.
- Hands folding.
- Voice softening.
- Expression tightening.
- Posture becoming composed.

When possible, make the gesture specific to the scene.

Instead of:

```text
Her expression tightened.
```

Try:

```text
Her fingers pressed into the carved arm of the chair.
```

Instead of:

```text
His voice was calm but firm.
```

Try:

```text
He did not raise his voice. He did not need to.
```

## Interior Narration

Rinya often explains what she feels. Keep that, but do not repeat the same realization too many times in one scene.

Good interior narration:

- Shows how Rinya interprets her new body and role.
- Connects present events to past identity.
- Reveals discomfort with courtly femininity.
- Shows her loyalty to Kaelira.
- Shows attraction, fear, or shame before she fully understands it.

Avoid explaining the same emotion three ways in a row.

Example to reduce:

```text
I felt small. I felt weak. I felt fragile. I hated how helpless I had become.
```

Better:

```text
I felt small, and I hated how easily the room made that true.
```

## Paragraph Spacing

Between paragraphs, use one single blank line.

Do not use multiple blank lines.

Dialogue may stand alone as its own paragraph. Keep each speaker in a separate paragraph.

## Transformation Tone

The protagonists should be written as fully feminine after transformation. Their bodies, voices, social roles, presentation, and magical harmony have changed permanently.

Important:

- Do not describe Kaelira or Rinya as merely disguised.
- Do not make the transformation feel temporary.
- Their old identities still matter emotionally, but the current social reality is womanhood.
- Rinya can resist, mourn, and feel alienated without the narration undoing the transformation.
- Kaelira and Rinya should remain feminine in appearance and social treatment after the ritual.

## Consistency Checklist

Before finishing a chapter, check:

- Names match the character files.
- Affinities match the guide files.
- Character roles match the current point in the journey.
- Rinya and Kaelira are treated as women after transformation.
- The Veilbound bond behaves consistently: emotional echo, stabilization, shared strain.
- The political stakes remain clear: pact, marriage, Veylan pressure, Elarian weakness.
- Travel group membership is correct for the chapter.
- Mages and ladies present in the scene match previous chapters.
- No source or EPUB artifact remains.
- Paragraph spacing uses one blank line only.
- No em dashes were added.

## Scene Revision Checklist

When revising a scene, ask:

- Can two dialogue tags be removed?
- Can one `voice/tone/expression` phrase become an action instead?
- Can one long introspective paragraph become two shorter paragraphs?
- Is the emotion already clear from action or dialogue?
- Is the same idea repeated in nearby sentences?
- Is there an em dash that should be rewritten?
- Does each paragraph move the scene forward?

## Preferred Prose Examples

Use action to carry emotion:

```text
Lady Maerel set her cup down. "That will not do."
```

```text
Therrin stepped between us and the door. "Stay behind me."
```

```text
Kaelira did not answer at once. Through the Veilbond, her fear brushed against mine.
```

Use plain tags when the line is strong:

```text
"I will not allow this," Eryndor said.
```

Use interiority when the moment needs Rinya:

```text
I knew that bow. I had given it a hundred times as a boy. Receiving it now felt stranger than any gown.
```

## Avoid

Avoid:

- Too many em dashes.
- Modern slang unless the scene intentionally calls for informality.
- Repeating `softly`, `quietly`, `gently`, or `firmly` on nearby lines.
- Explaining every facial expression.
- Long chains of courtly description that delay the action.
- Undoing the seriousness of the transformation.
- Treating Rinya's discomfort as comedy unless the scene is intentionally light.

## Target Feel

The writing should feel intimate, tense, and elegant, but not overdecorated.

Court scenes should feel controlled and dangerous.

Travel scenes should feel exposed and uncertain.

Private scenes between Kaelira and Rinya should feel honest, vulnerable, and bonded.

Action scenes should be clear, physical, and urgent.

The prose should let emotion breathe without explaining every breath.
