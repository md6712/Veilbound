Chapter 25

The luncheon was served for the ladies alone, and I had never attended such an event before.

As men, we would normally have taken our meal by the training grounds, a simple plate in hand, conversation mixed with the sounds of sparring and steel. This was nothing like that.

Here, we sat around a finely set table, each of us in our finest gowns, the gleam of polished utensils and the glint of delicate dishes reflecting the midday light. The table was adorned with small crystal vases holding fresh blossoms, their fragrance mingling with the scent of warm bread and spiced tea. It felt like a different world entirely, one of grace and precise manners, far removed from the rough camaraderie and hasty meals I had once known among the training yards. I could not help but feel slightly out of place, conscious of every gesture, every sip, as though all of it needed to be measured and exact.

The meal itself passed quietly, save for a conversation that began to circle toward Lady Maerel and her past with Sir Alvis. My ears pricked at the mention of his name, for I could not recall ever hearing much of their history.

Lady Anire was the first to venture upon the subject. “I daresay, Maerel, that Sir Alvis remains most devoted to you.”

Lady Maerel’s expression stayed serene, touched only by the faintest trace of amusement, and I could not help but suspect that her history with him ran deeper than I had ever been told.

Lady Maerel folded her hands. “I believe that may be so. Sir Alvis has ever conducted himself as a gentleman of the highest order, yet in all the years since my late husband’s passing, he has not summoned the courage to speak what lies within his heart.”

I found myself wondering how long one might silently cherish another, and whether such forbearance was born of kindness or of fear.

“He was bereaved, but a year passed,” Lady Anire observed.

“Indeed. A wound so recent must be given its due time to mend.”

Evelyne inclined forward, no longer able to hide her curiosity. “Pray, is Sir Alvis the gentleman with the dark brown hair, the one most might call handsome?”

Her earnestness was so transparent that I nearly smiled.

Elsin answered with a light laugh. “He is indeed a handsome gentleman, yet his hair is of a dark blonde hue. The knight with dark brown hair is Sir Alric, similar in name, though in manner most distinct. Sir Alvis is quick with jest and merry word, whereas Sir Alric bears himself with a more reserved dignity.” I took quiet note of the distinction; in such circles, these subtleties mattered greatly. It was indeed true, though I had never paid such matters much mind. My own instinct would be to compare them on the training field, for though both are formidable warriors, my wager would be on Sir Alvis.

A playful glint entered Lady Anire’s eyes. “A most observant young lady. Tell us then, which of the two would you say holds greater favour in your eyes?”

Elsin's cheeks warmed, and I found myself unexpectedly curious as to what her answer might reveal.

Instead of answering, she made the wise choice of steering the conversation elsewhere. "That is of no consequence," she said lightly. "There was, however, someone missing from our company today, and our Lady Rinya seemed most unsettled by it." At once, every gaze turned to me.

Evelyne, ever eager, leaned forward. "Pray, who was absent?"

Elsin answered before I could speak. "Young Lord Therrin was not with us, and it seems our Rinya here was quite put out by it." In truth, my displeasure had nothing to do with his absence, but rather with another matter entirely.

Lady Maerel gave the slightest nod. "Indeed, he was discourteous. Yet, my dear, I am equally certain you noticed Lord Therrin was absent from our party."

"I was, of course, curious as to where he had gone," I replied, "but not so much that I was unsettled."

Elsin’s lips curved into a knowing grin. "I cannot believe that, Rinya. He has been so attentive toward you the last few days. The entire affair with Lord Cale would never have come to pass had Lord Therrin been present."

I could not help but agree with her in part, though I felt her conclusion missed the truth entirely.

Soon the luncheon concluded, and the mid-afternoon light slanted across the castle grounds, yet there was still no sign of Therrin. A quiet worry began to take root in me, one I could not easily name. He was among the few I trusted, and in that moment I realised how much I relied on his presence.

My patience, which I had thought steady, faltered by the time the shadows lengthened. At last, I rose and made my way to the castle’s main yard, where Sir Alvis and Sir Tavren stood deep in discussion. Their words ceased as I approached, and they offered me a bow in return for my curtsy. The exchange had grown natural, though it still felt strange to my bones.

"Uncle, may I have a word?"

Sir Alvis excused himself to Sir Tavren and followed me for a short walk into the gardens. I was conscious of the height difference between us, his tall frame making my own steps feel smaller still, and my posture was confined by the delicate folds of my gown, so unlike his easy, manly attire.

As we walked, he studied me for a moment. "You seem troubled still, my dear. Has the young lord’s company weighed on you so heavily?"

It is strange that I feel this way still, the thought that I could be forced into marriage with him, that I have no future of my own. That is what unsettles me most, the way he spoke as though I were already his property. It still troubles me deeply. I hesitated, then admitted, "It unsettled me more than I care to confess. His words were forward and unkind, and I cannot rid myself of the unease they left behind."

Sir Alvis’s brow furrowed, but his tone was gentle. "You need not bear such discourtesy in silence. Be assured, I will watch more closely." His assurance brought a small measure of relief, quickly mingled with a flush of shame. It made me feel as though I were not enough on my own — that someone must always look out for me now. That, I realised, was the new reality.

Even so, in my present position, having someone to stand between me and the world was preferable to being left utterly alone.

At length, amidst the scent of late blossoms, I looked up at him and asked, "Do you know anything of Lord Therrin’s whereabouts?"

He smiled, calm and reassuring. "Do not fret, dear one. He will return before tomorrow afternoon. The prince has sent him ahead to our next stop, to ride the route and see if it's safe. He will be back before you miss him." His words were meant to soothe, but still I found myself asking, "And if something were to happen to him?"

This kind of worry was new to me, sharp and unfamiliar, yet impossible to dismiss. Sir Alvis added gently, "Do not worry, dear one, Therrin is one of our best. Traveling alone should raise no concern for him. You seem to care for him more than you realise."

-------

By the end of the day, Therrin was not yet back. Just after dinner, I returned to my chamber, only to be summoned by the Princess.

As I joined her, I saw she was already in her sleeping attire, seated at the side of her vanity. Her maid let us in, and it seemed she had been brushing her hair just before we entered.

I curtsied, as propriety demanded, though I was uncertain whether it was necessary when no one else was there to see. Both of us were weary of the endless courtesies expected of our station, yet the newly formed habit compelled me still. To ignore them entirely felt like a quiet rebellion, one I did not yet dare to commit. The gesture felt strange, unnecessary between comrades who had once trained side by side. The Princess rose at once, a soft smile easing her features as she took my hands and drew me close. Soon she was seated again, and I had taken up the brush to tend her hair—a small act of comfort that steadied us both. At last, we dismissed the maids to grant us privacy.

"I only wished to see if you are well," she said softly.

I nodded, yet after a moment’s silence, I told her of what Cale had said and how his words had left me unsettled.

She listened quietly, then spoke in a low, hesitant voice. "I feel much the same as you," she admitted. "There are so many worries pressing on me, and I cannot set them aside."

"What worries weigh most heavily, Kaelira?" I asked, setting down the brush for a moment.

She folded her hands in her lap. "The marriage. The prince of that foreign land. It is said he is proud, quick-tempered, and not known for kindness. I am to be his bride, and yet I have no choice in the matter. How strange it feels to say such words aloud—to call myself a bride when I once stood proudly as a man among men. The thought sits heavily within me, as though I am acting a role that does not belong to me. How can I reconcile the person I was with the woman I have become?"

I could relate to her, of course. My fate had been much the same, though without the promise of a prince. Everything else felt painfully familiar—the lack of choice, the weight of expectation. I nodded slowly, then shifted the conversation toward the thought that lingered most.

I sighed. "It is cruel that choice has been taken from you. You deserve more than duty alone."

She gave me a faint smile. "And yet duty is all that remains. What about you? You spoke of Cale’s words—did they wound you so deeply?"

"They did," I confessed. "He spoke as if I were already his possession. I felt stripped of my own will, as though the path ahead belonged not to me, but to any man bold enough to claim it."

"And did Sir Alvis’s counsel help?" she asked.

"Yes, and no," I answered slowly. "He eased my fears for Therrin, yet his reassurance also reminded me that I must lean on others for protection now. That dependence shames me."

She touched my hand gently. "Do not be ashamed. We both bear burdens not of our choosing. To need help is no weakness."

I looked at her searchingly. "And yet we are left with so little of ourselves. Does it not trouble you?"

"It does," she admitted, her eyes lowering. "I think of who I might have been had I not been born to this fate. Perhaps I could have remained free, like Serenya, choosing my own path.

But no—my life has been written for me."

I felt a sting in my chest. "And mine as well. From trial to ritual, from gown to gaze. None of it feels mine anymore."

Her eyes softened. "We may not have freedom, but we can have each other’s strength."

I nodded. "Yes. That much they cannot take."

We both fell quiet, the air between us heavy yet strangely comforting.

"Do you ever wish to simply run?" she asked suddenly.

"Every day," I admitted. "But I know I cannot. Nor can you."

"No," she said, shaking her head. "Still, the thought lingers."

"Then let the thought stay," I told her. "Even if we cannot act on it, it reminds us that we still dream."

She smiled faintly. "You always find a way to turn sorrow into something bearable."

"Do I?" I asked with a faint, self-conscious laugh. "That is my Veil, remember," I added with a small chuckle. "I feel only that I cling to what little I can."

"And that is enough," she replied. "For both of us."

I felt warmth rise in my chest. "Then let us promise—to hold each other through this."

"I promise," she whispered. And with that, her hand tightened on mine, as if sealing the vow.

After some minutes, we sat together at the edge of her bed, hands entwined. At last she asked, almost in a whisper, if she might embrace me. We did, and the moment seemed to stretch into eternity. It was a sisterly bond, though strange to name it so; brotherly would not fit, but sisterly did. I could no longer hide it from myself—it was clear and undeniable.
