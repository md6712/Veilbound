Chapter 30

The moment the attackers disappeared into the trees, the three strangers who had saved us broke into laughter. One of them, tall and broad-shouldered, who looked a bit older than us, slapped the nearest of his companions on the shoulder. The three exchanged quick high fives, their mood strangely light after such a battle.

Therrin and Nerran, however, could barely stand. They leaned against the side of the shattered carriage, breathing heavily, their swords still trembling in their hands. Dust hung thick in the air, the smell of sweat and burnt Veil lingering around them.

The tall stranger grinned as he approached. “That was quite the fight,” he said. “You two held your ground better than most fighters I have seen.”

Therrin managed a tired smile. “We would not have held it at all without you.”

The second man, slighter in build but with sharp, watchful eyes, nodded. “Then it is good we happened by when we did.”

Nerran straightened a little, sheathing his blade with a slow motion. “You fight like trained knights. Who are you?”

The three exchanged brief looks, and then the tall one, clearly their leader, stepped forward with an easy smile. “No one special,” he said. “Just wanderers passing through. We were headed to the border and saw trouble brewing. When we noticed you had ladies with you, we thought it best to lend a hand.”

Therrin frowned slightly, his exhaustion blurring the line between suspicion and gratitude.

“Still, you came at the perfect time.”

“Pure chance,” the tall man said with a shrug. “The road ahead is not safe these days.

Bandits, deserters, strange folk. You had best travel carefully.”

The third of the strangers, quiet until now, spoke in a softer voice. “Speaking of which, are the ladies unharmed?”

Therrin turned toward the carriage, where Kaelira and I were visible through the broken frame. “They are shaken, but safe,” he replied. “Thanks to you.”

The tall man inclined his head. “Then all is well. Rest a bit. The worst seems behind us, for now.”

He said it lightly, but something in his eyes suggested he did not believe his own words.

Therrin and Nerran came toward the carriage once the fighting had quieted and the strangers had stepped back to give them room. The carriage door was still wedged shut, the frame bent and splintered from the crash. They pushed and pried until the broken panel gave way, letting in a flood of cool air.

Inside, Kaelira and I tried to stand, but our dresses had caught in the cracked boards and twisted roots that had pushed through the earth beneath the wreck. It was awkward and humiliating, needing so much help just to climb out. Therrin offered his hand first, steady and gentle despite his exhaustion. Even then, it took several tries before they managed to free the hem of Kaelira’s gown, and then mine, from the splintered wood.

When we finally stepped out, blinking against the sunlight, the three strangers approached.

Their expressions shifted from mild amusement to surprise as they saw us clearly for the first time. The tall one straightened and gave a respectful bow.

“My ladies,” he said with a bow. “It is our honor to make your acquaintance.”

The other two followed suit, bowing slightly. Something about them felt unusual. Their clothes were plain, travel-worn, yet their manners and the way they spoke carried the ease of noble breeding. They seemed out of place, both common and refined at once.

Kaelira met their gazes with calm composure, and I followed her lead. We both curtsied politely, offering our thanks.

“You have our gratitude,” Kaelira said. “We owe you our safety.”

The tall man smiled. “No debt between travelers, my lady. We were glad to be of help.”

Still, I could not shake the sense that these men were more than they appeared. Then followed a silence.

---

As the tension began to settle, Nerran finally broke the uneasy silence. “Therrin, do you know who they were? The ones who attacked us?”

Therrin shook his head slowly. “No. But they knew our route. Someone must have told them about the change in plan.”

Nerran frowned. “You mean from our own company?”

“It is possible,” Therrin replied grimly. “Someone could have sent word before we split from the main caravan. They knew exactly where to find us.”

That thought hung in the air like a shadow. The idea that one of our own might have betrayed us was too heavy to voice further. Kaelira’s expression hardened as she listened, and I felt the same chill of realization run through me.

“We cannot stay here,” Nerran said finally. “They will return, and next time they will come prepared.”

The tall stranger, the same one who had led the three, nodded. “He is right. You need to move, and quickly. If you follow the road east, about half an hour’s walk, there is a small settlement on the border. Not quite a village, not quite a city, just a quiet stop where travelers rest before crossing.”

Therrin looked wary. “And what would we do there?”

“Find new clothes,” the man said. “You stand out like banners on a hill. Change how you look, and it will be harder for them to track you. New clothes, new faces. Buy what you can and leave quickly.”

I glanced at Kaelira, realizing how right he was. The silks and embroidery of our gowns could be seen from a mile away. Still, the suggestion sounded strange coming from a stranger.

Therrin’s jaw tightened.

“That is nonsense,” he said. “We cannot ask the princess and Lady Rinya to disguise themselves as common travelers.” The tall man was not truly surprised by this answer, though he acted as if he was. I do not know how I sensed it, but I did. I am Stillmark after all.

His expression shifted as he turned toward Kaelira with a look of mock formality. “Oh, we are in the presence of a princess,” he said, bowing with exaggerated grace. “Your Highness.”

The tall man’s smile faded. “Then you must choose, good sir, your pride or your lives.”

For a moment, no one spoke. Even the wind seemed to be still.

Kaelira steadied herself. “He is right. We will do what must be done.”

Therrin exhaled slowly, defeated but still protective. “Very well. We go to this settlement.

But we stay alert.”

The tall man gave a short nod. “Good. Keep close. The road ahead is not forgiving, and I suspect your enemies are not finished.”

The strangers exchanged quick glances before the tall one spoke again. “The settlement lies at the border of Veylan. Once you cross, you will be within their lands. The patrols there are strong, and it is safer than these forests.”

Kaelira straightened at the mention of the kingdom. “Veylan,” she murmured. “So we are close, then.”

The tall man nodded. “Close enough that you will reach the capital in a few days’ time if you keep to the main road.”

Nerran looked uncertain. “We cannot risk drawing attention. Even the border guards may ask questions.”

“True,” the stranger said. “But you will not travel alone. We are headed that way as well, toward the capital of Veylan. If you allow it, we can accompany you as far as our paths align. You can use our horses. The ladies should not be forced to walk.”

Therrin studied them for a long moment. “You would travel with us?”

The man inclined his head. “Yes. It is safer in numbers, and we owe you a measure of protection for what you have endured.”

Nerran shot Therrin a puzzled look, clearly uncertain. “Shouldn’t we wait for the others?” he asked.

Therrin thought for a moment. “We do not know if they survived, and we do not know if the attackers will return. We had better move while we can. We cannot go back. Only forward, it seems. And as for these men,” he glanced at the three strangers, “they have already proven themselves.”

Therrin met his gaze evenly. “They saved our lives, Nerran. Whatever doubts we have, we owe them that much trust.”

The tall man smiled faintly. “Then it is settled. We move as one group until the road parts us. If trouble finds you again, it will have to face us all.”

I said nothing, still watching them carefully. There was something both reassuring and mysterious about these three men. They seemed to belong nowhere, yet carried themselves like people who had seen every corner of the world.

With that, the plan was set. The three strangers began readying the horses while Therrin and Nerran gathered what little could be salvaged from the wrecked carriage. The sun was already beginning to dip toward the western trees, and shadows stretched long across the road that led toward the border of Veylan.
