Chapter 29

The carriage was moving far too fast. Each turn of the wheel sent us swaying from side to side, the walls creaking as if they might splinter apart. Kaelira and I clung to each other for balance, our hands gripping tightly so neither of us would be thrown against the cabin walls. Dina and Mirna held on to the seats, pale and frightened.

My heart pounded. The shouts outside had faded, replaced by the thundering of hooves and the grinding rattle of the wheels over uneven ground. We were fleeing, that much was clear.

The road tilted downward, and the carriage jolted violently. I hit my shoulder against the side and bit back a cry. Kaelira caught me before I slipped from the seat.

“Hold on,” she said breathlessly. “They are trying to get us clear of the fight.”

I tried to look outside, but the motion was too wild. The scenery flashed past in streaks of green and brown. I caught glimpses of tree trunks and branches whipping past the window. We were entering a wooded area, the forest that divided the two kingdoms. If this was the borderland, then our destination was not far.

Still, something was wrong. The wheels screeched as the carriage lurched over a patch of uneven ground. The horses whinnied in panic. I could feel the pull of gravity shift as the front dipped suddenly.

“Something’s wrong!” Mirna cried.

A cracking sound followed, loud and final. The left wheel snapped from its axle, and in the next breath, the entire carriage tilted violently. Kaelira and I were thrown sideways. Dina screamed as the world turned upside down.

We hit the floor hard. The wood splintered, glass shattered, and the sound of tearing metal filled my ears. For a few long seconds, all I could hear was the thunder of hooves fading into chaos and my own heartbeat hammering against my ribs.

Then, everything stopped.

The carriage lay on its side, motionless. Dust and splinters filled the air. I could hear the horses struggling somewhere ahead, their cries sharp and terrified. Kaelira was beside me, bruised but conscious, her hair tangled and her gown torn at the hem. Mirna was trying to push open what remained of the door, while Dina sobbed quietly in the corner.

“Is everyone all right?” I managed.

Kaelira coughed and nodded. “I think so. But the carriage… it is finished.”

She was right. The frame groaned under its own weight, one wheel still spinning uselessly in the dirt. We could no longer move, and the silence that followed was heavier than the fall itself.

For a moment, I simply lay there, my mind reeling. The danger behind us had not been escaped, only delayed. And now, trapped in the wreckage of our own flight, I felt a new dread rising in my chest.

---

Moments later, the sound of hooves approached again, fast and heavy. Before any of us could speak, a shadow moved past the broken door, and then Therrin’s voice came through the splintered wood.

“Are you all right?” he called.

Kaelira pushed herself upright. “We are inside! The door won’t open!”

I heard the scrape of metal, then the door cracked and gave way. Therrin forced it open with his shoulder, his sword drawn, his clothes streaked with dust and sweat. He looked at us briefly, relief flashing across his face, but he did not let us step out.

“Stay inside,” he ordered. “Do not move.”

“What is it?” I asked.

He glanced over his shoulder, eyes narrowing. “Riders. Six or seven of them. They are coming fast.”

Nerran appeared behind him, breathing hard, a smear of dirt across his cheek. “The others are still behind us. It’s just the two of us for now.”

I tried to look past them through the broken window. The forest ahead was dense, the ground dark with fallen leaves. But beyond the trees, I saw movement, riders in dark cloaks and masks, closing in on horseback. The sight sent a chill through me.

Kaelira gripped my hand tightly. “Who are they?”

“I do not know,” Therrin replied. “But they are organized. Keep low.”

He stepped away from the door, his sword gleaming in the slant of afternoon light. His stance shifted, balanced and ready, as he braced himself. Nerran moved to his left, sword ready. They took position together just as the masked riders reached the clearing.

There were seven of them, perhaps more beyond the trees. Two dismounted, advancing carefully, while the rest circled on horseback, their formation tight. I could feel the pressure of their presence even from inside the ruined cabin.

“They are trying to corner them,” Mirna whispered.

My pulse quickened. I wanted to do something, anything, but my body felt too light, too weak to be of use. I had trained all my life with a sword, yet now I could barely lift it.

Therrin moved first. He struck with precision, faster than I had ever seen him move. His blade cut through the air, meeting the enemy with a force that rang through the clearing. It was not mere speed, there was rhythm in his fighting now, something almost otherworldly. The way he shifted from one stance to another, it was as if he were many at once.

Nerran held the left side, his stance solid but lacking magic. Every move he made came from training and instinct alone. He fought bravely, deflecting blows and keeping his balance against the circling riders.

For a moment, the two of them managed to drive the attackers back. Two masked fighters fell, one clutching his arm, another forced to retreat. But the rest regrouped quickly, their movements too controlled, too disciplined to be mere mercenaries.

Therrin’s fighting grew sharper, his blade weaving arcs of light and shadow as if the air itself carried the memory of his strikes. I could not understand how he was doing it. He was fighting differently, better, more precise, and yet I sensed the strain it took from him.

One of the masked men hurled a dagger that shimmered faintly with Veil energy. It missed Therrin by inches and embedded itself into a nearby tree, hissing as it struck.

Magic. They were using magic.

Kaelira gasped. “They broke the wheel, didn’t they? From afar?”

I nodded, realizing it too. “That must be how they stopped us. But it should not be possible at this distance.”

The thought filled me with dread. Only powerful mages could channel Veil energy that far.

Whoever these men were, they were no simple bandits.

Therrin and Nerran were now surrounded. The two fought fiercely, blades flashing in the dappled light. Therrin’s strikes came in bursts of brilliance, every swing echoing like a memory reborn, while Nerran’s steady defense anchored the line. But even with Therrin’s strange, advanced skill, they could not hold all seven off at once. Nerran parried a blow, but not enough to avoid a cut to his shoulder. Dust and leaves rose around them as the battle pressed closer to the fallen carriage.

“Stay down!” Therrin shouted again. His voice carried over the chaos, fierce and unyielding.

I pressed my back against the cabin wall, clutching Kaelira’s hand. My heart raced as the fight raged just beyond reach. And still, I could not shake the thought that something about this fight was wrong, that the enemy was not here to rob or kill, but to capture.

The fight dragged on longer than any of us could have imagined. The sounds of clashing steel grew weaker, slower. Through the shattered window, I saw both Therrin and Nerran losing ground. Their movements had grown heavy, their breathing ragged.

Therrin’s blade no longer carried the same sharp rhythm as before. The echoes of his strange magic were flickering, fading between each swing. I could see the toll it was taking on him, the way his arm trembled with each strike, the way his eyes darted as though searching for strength that was no longer there. Whatever power he had drawn upon, it was draining him fast.

Nerran fared no better. His shoulder was bleeding where he had been cut earlier, and though he held his sword firmly, his parries were growing slower. He had no magic to call upon, only skill and resolve, and both were reaching their limit.

The masked attackers, meanwhile, moved with terrifying ease. Their coordination was unnatural, their blades guided by unseen precision. I could sense it even from where I sat, they were full of the Veil, their bodies humming with energy. They fought like mages who knew exactly how to wield both magic and steel.

Therrin must have realized it too. He stepped back toward Nerran, forcing himself to stay upright. His voice carried across the clearing. “We cannot outlast them. Fall back!”

Nerran nodded weakly. Together, they began to retreat toward the broken carriage. Each step they took was measured, desperate, meant to buy only seconds. The attackers advanced in a tightening circle, confident now, their laughter cruel beneath their masks.

I could do nothing but watch. My heart pounded so hard it drowned out the sound of the forest. I knew what capture meant for Kaelira and for me, and I prayed silently that the men would not fall, not yet, not now.

As the two reached the edge of the clearing, one of the masked riders raised his hand, gathering a pulse of dark light. Veil energy coiled around his palm, ready to strike. Therrin braced himself, lifting his sword in defiance though his body swayed with exhaustion.

Then, without warning, the spell broke apart in a flash of movement. Three figures appeared from the shadows of the trees, clad in worn, earth-colored clothes with rough cloth covering half their faces. They moved with the precision of masters, their blades flashing faster than I could follow. One struck the mage before his spell could release, another spun low, disarming a second attacker with effortless grace.

Kaelira gasped beside me. “Who are they?”

“I do not know,” I whispered, awe stealing my breath.

The newcomers fought as though they had trained together for years, each movement perfectly placed. Therrin hesitated for a moment, stunned, then quickly joined them, his sword finding rhythm again beside theirs. Nerran followed, pushing through his exhaustion. Together, the five of them drove the masked riders back.

The air filled with the clash of steel and the heavy breaths of men fighting for their lives.

The masked riders, once so certain of victory, began to falter. Their formation broke. One shouted an order, and the others began to retreat toward the woods.

“Leave them!” one of them yelled. “We’ll return with more!”

The five did not chase them. Therrin stood still, his chest rising and falling heavily. The three strangers lowered their weapons but kept their eyes on the treeline until the last sound of hoofbeats faded into the distance.

Silence followed. The clearing was a ruin of trampled grass, broken branches, and the lingering shimmer of spent magic.

Only then did Therrin turn toward the carriage. His face was pale, his breath uneven, but his voice was steady when he said, “It’s over. For now.”

But the way his hand tightened around his sword told me he knew,as did I,that they would be coming back.
