Chapter 31

Only half a day had passed since the ambush, and yet it sat in my chest like a stone that refused to shift. The border was close now, close enough that every rider kept one hand near a weapon and every mage rode with sharpened senses. I felt it too, even without casting anything: the tension, the thinning air, the weight of approaching Veylan soil.

We had already changed out of our blood-stained gowns earlier with the help of our maids.

Thank the Veil, the travelers had spare pieces of clothing, mostly rough men’s garments, but usable. Our maids wore those without hesitation. I wished I could have worn men’s clothes again, too, anything that let me sit in a saddle properly, but none of our party would allow it, insisting we couldn’t risk drawing attention. So in the end, Kaelira and I wore the plain servant dresses, and our maids donned the men’s clothing. An odd sight, but safer than the truth.

We weren’t alone anymore.

Pattin, Sedrick, and Judew rode ahead of us with a strange, quiet confidence, smooth for three men who claimed to have barely survived an ambush. They told us one story between them, a single tale about being merchants whose caravan had been overrun. But the more they repeated it, the more the edges didn’t fit together. Their words aligned too neatly. Their tones matched too well. Almost rehearsed.

Worse, they moved like seasoned fighters. It was obvious in the way they held their weapons, in the way their bodies reacted before danger even appeared, in the practiced way they handled magic. None of it matched the story of soft-handed merchants. When I questioned them gently, they only shrugged and said that nowadays, only those who can defend themselves can travel freely.

It wasn’t enough to calm my doubts.

And Judew… Judew watched everything. Too calmly. Like someone trained, not frightened.

Stillmark made it all clearer.

Not detailsjust tension. Something caged beneath their careful smiles.

They weren’t lying to mislead us. They were hiding something.

And Therrin… Therrin trusted them too easily.

Or so I thought.

When I finally confronted him, he didn’t pretend not to understand.

“Rinya,” he saidmy new name still felt strange. Hearing it from Therrin of all people made something twist inside me, as if the world kept shifting faster than I could steady myself.

Lowering his voice so the others wouldn’t hear, “I know. I feel it too. Whoever they are, they’re hiding something. But right now? They saved our lives. Yours and Kaelira’s dignity as well. You know what those bandits would have done had they taken the carriages.”

My stomach tightened.

“So we trust them?” I asked.

“No,” he said. “We usetheir help. Cautiously. We watch them. And we don’t push them away until we’re somewhere safer.”

He looked at me then, not the way he once looked at a comrade, but with something gentler.

“We need help, Rinya. All of us. Even if it comes from strange hands.”

I didn’t like it. But I didn’t press him further. Whatever Stillmark gave me, it wasn’t the courage to fight my own friends.

---

By midafternoon, the towers of Weddelreach rose ahead of uspale sandstone carved into shapes like frozen waves. But we did not ride in a grand column, or with banners lowered in peace.

No prince led us. No knights flanked us.

Therrin, walking ahead with his sword sheathed but ready. Neron, pale and wavering in the saddle, his bandaged side bleeding through again. Pattin, Sedrick, and Judew, the three strangers whose help we could not refuse and could not trust.

And Kaelira and I, forced to ride sideways because of our now maid dresses, clung to the saddles with far less grace than anyone would imagine, and me certain that if the animal so much as startled, I would topple straight to the ground. Nothing about this felt dignified or safe; it was miserable, awkward, and nothing like the travel songs ever promised.

We entered Weddelreach quietly, unnoticed. No one bowed. No one stopped. We looked like a ragged traveling group, exactly what we needed to look like.

The smell of baked clay and river reeds drifted in the air as townsfolk hurried past us, uninterested. Children ran barefoot through alleyways. No one paid us a second glance.

I wished the world would always look at us like that, without expectation.

Inside the city walls, the three men finally stopped. Pattin pointed toward a side street.

“There’s a tailor there. Cheap, fast, and discreet.” He said it too naturally, as if he knew this place far too well.

Too quickly. Too confidently.

Therrin leaned close to me. “Let’s use what help we can,” he murmured. “We need to blend in, and Neron needs a healer as soon as we can risk it.”

He was right. We had no choice.

We entered the shoponly Kaelira and I, not the men. Another soft reminder that we were not men anymore, no matter how much I wished for the comfort of their clothes. Inside were rows of linen dresses, shawls, aprons, simple bodices, and travel skirts. Nothing noble. Nothing fine. Everything that would let us pass unnoticed.

At first, we simply wandered between the racks in confused circles, touching fabrics without any idea what we were looking for. Everything felt strangetoo soft, too light, too meant for women who belonged in such places. Kaelira and I must have looked utterly lost.

A seamstress appeared from behind a curtain, hands on her hips, eyes sharp but kind. “Well now,” she said, voice bright and chatty, “you two look as though you’ve never seen a dress before. Need help finding something?”

I swallowed. “We… need new dresses.”

Her eyes flicked to our borrowed maid gowns, and she let out a knowing hum. “Court service, aren’t you? I can always tell. Those hems are palace-stitched, even if the rest is travel-worn. Come, comelet me show you some options.”

Before we could protest, she bustled around us, pulling down fabrics in soft creams, rose hues, and gentle greens. “You’ll want something simple, but not drab. Practical, but not homely. Oh, and something that won’t snag if a horse joltstrust me, dear, I’ve seen enough accidents.” Then she leaned past us and nodded toward the doorway, where Therrin and Neron stood guard outside like silent sentries. “And with those two young men waiting on you,” she added with a playful smile, “I’m sure you’ll want to look your best for them, hm?”

Kaelira froze. I nearly choked on air. Shock rippled through me so sharply I felt it in my fingertips heat still rushed to my cheeks.

Why did I blush? Why did we?

The seamstress only laughed softly, entirely unaware of the chaos she’d stirred.

Kaelira stood frozen as the woman draped a cream dress against her front. “How about this one?”

“I… I think so,” she whispered, still overwhelmed.

I couldn’t help but nod. It suited her.

“Do you think it fits?” she whispered.

In the same while, I looked around, and I found one. A sage green dress: soft skirts, long sleeves, no embroidery. Plain. Safe. It made me look… normal. Well, my new normal. I still didn’t know whether that comforted or frightened me.

Therrin and Neron picked out their own clothing from a different shop.

Afterward, we headed for a small riverside inn with faded shutters and drooping flower boxes.

“This will do,” Therrin said. He was steady, even exhausted.

Two rooms were bookedone for Kaelira and me, and another for our two maids. I wasn’t sure where the men stayed, and when I asked, Therrin only ruffled my hair lightly and said, “You needn’t trouble your thoughts over such matters.”

It stung more than I expected. In his eyes, I was someone fragile nowsomeone who shouldn’t worry, shouldn’t think too hard. A soft reminder of who I’d become.

Kaelira and I had to share a bed. I had never shared a bed with anyone before. The thought alone made my heartbeat stumble.

As we undressed, our maids helped us into the new night pajamas we’d bought at the tailor simple set of soft linen tops and loose shorts. Comfortable, airy, and unmistakably feminine.

Once we were changed, the maids left us alone in the small, quiet room.

It was strange, lying so close to someone so beautiful, someone I shared such a deep bond with. I could feel her warmth beside me, the soft shift of her breath.

And yet, despite all the strangeness, it felt natural. As if this was how it was meant to be.

We were exhausted, but still we talked a little before sleep.

Lying face to face in the quiet dimness, I hesitated before speaking. My voice came soft, unsure. “Kaelira… may I ask you something?”

She found my hand in the dark, holding it between both of hers. “Anything. We are bound now, you and I. Once brothers… now sisters, I suppose.” Her smile was gentle, almost shy.

That gave me just enough courage.

“It’s about our bodies,” I whispered. “These… strange feelings. I don’t know what’s normal.”

She squeezed my fingers. “You’re not alone. Truly.”

Heat crept into my cheeks as I admitted, “My chest… It’s so soft and sensitive. Even brushing against the fabric, veil, it shifts on its own. And it’s… distracting.”

Kaelira let out a quiet laugh. “Yes. I feel the same. Though yours are slightly bigger.”

I covered my face with one hand. “You noticed?”

“They’re beautiful,” she said simply. “I’m glad I don’t have to manage ones that size.”

My blush deepened, and she laughed softly again.

Then she was the one who grew hesitant. “For me… it feels empty. Down there. And I still struggle to… let go, when I need to. Everything feels messy.”

I nodded, relieved I wasn’t the only one lost in this new shape. “My sister taught me a trick,” I murmured. “So it isn’t so messy.”

Her eyes widened. “Truly? You must show me in the morning.”

I nodded, still red, but smiling.

And in the hush that followed, our hands remained tangled together, warm and steady.

After a long moment, I swallowed hard. “And… the entrance,” I whispered. “The thought that any man could just… do that to me now… it frightens me. I feel like I’m” The words caught, “like I’m made of softness meant to be used. I don’t even know how to say it.”

Kaelira shifted closer until her forehead rested gently against mine. Her breath warmed my cheek. “I feel you more than you think,” she murmured. “Through the Veil, remember? You don’t need to explain it.”

Then her voice trembled. “I already hate the prince I’m meant to marry. To imagine what he might do to me…” Her hand tightened around mine, and suddenly I felt her distress, sharp and overwhelming, pulsing through the bond. It struck my chest like a wave.

Our eyes met in the dim lighthers wide and pleading, mine already softening.

“May I… hug you?” she whispered.

I didn’t answer. I simply moved into her arms, and she into mine. And then I don’t know why I leaned forward and kissed her.

Our lips met softly, uncertainly, yet the moment they touched, a sharp, electrified spark rushed through both our bodies. Not painfuljust startlingly alive. It felt as natural as breathing, as if our bond had always known this and simply waited for us to discover it.

I lingered there, kissing her gently, until I finally opened my eyes.

She was smiling.

We drew close again, arms wrapping around each other, warm and certain.

The night took us soon after.
