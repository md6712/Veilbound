Chapter 26

Therrin returned before midday. News of his arrival spread quickly through the castle halls, and I caught sight of him from the terrace as he rode in, his horse flecked with dust and the edge of his cloak stained from the road. Relief rippled through the servants and guards alike, though no one spoke it aloud. He was greeted at once by Sir Alvis and escorted to the prince’s council chamber.

Shortly after, the luncheon bell sounded. The princess and I joined the other ladies in the hall, though most of our thoughts were elsewhere. The meal was quiet, filled with polite conversation that none of us truly followed. Even Kaelira, who always tried to keep the tone pleasant, seemed distracted. Every so often, we glanced toward the doors, waiting for some word from the meeting.

When the meal ended, the men withdrew to the council chamber. We watched them leave with calm, intent expressions. Therrin was among them, still in his travel gear, speaking in low tones to Sir Alvis and the prince. The heavy doors closed behind them, and the silence that followed seemed to settle over everything.

The rest of the afternoon passed slowly. Kaelira and I returned to her chamber, both pretending to read but neither turning many pages. We could hear footsteps and murmurs from the hall below, the faint movement of servants carrying messages. Waiting felt worse than fear itself.

At last, near the end of the hour, there was a knock at the door. The prince entered, calm but weary. We rose at once.

Kaelira did not curtsy; she simply stood tall and said, “Eryndor.” I, however, bowed my head and murmured, “Your Highness,” as was proper.

“The council has finished,” he began. “We have heard the reports and weighed the risks. Two paths lie before us, the shorter road to the south, and the longer road through Bisiman.

Both have their uses.”

He paused before continuing. “We will divide our company. The main caravan will take the short road. It is smoother and better suited for the carriages and guards. But to draw attention away, a smaller party will travel through Bisiman.”

Kaelira’s brow furrowed. “And who will take the longer road?”

“You, Kaelira,” he said, “and Lady Rinya. You will go with Sir Alvis, Therrin, and a small guard. You will travel ahead through Bisiman and rejoin the main caravan at the western ford. The journey will take two days at most. It is certainly not the best option,” he added, “and that is why it is safer. Those who seek you will not expect you to take that path.”

Kaelira’s lips parted slightly. “We are to be separated from the others?”

“For a short time only,” he assured her. “It will mislead anyone who might be watching the main group. Therrin knows the route well, and Sir Alvis will see to your safety.”

Out of curiosity, I asked who else would guard us. Though I tried to sound calm, a quiet worry crept into my voice. Surely more knights would attend us.

Eryndor’s tone remained steady. “All of Therrin’s men will join him,” he said. I understood then that Therrin had shown promise and now led the other five of our friends and Cale. I can no longer regard Cale as my friend.

My stomach tightened as the thought struck me. “That means Lord Cale will go as well?”

“Yes,” the prince replied. “All of them, and some of my own guards, including Sir Alvis.”

I nodded, though unease flickered within me. Kaelira noticed at once. She rose, walked over to her brother, and whispered something in his ear. He listened, then gave a small nod.

As Kaelira returned to where I stood, she winked, a hint of amusement softening her face. The prince then spoke aloud. “I have remembered that Lord Cale has other matters to attend to and must travel with the main caravan. Instead, Sir Tavren will accompany your party.”

I bowed my head. “Thank you, Your Highness.”

He gave a small nod. “Then rest well tonight. You depart at first light.” He turned and left, his footsteps fading down the corridor.

Kaelira and I stood still for a moment, uncertain what to say. The idea of being apart from the main group unsettled us both.

“So,” Kaelira said, sinking into her seat, “we take the longer road after all.”

“It seems so,” I replied. “But Therrin will be with us, and Sir Alvis too. We will be safe.”

She gave a faint smile. “I suppose I should be grateful. At least it will be quiet.”

The rest of the evening passed in quiet work. We packed our small travel chests and prepared what little we could. Below, in the yard, I could see Sir Alvis and Therrin marking maps under the lantern light while the guards checked the horses. Meanwhile, Cale joined them and spoke briefly with Sir Alvis before leaving in clear annoyance. Perhaps he had just been told he could not accompany the group. Therrin stood aside at first and watched, uncertain. After Cale left, Therrin seemed to question Alvis, who explained something to him. His face darkened in anger, and then he looked up toward the chambers, toward where mine should have been. Thankfully, the lights there were out, for I was still with Kaelira. A moment later, he turned and went to his own quarters. The air was thick with the smell of hay and oil.

When night fell, Kaelira stood beside me at the window. The castle had grown still, save for a few quiet voices near the stables.

At last, she said, “Let us rest. Tomorrow will come quickly.”

We said goodnight soon after. As I lay down, my thoughts lingered on the road through Bisiman, long, quiet, and hidden among the hills.

Tomorrow, we would leave at dawn.

The morning came before the first bell. The castle stirred earlier than usual, the sound of hooves and hurried voices echoing through the courtyard. When Kaelira and I stepped into the hallway, servants were already busy carrying chests and saddlebags toward the stables.

Mist hung low over the yard. Lanterns burned pale in the damp air as Sir Alvis and Therrin oversaw the final preparations. Horses were being fitted with packs, and the small escort meant to accompany us gathered in quiet order. The smell of hay and leather mixed with the faint scent of morning bread from the kitchens.

Kaelira wore a traveling cloak of deep green, her hair tied simply beneath its hood. Beside her stood Lady Maerel, serene as ever in a cloak of soft gray trimmed with silver thread.

Elsin had chosen a dark blue traveling gown, her cloak fastened neatly with a silver pin, while my sister Serenya wore pale cream, her hair braided high in the manner of Elarian court ladies. I had chosen a plain brown cloak, the weight of it strange but comforting over my shoulders. A maid adjusted the clasp while I tried to calm the flutter in my chest. Together we formed a small circle of color against the mist.

Evelyne and her mother, however, were not joining us; their gowns were far more delicate, soft silks and lace meant for the comfort of the hall rather than the strain of travel.

I had not seen the mages of the High Court that morning either. Perhaps they had chosen to travel separately, their route and timing kept secret for safety.

Outside, Therrin was already mounted, speaking with Sir Tavren. Both men looked toward us as we appeared at the steps. Sir Alvis came forward to offer a steadying hand as we descended.

“The horses are ready,” he said. “We will ride at a slow pace until the sun is higher.”

Kaelira nodded. “Thank you, Sir Alvis.”

I looked once toward the main gate where the larger caravan was forming, the carriages glinting faintly through the fog. For a brief moment, I could see the prince among them, giving orders to his captains. Our paths would soon divide.

Elsin, Serenya, and Lady Maerel joined the main carriage, its gold-trimmed panels glinting faintly through the mist. Kaelira and I, however, were placed in a much plainer carriage, one meant not to draw attention. Kaelira, ever daring, had even suggested that we ride with the men on horseback to appear less important. The prince, however, would hear none of it. He only gestured toward our carriage with a quiet insistence that ended the matter.

Prince Eryndor had said, “You will travel in the carriage. I will not have either of you riding among the men. Appearances must be kept.”

I had replied, “Your Highness, it would draw less attention if we rode as common travelers. No one would suspect the princess then.”

He gave a small, patient sigh. “Even so, the court will hear of it. My father would call it shameful. You will ride in the carriage, and that is final."

Once seated inside the carriage, my thoughts drifted toward Therrin. He was busy outside, setting the caravan to motion, his voice carrying brief commands to the men. He glanced toward our side only once, gave a short nod, and then returned to his work. I had not spoken to him since his return, not that I had anything particular to say, yet the silence between us felt strange and heavy.

When the signal horn sounded, Sir Alvis gave the word to move. The guards formed around us, and the small company began to ride from the castle courtyard. I glanced back once more. The prince and his grand caravan were still gathered in the castle courtyard, the carriages shining faintly in the gray light as orders were shouted and banners stirred in the morning breeze. I watched for a moment as the towers faded behind us, then turned my eyes toward the road ahead, the path through Bisiman waiting in the morning mist.
