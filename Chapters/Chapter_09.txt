Chapter 9

The next few days, Prince Kaelen and I remained confined to his chambers. We were bonded now, and the kingdom wasn’t willing to risk anything. We were guarded more tightly than ever. Our training paused. Our world shrank to four walls, quiet meals, and silence.

Finally, the day of the ritual arrived.

The Council mages came to prepare us. We were both given long gray robes—gender-neutral by request. We didn’t want anything that would feel like a costume for what we weren’t yet.

Then we were asked to remove any metal we might be carrying. We had none.

We were instructed to test our bond one final time. We did—and to our relief, it held easily.

The flow was smooth. If our synchronization failed during the ritual, it could kill us.

Then they told us to follow.

As we stepped into the hallway, we realized how high the stakes had become. More than a hundred guards formed a corridor from Kaelen’s door to the great ceremonial hall. Each one silent. Watching.

When we entered the ritual chamber, the hush broke into murmurs. It was enormous—stone walls arched high overhead. The space had been transformed. Multi-level seating wrapped the walls, filled with lords and royals from across the realm and beyond. Dignitaries in formal robes.

Delegates with guarded eyes.

But as we stepped further in, silence fell again.

At the center of the hall, nearly a hundred mages sat in a perfect circle, each in a chair carved with ancient sigils. They had traveled from across the continent, their presence a testament to how rare this was.

We were guided to the center, where two cushions waited. We knelt, facing each other.

I glanced upward. The king and queen sat upon their thrones. My father stood beside them. I didn’t meet his gaze. I couldn’t. I was no longer the son who would follow his path.

Then, from among the circle, a mage in green rose. Old, yet strikingly calm. He walked to us and knelt beside us.

"I am Mage Vistan," he said. "Veilmage of the High Council. I will lead the ritual today."

He spoke kindly, but firmly. "All will go as it must. If at any time your bond feels unstable, signal me. I will halt the procedure. But I do not expect that. You’ve done well."

He smiled faintly. "You may feel dizzy at the end. That is normal."

Then he looked at us both. "Do you consent to this transformation?"

Kaelen and I turned to one another—for the first time in days, really saw each other—and nodded.

"Hold each other’s hands," Vistan instructed.

We did.

Then he returned to his seat.

The room fell deeper into silence.

Even the whispering stopped.

“Bond,” the Veilmage said.

We tried. Stress made it harder, the weight of a hundred eyes pressing down. But slowly, surely, the bond emerged. And it held.

It was beginning.

The world was about to witness one of the rarest rituals in living memory—and one of the rarest magical events known to our age. All eyes were fixed on us, but we didn’t waver. We couldn’t. A single lapse of focus could kill us both. I wouldn't let that happen. Not for our sake—but for the realm, for the children and citizens whose safety now rested on our discipline.

Around us, the Soulshaper mages rose and joined hands—many women, many men, all of them older. None were young. I guessed most were Veilwardens, and perhaps a few were even Veilmages.

Minutes passed, and our bond began to reach harmony. It was like breathing in time, like matching pulses. And then we began to feel it—the slow, unmistakable swirl of Veil gathering around us.

Kaelen looked at me. I looked at him.

The pressure built.

The Veil wrapped around us, the shared magic inside us keeping its rhythm. And then something shifted. A thread of Veil from the circle of mages reached toward Kaelen. Another strand reached for me. It entered us gently, like a breath shared between souls.

I felt it move through me—not as my own Veil did, but something deeper. It wove into my being, not to change who I was, but to reshape my form. I could feel it, in every cell.

Not my essence—but my body.

Transformation had begun.

Moments later, I felt dizzy—just as Mage Vistan had warned. It was normal, he had said. So I let go.

---

When I opened my eyes, there were people all around me. At first, I didn’t recognize anyone through the blur. But then I spotted Miranda, and beside her—my father, my mother, and my sister. She had been watching this.

I tried to sit up. A few paces away, I saw another ritual circle. The king and queen were seated there. Then I remembered—Kaelen. My eyes searched, and in the center of that space sat a beautiful girl. She looked so much like the late Princess Elmira that for a moment, I couldn’t believe it.

Then it clicked. She was Kaelen.

I looked down at myself—and froze. My hair fell in long waves around my shoulders and chest.

My chest... I had breasts. My hands trembled as I reached up to touch my face.

I turned to my sister, wide-eyed in disbelief.

"You’re beautiful," she said softly.

Mage Vistan’s voice cut through the murmuring that had begun. He turned to the king. "The procedure was successful."

King Thalen nodded once, deeply. Then he stood and addressed the hall.

"All of you, leave us. Give Princess Kaela and Lady Maeren privacy to recover."

The crowd stirred. Murmurs grew. Footsteps echoed. But slowly, the great hall emptied.

Only a few remained—my mother, my sister, the queen, several maids, Miranda. Only women remained. My world had changed then.
