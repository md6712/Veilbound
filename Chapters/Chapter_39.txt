Chapter 39

The gates opened slowly before us.

Not with haste, not with warmth, but with the measured certainty of a city that did not need to hurry for anyone.

As we passed beneath the shadow of the walls, I felt something shift inside me. The air was different here. Heavier. Watching.

We had entered Veylan.

And waiting just beyond the gate was a man.

He stood surrounded by guards clad in dark armor, his posture relaxed in a way that felt deliberate rather than natural. His cloak fell perfectly across his shoulders, his gaze sharp, assessing, already fixed on the approaching riders.

“The second prince,” Lady Maerel murmured softly beside us.

Prince Maedric.

He stepped forward as Prince Eryndor dismounted, and the two men greeted one another with formal nods, followed by a brief exchange of words too low for us to hear.

What struck me was not the greeting itself. But what did not happen. He did not look toward the carriages. Not once. Not at Kaelira. Not at me.

It was as if we were not there at all.

I frowned slightly, my fingers tightening against the curtain.

“Perhaps it is their custom,” Lady Maerel said quietly, as if reading my thoughts. “Women of rank are not greeted in the open. Not until the court receives them properly.”

I was not sure if that made it better. Or worse. The signal was given, and the caravan began to move again. We passed fully through the gates. And the city opened before us. It was overwhelming.

The streets were wide, paved in stone far smoother than any road I had seen before. Buildings rose on either side in layers, tall and tightly packed, their upper floors leaning slightly inward as if trying to claim the sky for themselves.

Shops lined the lower levels, their fronts open to the street. Cloth merchants displaying bolts of deep crimson and gold. Smiths hammering glowing metal into shape. Bakers pulling fresh loaves from ovens that filled the air with warmth and scent.

People were everywhere. Not just dozens. Hundreds. Perhaps thousands.

Voices overlapped in a constant hum. Bargaining, calling, laughing, arguing. Children darted between legs and carts. Guards moved through the crowds with quiet authority, clearing a path as our caravan passed.

Fountains broke the rhythm of the streets, water catching the light in shimmering arcs.

And ahead…

The castle.

It rose above the city like a second sky.

Black stone, sharp lines, towers that seemed carved from shadow itself. It was not merely large. It was imposing. Built not just to house power, but to remind all who saw it that power was absolute.

As we approached, I felt it. Kaelira. Her emotions pressed against mine through the Veilbond, no longer contained.

Fear.

Not the sharp fear of battle. Something deeper. Heavier.

She was going to be wed. To a prince she had never met. In a land that was not her own. And only days ago… she had been a man. I understood. No one better than I did.

I shifted slightly closer to her, letting our shoulders brush, offering what little comfort I could without words.

We entered the castle.

The world shifted from movement to stillness.

They were all waiting.

Rows of men and women of rank lined the approach, arranged with careful precision. Silks, velvets, polished armor, jewels that caught the light in quiet flashes. Faces composed, curious, measuring. Some whispered behind gloved hands. Others watched in silence, their expressions unreadable.

There were so many of them.

Too many to know. Too many to understand.

Lady Maerel leaned slightly forward beside us, her voice barely a breath. “That is Lady Verinne of House Solvar… and there, Lord Kethran. I did not expect him.” She paused. “The rest… new to me.”

At the far end, set apart from the rest, stood a man.

Prince Jaelric.

He was handsome. Tall and built, with the kind of presence that drew the eye even when he stood still.

He did not move to greet the carriages. He did not step forward.

He waited.

Prince Eryndor dismounted and approached him. The two men greeted one another, a measured exchange, polite but cold beneath the surface. Too far for us to hear, yet close enough to feel the weight of it.

Then Eryndor turned back. He came to our carriage. The door opened. Light spilled in.

He reached in first for Kaelira. “Sister,” he said, softer now. He offered his hand, and she took it, stepping down with careful grace.

I could still feel her anxiety, a tight, trembling thread through the Veilbond that would not ease.

Around us, the knights moved into place.

Doors opened. Hands offered.

My turn. A hand appeared before me. I looked up.

Cale.

Of course. A faint smirk touched his lips, as if this were some private amusement. My stomach tightened instantly.

I glanced past him, searching. Therrin. He was there, further down the line, helping my sister dismount, too far to reach me now.

No escape. I placed my hand in Cale’s. He helped me down with exaggerated care, fingers lingering a moment longer than necessary. I withdrew as soon as my feet touched the ground.

Nearby, Sir Alvis offered his arm to Lady Maerel, guiding her down with quiet respect.

All around us, the court watched. Waiting. Measuring. Seeing us for the first time.

And this time… there was no curtain left between us and the world.

A murmur spread at once among the gathered ranks. Not loud, not crude, but persistent, like wind moving through tall grass. I caught fragments as they passed from one to another. The Veilbound. The Elarian miracle. The prince who became a princess. Their eyes lingered too long. Some with curiosity, some with thinly veiled fascination, some with something colder I could not name. We were not merely guests. We were spectacle.

And yet, at the center of it all, the princes remained unmoved.

Prince Jaelric’s gaze passed over us as if we were an afterthought, his expression unreadable, almost bored. Prince Maedric said nothing at all. No greeting. No acknowledgment.

Instead, a short exchange followed between them and Eryndor, too quiet to catch, and then Jaelric spoke just loudly enough for those nearest to hear.

“Arrangements have been made for their rest.”

Nothing more.

No welcome.

No courtesy.

With that, both princes turned and left, their retinue following without question, the court parting around them like water.

The murmurs continued behind them, but something in the air shifted.

I never thought the princes would be kind, but this was more than that. Indifferent. Almost impolite.

Prince Eryndor returned to us. “We need to rest,” he said quietly. “Tomorrow you will be presented to the court, and to the king and queen.”

I looked at Kaelira. Through the Veilbond I felt the same weight pressing on her. Poor her.

She had to marry one of the two. From what I had seen, even if she could come to think of a man as a partner, these two were the worst of choices.

I wanted to comfort her, but the words would not come. Not after this.

We were guided away then, through the waiting ranks, toward the inner halls. Thankfully, my chamber was set close to Kaelira’s. Our maids, Mirna and Dina, hurried to us, each carrying what they could of our belongings. The rest, including the dresses, would follow with the men.

Step by step, we were led to our rooms.
