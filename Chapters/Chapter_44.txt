Chapter 44

It was later that day when Kaelira came to me.

By then, the worst of the dizziness had passed, though a lingering weakness remained in my limbs. The Veil within me still felt unsettled, as if it had not fully returned to its place after being pulled so violently in the court. Mirna had tried to make me rest, and though I had resisted at first, exhaustion had forced me into stillness.

When the door opened, I did not need to be told who it was. I felt her.

The bond between us stirred softly, no longer strained as it had been before, but still carrying the echo of everything we had endured.

I turned. She stood at the threshold for a moment, as if gathering herself, and then she came to me.

We did not speak. We simply embraced.

Her arms wrapped around me tightly, almost desperately, and I felt her break against me. The strength she had held in the court was gone now. What remained was raw and unguarded.

She began to cry.

Not loudly, not uncontrollably, but in quiet, shaking breaths that pressed against my shoulder. I held her without hesitation, my arms firm around her, one hand rising instinctively to her back, steadying her as she had needed to be steadied so many times before.

“It is alright,” I whispered softly. “You are safe.”

The words felt simple, but I meant them.

She did not answer at first. She only held onto me as if letting go might undo everything that had just been secured.

After a while, she pulled back slightly, though she did not move far. Her eyes were still bright with tears, her composure not yet fully returned.

“I was so afraid,” she said quietly.

“I know,” I replied.

“I know you do,” she said, almost with a faint breath of relief. “But I need to say it.”

I nodded.

“Then say it.”

She drew in a slow breath, as if steadying herself not for the memory, but for the act of giving it form.

“When they spoke… when they questioned me… when they laughed…” Her voice faltered slightly.

“I thought I would break in front of them. I thought I would not be able to stand.”

My hands tightened slightly around hers.

“I felt it,” I said.

She gave a small, fragile smile. “Of course you did.”

She looked down for a moment, gathering her thoughts, then continued.

“And when the Queen said… that…” She did not finish the sentence, but she did not need to.

I felt it again, the echo of that humiliation.

“I thought it was over,” she said softly. “Not just the court. Everything. I thought we would leave in disgrace, or worse.”

I did not interrupt her.

She needed this.

“And then he spoke.”

There was a shift in her voice when she said it.

“Judew,” she said, more quietly now.

I watched her carefully, sensing how her mood was shifting, both from within the Veil and from the world pressing in around her.

“He stepped forward,” she continued, “and everything changed. They stopped. All of them.”

Her fingers tightened slightly around mine.

“He did not hesitate. He did not falter. He simply claimed it, as if no one there had the right to deny him.”

There was something in her expression now that had not been there before.

Relief.

“I felt…” She paused, searching for the right word. “I felt seen. Not as something they were questioning. Not as something made. But as someone he chose to stand beside.”

I understood that feeling more than I wanted to admit.

“He saved you,” I said quietly.

She nodded.

“Yes.”

Silence settled between us for a moment, but it was not empty. It was steady.

“Are you afraid now?” I asked.

She considered the question carefully.

“Yes,” she said at last. “But not in the same way.”

She looked at me again, more grounded now.

“I am relieved,” she added softly.

I felt something shift at those words.

“Relieved?” I repeated.

She nodded.

“If it had been one of the others…” She did not finish, but her meaning was clear.

Jaelric.

Maedric.

I did not need to imagine that path. I had seen enough of them already.

“Judew is… different,” she said.

I remembered the journey. The quiet conversations. The way she had looked at him then, uncertain but not afraid.

“Yes,” I said slowly. “He is.”

She let out a small breath, something lighter than before.

“I never thought I would say this,” she admitted, “but I am glad it is him.”

The words settled heavily within me.

I understood her reasoning. Anyone would. Compared to the other two, Judew was a far better choice.

And yet… The thought did not sit easily. The idea of being someone’s bride. It was still strange to me. Unsettling in a way I could not fully explain.

If it could happen to her… It could happen to me. That realization came quietly, but it struck deep. It frightened me. Yet, I did not show it. Instead, I gave her hand a small, reassuring squeeze.

“You are right to feel relieved,” I said. “It could have been far worse.”

She nodded.

“Yes,” she whispered. “It could have been.”

We both knew how much worse. For a moment, neither of us spoke. Then she leaned forward again, resting her forehead lightly against mine.

“Thank you,” she said softly. “For being there.”

“I will always be there,” I replied.

That, at least, was something I was certain of. No matter what changed. No matter what came next. We would face it together.
