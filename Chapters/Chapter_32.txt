Chapter 32

The morning light crept through the shutters long before I was ready to wake. Warm gold brushed over the small room, and for a moment, I didn’t want to move. Kaelira lay close beside me, her hair loose over the pillow, her breathing soft and steady. Peaceful, and somehow still strange.

We stirred almost at the same time, finding each other’s eyes already open. For a few quiet seconds, we simply looked-caught between sleep and the memory of last night’s closeness, especially the kiss. Heat rose to my cheeks before I could stop it.

A sudden knock broke the stillness.

Our maids slipped inside, this time without waiting for permission, each carrying folded dresses. They had already risen, somehow looking far more collected than either of us. “Good morning, my ladies,” Mirna said. “We must dress quickly if you wish to leave before the streets grow busy.”

Quickly. That meant another reminder of what our bodies had become.

They helped us sit up, helped untangle our hair, and dressed us in our plain traveling gowns-lighter fabrics, muted colors, nothing that would catch the eye. Mine was the sage-green we had chosen yesterday. Kaelira’s was cream, soft and simple. The maids laced the backs, adjusted the hems, and checked every detail as if we were pieces of fragile glass.

I still wasn’t used to any of it. Every tug of the laces, every gentle correction of posture, every brush of fabric against skin reminded me how recent-and how irreversible-our transformation truly was. The gowns felt foreign, the softness unfamiliar. Even the way my hair fell over my shoulders made me feel like I was wearing someone else’s body. Kaelira met my eyes in the mirror once, and I could tell she felt the same quiet bewilderment. We were learning how to exist all over again.

When we descended the stairs, the men were already waiting. Therrin, Pattin, Sedrick, and the tallest of them, Judew, sat crowded around a small table, arms crossed, legs jittering with impatience. They had clearly been waiting a long while but didn’t dare say a word.

Therrin rose the moment he saw us. “Finally,” he began-and then caught himself. “I mean… you both look well rested.”

I almost laughed.

We stepped outside into the cool air where the horses were ready. During the night, the men had managed to find two extra mounts-thin, worn creatures, but horses nonetheless. The inns here must have been used to travelers selling animals.

The plan had changed.

Only Kaelira and I would ride with the men, just the six of us: Therrin, Pattin, Sedrick, Judew, Kaelira, and me. Nerran was staying behind with our maids. His wound made riding impossible, and he needed rest. But he and the maids had a far more important task: to get a message to Prince Edren, Prince Eryndor or Sir Alvis.

They had to spread word of our new plan, of how we would avoid the main road, slip between cities, and reach the Veylan capital unseen.

Nerran approached Kaelira gently, then me. “Be safe,” he whispered.

Therrin helped us onto our horses, adjusting the reins, tightening the saddles. “We ride slowly,” he said. “You two can’t handle a fast pace yet.”

He was right. Riding sideways again felt clumsy and unstable. Every shift of the horse sent a wobble through my balance.

Our destination was the next city on the smaller trade route, a place Pattin had suggested called Falcrest. A quiet, middle-town between Weddelreach and the great Veylan road. If all went well, we would reach it before nightfall.

We moved through the morning streets at a careful pace, our horses stepping lightly over the cobblestones. The early vendors were just beginning to set up their stalls, turning curious heads as we passed. To them, we must have looked like an odd group-a cluster of weary travelers with mismatched clothing, guarded by men who moved with the quiet alertness of seasoned fighters.

The city slowly faded behind us, replaced first by low stone walls and dry fields, then by the wide sweep of open countryside. Morning fog clung to the earth in pale ribbons, stirred by each slow step our horses took. The air smelled of dust and distant river reeds.

Our small company rode together in a loose formation. Pattin, Sedrick, and Judew took the lead, speaking in low voices as if discussing the road ahead. Therrin stayed at our side, guiding our pace, his hand never far from his sword. He scanned every hill, every bend in the path, as though danger could rise from the ground itself.

Kaelira and I rode carefully behind them, our horses slow and patient. Neither of us could ride fast like this, and every jolt reminded me how little control I had in this new body.

Still, with Therrin so close and the others ahead, I felt… safe enough.

Although the three men were still deeply suspicious. I couldn’t shake it. I didn’t understand why Therrin had agreed to leave Nerran behind. Now it was just him… and the three of them. If they chose to turn on us, Therrin alone would hardly be able to stand against all three. The thought gnawed at me. This helplessness felt heavier than ever, the sharp truth that in this shape my help counted for nothing.

I had tried to argue with him earlier, tried to make him see the danger. He only smiled-that soft, patient smile he used when he thought I was worrying too much-and said, "There is no other choice. We must travel lighter, and Nerran needs rest more than any of us. He cannot fight like this. He needs healing, not more roads."

Easy words for him to say. Harder for me to accept. But in the end, I had no say in the matter. Therrin made the decision, and I could only follow.

The day stretched long and warm. Birds skimmed the tall grasses. A breeze tugged at our shawls. And for once-mercifully-nothing happened. No ambush, no shouting, no sudden magic.

Just the steady rhythm of hooves on the dirt road.

By Therrin’s estimate, we would reach Falcrest by evening, with time to find an inn before the gates closed.

After several hours of slow riding, fatigue crept into our legs and backs. Therrin finally called for a break. We stopped beside a quiet river on the outskirts of a thick woodland-the edge of what locals simply called the Greenwall Woods.

Kaelira and I dismounted carefully, stretching our aching muscles while Therrin helped steady the horses. Pattin, Sedrick, and Judew took seats on the smooth stones near the riverbank, a little distance away, speaking among themselves.

I kept watching them with wide, alert eyes. Every movement, every glance, every whisper.

Suspicion clung to me like a second skin. Therrin, Kaelira, and I sat together under the shade of a willow, and I barely blinked as I tracked the three men.

At some point, Judew, the tallest of them-rose and walked toward us. I tensed instantly.

He stopped a few paces from me, hands open in a calm gesture. “You have been staring at us as though we climbed out of the river dead,” he said. “I thought I should at least attempt a defense of our honor.”

“I’m watching,” I replied coldly. “Because your story is wrong. Merchants do not move like you. They do not cast like you. And they certainly do not fight like you three fought.”

Judew exhaled, a small, rueful smile appearing. “Yes. Our story is false.”

Therrin’s head snapped toward him. Kaelira stiffened. I felt the air leave my lungs.

Judew lifted a hand. “But we cannot share the real one. Not yet. And we hope you can respect that privacy.”

His tone was even, polite-too polite to push back against without seeming discourteous. It stopped my next accusation in my throat.

“But,” he continued, “false story or no, our intentions are not harmful.”

I narrowed my eyes. “You know who we are. You know why we are traveling into Veylan lands.

You know what awaits us. How?”

Judew met my stare without fear. “Because everyone knows. The tale of Princess Kaelira and her Veilbound companion has traveled farther than you think. People speak of you in taverns.

In markets. Along the roads. You are not as hidden as you hope to be.”

My stomach tightened.

He went on, “We know who you are. But that does not mean we mean you harm. Some of us simply want to give a hand where we can.”

I felt anger flare. “And for what in return?”

Judew shrugged softly. “Nothing.”

“Nothing?” I echoed. “No one does something for nothing.”

“At times,” he said, quieter now, “men help because pride demands it. We would not have stood aside and watched a princess and her Veilbound be butchered. Or worse.”

The last word landed like a stone in my chest.

Raped.

The truth hovered between us like a shadow. My breath tightened. My hands trembled. Being killed would have been mercy compared to that.

Judew must have seen it in my face. His voice softened. “We did not know you two. But we knew the kind of men who attacked you. And we refused to let them touch you. That is all.”

Silence stretched. The river murmured. Leaves whispered overhead.

Kaelira touched my arm gently. "Rinya…"

I swallowed hard. For the first time, the panic, the what ifs, the unspoken terror flooded through me. We truly could have been taken. Used. Broken.

And these men-whatever else they were-had stopped it.

Judew rose slowly, but before he could turn away, Kaelira stood as well. "Wait," she said.

He paused.

She stepped forward with quiet grace, still learning how to move in this new body, yet carrying herself like the princess she truly was. "Good man Judew, I thank you. Truly. For what you and your companions did for us. We are in your debt."

Judew blinked, surprised. Then, with a respectful bow, he took her hand gently and brushed his lips across her knuckles. "I am honored to be of service to the Princess of Elaria."

I felt Kaelira blush, a soft rush of warmth through the Veilbond that fluttered inside me like startled wings. She hid her reaction behind a polite nod, but I felt it all the same.

Butterflies. Confusion. Something delicate.

"Thank you, good man," she whispered.

Judew released her hand, bowed once more, and returned to Pattin and Sedrick.

Before I fully caught my breath, Therrin was already saddling the horses again. The moment passed like wind through leaves.

Soon enough, we were back on the road.

As we rode, the three men kept a little ahead of us, and Therrin lingered a few paces behind.

It gave Kaelira and me a rare moment to speak freely.

"I still feel strange about these men," I murmured.

Kaelira kept her eyes on the road. "They do not seem dangerous. They have been polite and…

genuinely kind so far."

"Yes, I know. Especially the way you blushed," I said, letting a note of annoyance slip through.

She shot me a look. "I did not blush."

"Oh, you did. I felt it through the Veil as strongly as if it were my own."

Her cheeks colored for real this time. "Shut up. You do the same when Therrin is close."

"That is not true." I sat a little straighter in protest.

At that very moment, Therrin urged his horse closer. Both of us went silent at once, backs stiffening like guilty children caught whispering. And the road ahead felt longer than ever.
