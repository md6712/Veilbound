Chapter 19

Not long after we settled at the riverside, the other two noblewomen joined us, Lady Maerel and Lady Elsin. Their arrival was quiet, but unmistakable. Maerel moved with stately command, every step measured, every gesture deliberate. Elsin, by contrast, floated like a swan, serene, graceful, and utterly aware of the eyes she drew.

Lady Elsin eased onto the blanket beside Kaelira with a soft smile. "I thought we’d find you all in silence. But you look quite comfortable."

She looked radiant in a gown of pearl and pale rose, her hair coiled like sculpture. I found myself suddenly shy at her arrival. Once, I’d had an eye for her, a distant, quiet admiration.

And now she sat near me, more alike to me than I ever imagined. Graceful, poised, dressed as finely as I was. Delicate, like I now seemed to be. How strange. How disarming.

"Trying to be," Kaelira replied. Her voice carried that careful politeness we’d both been practicing, the one meant to sound effortless but weighed with effort.

Lady Maerel lowered herself onto the far end of the blanket. "It is lovely out here. Almost enough to forget why we travel."

They brought with them the scent of sandalwood and polished leather, and with them came the tide of conversation.

Serenya picked up a thread about fabric dyes that I couldn’t even follow, something about imported saffron yielding better saturation than Elarian poppy. Elsin took up the thread at once, amused by how fickle last year’s dye trends had been and declaring that she would never wear citrus tones again. Maerel only nodded and observed that bold colors had their place, but elegance lived in restraint.

From there, it flowed into gown design. Serenya described corset stitching from Everis. Elsin offered details about a dress that unraveled into a shawl with a hidden ribbon pull. I hadn’t known that was possible.

"Layered lace is back," Elsin said. "Everisian ladies are wearing it on gloves."

"It’s impractical," Maerel replied, sipping her tea. "But flattering."

"Did you see Lady Theryne’s autumn banquet gown?" Serenya added. "Three layers of satin and not a single wrinkle."

"Witchcraft," Elsin replied with a wink. "Or seventeen maids."

"The neckline," Maerel said. "It was barely there."

"Barely there," Serenya repeated with mock scandal. "Half the hall was staring."

"At the sapphires across the hem," Elsin corrected. "That gown cost more than a cavalry horse."

They laughed, soft and unhurried. Even Maerel allowed herself a quiet chuckle.

They layered opinions on silk textures and the politics of color in court presentations. It was a world I had never looked at before. Somehow, they made it sound like war councils, just with more ribbon.

Kaelira and I exchanged a glance.

We were utterly lost.

I tried to listen. I did. But they spoke with such ease, such casual elegance, as if these were matters of statecraft, not slippers and sleeve hems.

Kaelira leaned toward me. "I don’t even know what half these stitches are."

"I didn’t know people had opinions about neckline geometry," I whispered back.

Elsin must have overheard, because she laughed. "You’ll learn. Trust me, even when you hate it, you start to notice."

Maerel raised a brow. "Some things aren’t meant to be loved. Only mastered."

"Like swords," I offered dryly.

"Exactly," Maerel said. "But prettier."

They kept talking. We listened. And maybe that was enough for now.

I didn’t speak much. Kaelira even less. We were still figuring out the rhythm of our lives.

But watching them, three women unafraid of their femininity, unapologetic in their confidence, it stirred something in me.

Maybe, in time, we could belong here too.

After a while, Lady Elsin offered me a small pouch of candied violets and leaned closer. "Try one. You can’t be a true court lady if you haven’t ruined your teeth with these."

I smiled faintly and took one.

The laughter, the conversation, the delicate clinking of tea cups and rustle of skirts, none of it felt familiar. But it didn’t feel hostile. Just distant. Like watching another language being performed before me.

Then, amidst the glint of sunlight off polished silk, Prince Eryndor and Therrin approached.

Their presence was quiet, respectful. Therrin bowed slightly, and Eryndor offered a polite smile.

"My ladies," Eryndor said, "I’m sorry to interrupt your privacy, but the scouts have signaled. It’s time to continue our journey."

Hearing him speak as though he were interrupting a secret gathering made me realize, he had no idea. None of them did. This was what women spoke of in their circles, their safe spaces. I had never known before. Now I did.

I had been part of it. And those who had once been my comrades, those boys weren’t.

The men offered their hands as we stood. Eryndor helped Kaelira and Serenya with practiced grace. One of the prince’s knights assisted Lady Maerel. Therrin turned to Lady Elsin and me.

He gave me a quiet, sincere smile as he helped me to my feet.

As I rose, the skirt of my dress flowed around me, fabric whispering against the grass. I hesitated before taking his hand, but the ground was uneven. He helped me anyway.

Climbing back up was easier than coming down. But still, Therrin stayed close. He would have offered his arm, but like the other ladies, I needed both hands to lift my hem to avoid snagging it. He hovered behind, just enough to catch me if I stumbled.

As we reached the carriage, Serenya turned to Eryndor.

"Would it not be better if we all rode together in the princess’s carriage? There’s space, and it would be more pleasant."

Eryndor considered. "I see no harm in it."

Therrin, always cautious, added, "It may look strange if the second carriage is empty."

"No one will know," Eryndor said. "The windows are curtained."

So it was decided. Lady Elsin and Lady Maerel would join us.

Serenya looked pleased. Our cabin had been too quiet. Now she had companions to lighten the mood.

But for me, the thought felt heavy. More talk I wouldn’t understand. More reminders of how far I still was from their world.

As we approached the carriage, Lady Elsin lingered beside me. She leaned in, voice low enough for only me to hear.

"Don’t worry," she whispered. "I’m quite sure we’ll have fun."
