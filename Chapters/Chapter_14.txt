Chapter 14

The next morning, two days before our departure, I was in my chamber. Or rather, imprisoned by it. The walls, once neutral, now seemed too pale, too polished, too full of other people’s expectations. My maid, Mirna, hovered with folded arms and a pile of pastel garments that looked more like sugar sculptures than clothing. I was in no mood.

"Milady, please," she said, but firmly.

The word hit wrong. Everything hit wrong. Being called that was strange, sure, but it wasn’t the word that broke me. It was everything else. Every inch of my body felt foreign. Soft balloons on my chest shifted whenever I moved, refusing to settle, mocking me with their weight. Finding a way to sleep without being crushed or stretched felt like a nightly puzzle I couldn’t solve.

And then there was the absence, between my legs, a quiet void that sometimes felt like a missing limb. I moved differently now, walked differently. Even relieving myself was an ordeal, awkward, unfamiliar, humiliating. What once had been simple was now an exercise in learning how not to cry over porcelain.

My skin was too soft. My clothes were too soft. They clung and whispered against me, a constant reminder of who I had become, or been made into.

So when she called me *milady*, it wasn’t the title that stirred my anger. It was that it tried to pretend everything else was normal. Normal when nothing felt grounded, nothing felt true.

I stared at her, incredulous. "I’m not wearing that."

She lifted the offending article, a ruffled thing in blush and ivory, as if it might change my mind by fluttering more pathetically.

"You can’t go out in your bedclothes," she said.

"Why not? I did before," I snapped. "I used to train shirtless. No one cared then."

"You weren’t a lady then."

"I’m not a lady now."

"You are, my lady," she said, voice faltering only slightly.

That did it. I exploded.

"Then why do I feel like a ghost in a dressing room? You want me to dress up like something I’m not, curl my hair and paint my face like a doll, and smile? For what? For their approval?

For the court to see how well I’ve folded?"

Mirna took a breath, holding the gown closer to her chest like a shield. "Not for them. For you. You deserve to walk in with your head high, not looking like you just rolled out of the stables."

I laughed, sharp, bitter. "Oh, I’d *prefer* the stables. I’d *prefer* my sword and a clean sparring circle. Let them stare at the scars. Let them whisper. That was *me.*"

She approached, slowly, as though not to spook a feral animal. "You can still be that person.

But they won't let you in their halls if you're dressed like a boy who lost a bet."

I had already stormed past her before she finished the sentence, barefoot and half-wrapped in the oversized tunic I had slept in. She gasped behind me. "Lady Rinya! Wait!"

"I’m going to the practice yard."

"You can't go out like that!"

"Why not?"

"Because, because you're *not modest!*"

I paused in the hallway, half-turning, hair a wild curtain over my eyes. "You think modesty ever protected a woman on the battlefield? You think silk makes me invincible?"

She reached for my wrist but didn’t grip it. Her hand hovered, trembling.

"No," she said. "But it might protect *you* today. Just for today. Because they don’t see a warrior when they look at you. They see weakness."

She paused, then added, "Don’t let them see that. These clothes, if you wear them with pride, they hide that. They make them look instead at what you choose to show."

That stopped me.

Not because I agreed.

Because I understood.

She wasn’t talking about dignity. She was talking about survival. And that... I could still understand.

I returned to the room, fists clenched, fury burning under my skin like a sun beneath frost.

"Fine. But not the pink one. Not today."

She smiled, only a little. "Lavender, then? With the higher collar?"

"I swear to all twelve Veils, if there is a bow on the back, I will set it on fire."

---

The *Twelve Veils* are sacred layers of spiritual and mystical truth in this world. They aren’t types of magic, but deeper principles, used in oaths, rituals, and the lore of transformation. Each Veil represents a core force of existence: birth, blood, flame, death, emotion, silence, change, nature, memory, truth, secrecy, and sacrifice. To swear by all twelve is to call on the entire shape of life and soul.

---

"Fair enough," she said.

The dress we chose in the end was lavender, as promised, but it was far from plain. It hugged my waist with silken ease, the bodice embroidered in pale silver vines. The sleeves were sheer and tapered like something out of a dream, delicate to the point of ridiculousness. The skirt billowed with layers of gauze and lace, floating with every breath of movement. A ribbon, yes, a ribbon, cinched the back, though thankfully, it wasn’t a bow. It made me feel like glass. Like a thing too soft to survive the air.

And yet, there was something else. As she helped me into it, Mirna moved with practiced gentleness. Her hands fastened every clasp, adjusted every seam. And as each piece settled against my skin, something else settled in me too. Not peace. Not acceptance. But a kind of pause. A readiness to walk forward, even if I hated every step.

---

We were summoned by the queen, as expected. As we left my chamber, I noticed Sir Mollian standing with two guards outside our door. Tall, armored, sword at his hip, he looked like something carved from certainty.

Immediately as we exited, he stepped close to greet us. "Lady Rinya," he said with a slight bow.

The words hit harder than expected. I felt so small beside the deep blue and steel of the royal guard, beside the practiced composure in every movement he made. I, on the other hand, felt delicate. Fragile. Feminine to a fault in that frilly lavender dress. My skin soft, my posture uncertain. I could barely look him in the eye.

I tried to curtsy in return. I couldn’t bow like him, it didn’t feel right, not in this body, not in this gown.

The gesture felt strange, like muscle memory from a body that had never practiced it. But I held it. I gave the motion as gracefully as I could, as if I had always known how. That’s what they wanted to see.

As we walked the corridor, I felt the eyes of every guard on us. Whether curiosity, pity, or quiet reverence, I didn’t know. Only that I felt exposed. As if my skin were paper and my heartbeat ink. Every step made noise I hadn’t meant.

Mirna walked behind me, adjusting the hem of my dress whenever it caught. She whispered soft reminders about posture, about breathing, about letting the world see strength wrapped in silk.

We reached the princess’s chamber, where the queen was supposed to be. We knocked.

I stood straight, but my fingers trembled.

And then I heard her voice inside.

"Come in."

I swallowed once and opened the door.
