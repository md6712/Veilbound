Chapter 35

We woke late.

Not because the bed was soft, it wasn’t, but because our bodies had finally collapsed from sheer exhaustion. I didn’t even remember falling asleep. Only the crying. The tight, heaving sobs that emptied us until nothing was left. We must have curled into each other and passed out like that, tangled and shaking.

And now… we paid for it.

Kaelira’s hair had turned into a storm.

My own wasn’t much better.

Strands stuck to our cheeks, twisted into knots, flattened in strange angles where our tears had glued them to our skin. It looked like something had clawed through it. Several somethings. Her curls were a wild, tangled halo. Mine was an uneven curtain stuck in dreadful coils.

She groaned when she saw her reflection. “Oh no… no, no, no…”

My stomach sank. “We should have braided it last night.”

“Or brushed it. Or… done anything.”

We hadn’t. We had just cried ourselves into oblivion.

And now two young ladies with delicate hair and reputations to uphold looked like feral swamp creatures.

What unsettled me most was how deeply it mattered. A few days ago, this wouldn’t have even registered as a problem. We would’ve run a hand through it, shrugged, and gone on with our day. But now? After all the hours spent surrounded by women who treated hair as sacred, after maids gasping at a single loose curl, after being tutored in braids and ribbons and propriety, somehow it had become central. Important. I could hardly believe how quickly these expectations had rooted themselves inside us.

We tried to fix it.

It was awful.

Our hair was different now, softer, longer, finer at the ends. More fragile. It didn’t obey the way it had when we were boys. It clung. It knotted. It snarled. Every stroke of the comb caught on something that pulled at my scalp and sparked tears in my eyes.

Kaelira hissed in pain. “Ow, ow! Why is this so difficult?”

“Hold still,” I muttered, working through a knot that clearly wanted to murder me.

We managed. Barely. Not beautifully, but enough to look like we hadn’t slept on the ground during a thunderstorm.

Kaelira was halfway through smoothing one last curl when,

Knock, Knock, Knock,

Sharp. Urgent. The kind of knock that didn’t belong to sleepy travelers or innkeepers delivering breakfast.

“I’ll get it,” I whispered, though my voice felt too small for the moment.

I padded across the cramped room, skirts whispering around my ankles, and opened the door just enough to see who stood on the other side.

Therrin.

His voice, when it came, was low and controlled, but threaded with something thin and brittle, something he was fighting to hide. “Rinya. We have a problem.”

The words dragged the breath from my lungs.

Behind me, Kaelira went completely still, the air around her tightening in an instant.

Therrin continued in a harsh whisper, “There are men in the inn. Skilled ones. They closed off every entrance. They’re asking about two girls traveling with companions. They’re describing us.”

My heart went cold.

I sensed it, beneath his careful mask, Therrin was afraid. His Veilflow vibrated like a taut wire.

Kaelira’s panic shot through the room like a spark. “We’re trapped,” she whispered. Then louder, trembling, “We’re trapped, they’re going to take me, they’re going to take us,”

She spun in circles, breath quickening to the edge of a sob.

Before she could spiral further, I crossed the room and caught her shoulders. Her whole body jolted under my touch, trembling so hard it felt like she might shake herself apart. I guided her in, pulling her into my arms before she could lose her footing.

“It’s okay,” I whispered, soft, steady, the kind of tone I didn’t even know I could make.

It wasn’t.

But somehow, maybe Stillmark, or maybe the shock of freezing my own fear, I stayed calm. Calm for her. A strange stillness unfurled inside me, like someone smoothing a hand over rushing water until it lay flat.

Her breathing steadied against my chest, each inhalation less frantic than the last, the awful edge of panic softening as she anchored herself against me.

A voice cut in from the hallway.

“Curse it all,” Judew said as he strode forward. His expression was grim. “We need to get out. Now.”

“We can’t,” Therrin snapped. “They blocked every exit.”

Judew shook his head. “If we stay, they’ll find you. These aren’t drunks or hired thugs. They move like trained fighters, and they are too many for us.”

Kaelira clutched my sleeve. “What do we do? What do we do?”

I swallowed. A thought sparked.

“Kaelira.”

She looked at me, eyes wide and terrified.

“You can help us. Your affinity, Soulshape. You can alter appearances.”

“I can’t,” she whispered. “I’ve barely practiced. Real changes, true reshaping, take rounds of work. Even the strongest Soulshapers can’t do it in one try. A big transformation needs dozens working together, like in our ritual. I can only make the smallest shifts… and only for a few minutes.”

“That’s enough,” Therrin said. “We only need a few moments.”

Kaelira trembled. “I don’t know if I can,”

I squeezed her hands. “Try. Please.”

Her fear wavered, then hardened. She nodded.

A soft glow shimmered between her fingers.

The air thickened, warm, humming, familiar. Soulshape energy brushed my skin like a breath of warm wind. Kaelira turned her focus inward as she concentrated, hands hovering inches from my face.

My cheeks tingled. My jaw tightened, then softened. My hair shifted in shade, darker, plainer. My nose narrowed. My lips thinned.

The light faded.

I looked… different.

Not unrecognizable, but enough to pass as someone forgettable.

Kaelira altered herself next, eyes dimmer, cheeks rounder, hair slightly shorter. A simple village girl.

She swayed with exhaustion. I caught her.

Therrin immediately stepped closer, steadying both of us with a hand at my back as if expecting her to collapse entirely. Judew hovered at her other side, watching her face with sharp, assessing eyes, the look of a man measuring just how much strength she had left and how fast they needed to move.

Kaelira swallowed, lifted a trembling hand again, and without a word reached toward Therrin and Judew.

A faint shimmer brushed over Therrin, his jaw softening, hair darkening slightly, the stern soldier replaced by a younger-looking man with softer features.

Therrin touched his cheek in surprise. Judew raised a brow, then snorted. “Hells. he looks like a harmless farmhand.”

Kaelira managed a weak smile. “That’s the point.”

Judew nodded approvingly. “Good. Now we play the part.”

“Part?” Kaelira asked weakly.

“We leave as two couples,” Therrin said. “Walking out the front like everyone else.”

Judew smirked. “I’ll take the princess.”

Kaelira blinked. “What?”

“You look like a girl from a river village. I look like a man who’d be courting one.”

Therrin stepped beside me and offered his hand. “And you… you’re with me.”

My cheeks heated.

But I took his hand.

Judew adjusted Kaelira’s shawl, lowering it to half-hide her face but not enough to draw suspicion.

“Walk slowly,” he said. “Stay close. Smile if you can. We don’t have much time; we need to leave before this charade fades off.”

Kaelira attempted a smile.

It looked like pain.

“Good enough,” Judew muttered. “Let’s go.”

Hand in hand, we stepped into danger.
