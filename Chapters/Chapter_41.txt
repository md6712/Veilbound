Chapter 41

Morning came too quickly.

There was no softness to it. No slow waking. The castle stirred early, and with it, so did we. Voices in the corridors. Footsteps. Doors opening and closing. A quiet urgency that left no space for hesitation.

Today.

The maids moved around us with purpose, but this time it was not only them. Lady Maerel, Lady Elsin, even my sister, all had a say. Colors were discussed. Fabrics compared. What suited us, what would send the right message, what would be remembered.

We did not choose.

They chose for us.

Layers of fine fabric were brought forward, far richer than anything we had worn before. My gown was soft and flowing, a pale ivory touched with faint silver embroidery that shimmered softly when I moved. It clung where it should, loosened where it needed, shaping me into something delicate, refined, unmistakably feminine.

Kaelira’s was even more striking. Deep crimson, threaded with gold that caught the light with every movement, rich and unmistakable. It was the kind of color that did not ask to be seen, it commanded it. A presence that marked her as the princess without question. Every detail was deliberate, from the fall of the sleeves to the way the fabric framed her shoulders.

Hands moved constantly. Adjusting. Tightening. Smoothing. A strand of hair placed just so. A ribbon tied again. Jewelry chosen and changed and chosen once more.

There was no room for error now. No room for anything less than perfection.

I caught my reflection briefly. And paused. It was… beautiful. There was no denying it.

A part of me admired it. The elegance. The care. The way everything came together so effortlessly under their guidance.

And yet… Something in me remained uneasy.

The softness. The delicacy. The way the gown shaped me, moved with me, defined me. It felt right.

And that was what unsettled me the most. No one said it aloud, but we all knew. We were about to be seen. Fully.

--------

When the time came, we were gathered with the rest of the Elarian party. Prince Eryndor stood at the front, composed and firm, the weight of his role clear in every movement. Knights took their places. Ladies adjusted their gowns one final time.

Kaelira stood beside me.

Through the Veilbond, I felt it again. The tension. The fear. The steady effort to remain calm.

I did not need to speak. She knew I was there. Together, we began to walk.

The path to the court was long, lined with guards and attendants who stepped aside as we passed. The closer we moved, the quieter everything became, as if the castle itself demanded silence before what was to come.

And then, we entered. The court was already full.

Rows upon rows of nobles stood on either side of the great hall, dressed in colors that marked their houses and allegiances. Silks, metals, jewels, each one carefully chosen to display rank and power. Conversations moved in low murmurs, small clusters of voices weaving through the space.

All of it slowed as we stepped inside. Eyes turned, first one by one and then all at once.

At the far end of the hall, raised above the rest, stood the throne.

The King and Queen of Veylan were already seated, their presence calm, unmoving, absolute.

Beside them, slightly lower but no less prominent, sat their two sons. Prince Jaelric. Prince Maedric.

The court parted slowly as we advanced, creating a clear path through the center of the hall.

Every step echoed more loudly than it should have. Prince Eryndor led, Kaelira followed and there I was walking beside her. Into the gaze of an entire kingdom.

Into the moment that would define everything that came after.

---

Prince Eryndor stepped forward and bowed. “Your Majesties.”

The King inclined his head, the Queen watching with measured calm.

“King Valtar. Queen Seralyth,” Eryndor said, his voice carrying clearly through the hall. “I present Princess Kaelira of Elaria, and her Veilbound lady in waiting, Lady Rinya.”

We moved forward.

Together.

We curtsied as best we could, the motion practiced but still not entirely natural. The weight of every gaze in the room made it harder, sharper, more real.

Then we stepped back.

Others followed. Nobles of Elaria, one by one, or in small groups, stepping forward to bow, to curtsy, to present themselves before the throne. When it was done, the King rose slightly in his seat.

“Welcome,” he said. The word felt strange. It was not unkind, but it was not warm either.

It sounded formal and measured, as if it were spoken because it must be, not because it was meant.

Still, it was a welcome.

“We receive you in the name of Veylan,” he continued. “As has been done for generations.”

There was a pause.

His gaze shifted, briefly, toward Kaelira.

“It is our tradition that a prince of Veylan shall take a princess of Elaria to wife, and through this bond, peace is kept between our lands.”

He straightened slightly.

“My late wife, Queen Elyra, who was kin to your house, was dear to me.” His voice lowered, not with grief, but with something distant, remembered. “Though her days were too short, her impact was not. For twenty years, there has been peace between Veylan and Elaria.”

I noticed, just for a fleeting moment, Queen Seralyth’s fingers tighten against the arm of her seat at the mention of Queen Elyra, a small, almost imperceptible reaction, but one that did not go unnoticed.

I also realized how little I truly knew of this kingdom. How could that be, when they had once had a queen from Elaria? But that had been long ago, perhaps even before I was born, and whatever ties had existed then had clearly faded into something colder, more distant.

The King continued, his voice steady as he laid out what many in the hall already knew, and what we had only heard in fragments.

“The tradition did not begin in peace,” he said. “It was born from the edge of war.”

A murmur stirred, then settled.

“Veylan, as the greater power, held dominion over Elaria for generations. But Elaria sought its independence, and in time, it claimed it. To preserve that independence, and to bind our fates away from conflict, a treaty was forged two centuries past.”

His gaze swept the hall.

“A royal union. A prince of Veylan and a princess of Elaria.”

He paused, letting the weight of it settle.

“For many years, this bond has held. It has spared both our peoples the cost of war.”

Another pause.

“Princess Elmira, your late sister, was to fulfill this duty,” he went on. “But her tragic passing cast doubt upon the path set before us.”

Silence tightened across the court.

“By right,” the King said, more firmly now, “Veylan may lay claim should the tradition fail.”The words did not rise. They did not threaten. But they did not need to. The meaning was clear enough.

It seemed to me that King Valtar was not eager for war. It made a certain sense. His last wife had been from Elaria, and whatever he felt for her still lingered in the way he spoke of peace.

But the thought would not settle. If he did not want war… then who had ordered us hunted on the road to Veylan?

Then he looked to Kaelira and addressed her directly, his tone polite, almost gentle, asking if she truly consented to such a union.

Kaelira stilled. For a heartbeat she did not move.

I reached inward through the Veil to understand her, but the hall was crowded, too many minds, too much presence, and the bond felt thin and distant. Even so, I caught fragments.

The weight of the moment pressing down on her all at once.

She did not speak. She simply nodded.

The King studied her a moment longer, then lifted a hand. “My sons,” he said. “Stand. Come forward.”

They did.

Prince Jaelric rose first, composed, unreadable, stepping down with the confidence of someone who had never been denied. Prince Maedric followed, quieter, watchful, his eyes moving not just to Kaelira, but across the room, measuring.

The King turned to the court. “One of my sons must wed this lady. That is the course set by fate and by our law. Should more than one lay claim, then Princess Kaelira shall choose between them. So it has been, so it shall be.”

A ripple passed through the hall.

Then the Queen rose.

“I cannot accept this,” Queen Seralyth said, her voice cutting clean through the chamber. “I will not see my sons bound to a false bride.”

A collective gasp answered her.

“We do not even know if she is a proper woman,” she went on, sharper now. “I will have heirs, true heirs. I will not have dark magic placed in my line.”

Whispers erupted. Some hushed, some eager.

Several nobles inclined their heads in agreement. Others stiffened in discomfort. A high-ranking minister stepped forward, bowing slightly before speaking.

“Your Majesty,” he said, “there is merit in the Queen’s concern. We have not proven the Princess beyond doubt. There are… rumors. That this is no princess, but a prince in disguise.”

A few laughed. Others murmured. Some fell silent entirely. The reactions fractured the room.

I felt the heat rise in my chest. Beside me, Kaelira went rigid. Humiliation settled over us both, heavy and immediate, impossible to ignore under the weight of so many watching eyes.

Prince Maedric, the more candid of the two, let out a short, careless laugh. “She is too beautiful for a boy,” he said, loud enough for many to hear, “but a boy nonetheless. I will not marry a false bride. I agree with my mother.”

He turned away and returned to his seat as if the matter were already settled. All eyes shifted to Prince Jaelric.

He hesitated.

For a moment, I thought he might speak differently. But the Queen’s words had already shaped the air. Whatever thought he held, it did not find voice.

From somewhere in the crowd, a voice called out, “It would be a cruel fate for all the ladies of Veylan if the charming Prince Jaelric were made to wed a boy!”

Laughter broke out.

Heat rushed to my face. Beside me, Kaelira went still. I could feel it through the Veil, the humiliation, sharp and suffocating. She was holding herself together, fighting not to crumble, and I felt how hard it was becoming with every heartbeat. Even I was on the verge of tears.

"That is enough." Prince Eryndor stepped forward, his voice cutting through the hall. “We stand here as your guests. I will not allow such treatment of my sister. To dishonor her is to dishonor all of Elaria.” He chose his words with care, naming her as a woman and insisting on the dignity owed to her.

The King rose slightly, his expression tightening. “You are right,” he said. “This is not fitting.” He turned toward Eryndor with a measured nod. “I offer apology, on behalf of my sons, and on behalf of this court.”

His gaze shifted then, not just to his sons, but to the nobles who had spoken. It was not anger alone, but warning.

I found myself studying the currents beneath it all. The politics of Veylan were laid bare in that moment. Too many houses held too much power. Some flinched, some measured their reactions, and some did not react at all, as if none of this could touch them. It was clear that many in Veylan desired war, or at least would not shrink from it.

One of those who did not yield was the Queen.

She rose again, her voice sharper, unrestrained. “Then let it be proven,” she said. “Let her undress before the court and show she is truly a woman. Otherwise, I will not allow this farce to continue.”

I felt Kaelira shake beside me. She was holding herself together by will alone, and I could feel it slipping. I stepped closer, placing my hand against her back to steady her. She swayed, dizzy, breath unsteady.

Eryndor saw it at once. His expression hardened, and when he spoke, his voice did not rise in panic but rang with controlled fury. “Nonsense. We leave. This ends now.”

He stepped forward, placing himself between Kaelira and the court, as if shielding her from every gaze in the hall.

“Elaria will not accept this,” he continued, each word measured, deliberate, impossible to ignore. “We did not come here to endure humiliation. We did not come here to see our princess degraded before your court.”

Whispers spread through the hall. My sister and Lady Maerel moved in quickly at our sides. I glanced once toward Therrin. He caught my eye and gave a small, steadying smile.

Behind it all, I heard Maedric laugh. Low. Amused. It sent a chill through me.

Eryndor drew in a breath, the final words forming with the weight of a decision that would break kingdoms.

“We return—”

Then something shifted.

A disturbance at the edge of the hall. Not loud, not hurried, but certain. A figure broke from the ranks and stepped into the open path. I had been watching only Kaelira. I almost missed him.

“I will take Princess Kaelira as my bride.”

The voice was clear and steady. It did not rise, yet it carried to every corner of the chamber.

The sound struck the room like a blade.

Every whisper died. Every movement stilled.

The entire court fell into silence.
