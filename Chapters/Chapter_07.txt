Chapter 7

The next day, everything moved faster than I expected. The Council had already approved the ritual. Invitations were being sent to Soulshapers across the continent. The prince and I were examined again, this time for deeper preparation.

During the session, we began asking questions. Not just about what would happen, but about the magic itself, especially dark magic.

One of the Council mages, a woman named Miranda, took it upon herself to explain. She had sharp eyes and a calm voice, as though none of what we asked surprised her.

"Dark magic," she said, "includes powers like Deathroot, which transfers life force from one person to another; Mindlock, the power to control thought and memory; and Veilshard, which can rip or bind souls. These are banned without exception. Their use brings corruption, pain, and often madness. Unlike most magic, dark magic is not affinity-dependent. It often comes through cursed or ancient artifacts, forbidden texts, or twisted rituals. Anyone experienced enough with the Veil can potentially use it, though doing so almost always comes at a terrible cost."

She looked between us. "Soulshape is an affinity, rare, powerful, and neutral in nature. While dangerous, it is not inherently corrupt. It is tightly restricted, but not evil."

She explained more. Soulshape was a neutral-gendered affinity, neither masculine nor feminine.

Both men and women could possess it, though it was incredibly rare. "Only one in every hundred thousand," she said. "In a kingdom of five million, there may be fifty Soulshapers total. Most are known, registered, and closely watched by the Council."

Full transformations were nearly unheard of. Still, Soulshapers were among the wealthiest and most influential magic users in the realm, offering costly services to nobles and royals alike. Some were famed for their artistry, sculpting appearances, healing scars, or crafting subtle enhancements admired in courts.

"But everything is regulated," Miranda added. "Every transformation, no matter how small, must be approved by the Council. If it alters the body’s structure in a noticeable way, a face too different, a bone reshaped, the Council denies it. Only minimal changes are permitted."

Despite that, she admitted, some Soulshapers operated in secret. There had been executions, mages and clients both, just for beautifying a face too far beyond the natural.

Kaelen and I were listening the whole time. We were both quiet, worn thin by what lay ahead.

Our fates were sealed. We had to go through with it, for the realm, for peace.

We didn’t look each other in the eye. It was too much.

Yet there was comfort in knowing I wasn’t alone.

Then Kaelen, a Soulshaper himself, finally spoke. "As a prince, I don’t need wealth or influence. What good is this affinity to me?"

Miranda nodded, not unkindly. "Soulshaping isn’t useful in war or court politics, it’s true.

Not directly. And to become a practicing mage, even with the affinity, takes years of training. Most royals have little time for that."

Kaelen’s shoulders slumped slightly. It was disappointing to hear.

But Miranda continued, with a different tone. "As a princess, though? It may be more valuable than you think. You’ll be expected to maintain appearances. With Soulshape, you can hide a scar. Adjust a mole. Smooth the lines of age or stress. In court, beauty can be a weapon, too."

Kaelen said nothing.

But I felt the weight of that shift in him. He didn’t believe it, not fully. Like me, he hadn’t accepted what was coming. Hearing someone refer to his future as a princess was too much. I could sense his anger, even if he never said a word.

I got curious and asked Miranda about my own affinity. She was kind enough to explain in detail.

She herself was a Heartsense, and according to her, Heartsense and Stillmark were among the most feminine rare affinities, both deeply connected to healing and calmness. "Heartsense,"

she said, "is empathic and responsive. It lets you read emotions, ease pain, and support the body’s recovery. Stillmark, also known as the Mark of Stillness, is projective and suppressive.

It doesn't read emotions; it dulls them."

She continued. "Stillmark allows you to induce calm in individuals or groups. It can soothe panic, rage, fear, or grief, not by healing, but by suppressing intensity. Heartsense is about understanding pain. Stillmark is about preventing reaction."

I listened, trying to absorb what that meant.

"The Veil doesn’t always give us what we want," Miranda finished. "But it often gives us what the world needs from us. And Stillmark... is no less powerful for being quiet."

I hesitated, then asked, "Can I use it to suppress my own feelings? The despair?"

Miranda’s expression turned serious. "Technically, yes," she said. "Stillmark can be turned inward. But I would caution you strongly against it. You haven’t been trained. If you attempt to dull your own emotions too frequently or too deeply, you could fall into emotional numbness. Worse, you might enter a dangerous feedback loop, charging and discharging the Veil endlessly without rest. That kind of imbalance can lead to permanent Veil loss... or death."

I nodded slowly, still uncertain. "So when will I be able to cast something safely on myself?"

Miranda gave a knowing smile. "That's a good question. You’ve studied some of this, I imagine, but let’s go over it again. There are five recognized tiers of Veil users in the realm."

She held up a hand, counting each off with her fingers.

"First, Veilmarked, those who show a dormant affinity. Watched, sometimes tested. Common among noble-born children. No authority, no casting."

"Second, Veilspark, someone who has manifested but is raw and untrained. That’s where you are now. You’re registered but not licensed. You can assist, but only under supervision."

"Third, Veilcast, a fully trained, licensed mage. Capable of independent casting. These are the court mages, temple healers, and military casters. They have permission to act alone."

"Fourth, Veilwarden, a master of their affinity, Council-recognized. These mages can lead rituals, train others, oversee magical law."

"And finally, Veilmage, a title rarely given. Someone who has survived or anchored mythic-level magic. They’re more symbol than servant. These are the high mages. There are only a handful in each kingdom, and most of them reside within the High Council."

She lowered her hand and looked at me directly. "Until you reach at least Veilcast, you shouldn’t attempt solo use of Stillmark. Even at that stage, casting on yourself is risky.

For now, you’ll be watched. And trained. Carefully."

Kaelen tilted his head, curious. "And which level are you?"

Miranda glanced at the other mage in the room. They shared a look, council veterans, no doubt used to keeping rank and title vague.

"We are both Veilwardens," she said at last, smiling faintly. "But you’re not supposed to ask that directly. It’s rather rude, Your Highness."

I couldn’t help but smirk. They were clearly high on the ladder.

Then, for the first time, the other mage spoke. His voice was smooth and quiet. "Tomorrow, Mage Vistan will arrive. He is to lead the ritual. He is a Veilmage, the only Veilmage known to possess Soulshape."

My breath caught.

"I and many other Soulshapers will assist," he added. "Most are already en route. They will arrive within two days, at most."

I frowned. That seemed unlikely. "Last I asked, it takes four days to travel from here to the High Council Palace."

The mage nodded. "Yes, normally. But they left before the selection process even began. The Council planned ahead."

That explained it.

He continued, "Not all are coming from the Council itself. Around ten Soulshapers are official Council representatives. Another ten reside within this kingdom and are already nearby. The rest are coming from neighboring realms."

He paused, letting that sink in. "And it’s not only mages. Representatives from each kingdom will be present. The Council has sent formal invitations to every allied realm. Each will send one royal representative to witness the rite."

"The ritual will take place in five days."
