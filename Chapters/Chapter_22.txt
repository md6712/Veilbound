Chapter 22

The dining hall of Greenhall was wide and high-beamed, its vaulted ceiling trimmed in old cedar and lit by a sprawling iron chandelier that hung like the ribs of a fallen star. The walls were hung with tapestries of old Elarian hunts and victories, now faded but no less proud. Servants moved briskly but silently between the spaces, setting the last of the silver and lighting the candles.

The table, long, polished, and easily able to seat twenty-four or more, dominated the room. But even that would not suffice for our full company. So a second, slightly smaller table had been set beside it, arranged in a way that kept the nobles and royal circle at the main setting, and the knights, aides, and staff at the second. It was not division in disrespect, it was simple logistics.

At the high table sat the lord and lady of Greenhall, the royal siblings, and the ladies of the court. Kaelira was placed to the right of Lord Halric, with Prince Eryndor across from her beside Lady Anire. Serenya and I were seated close together, with Therrin on my other side, and Elsin beside her. Cale, fortunately, had taken a place at the second table among the knights.

It was strange how this arrangement, the placement of chairs, the direction of gazes, the balance of silks and the attire of well-dressed men, fine coats, sashes, and boots suited to a knight or prince rather than armor, could carry so much meaning. Who spoke first? Who poured the wine? Who laughed, and who only smiled.

I had dined with the other noble houses plenty of times before. But never like this. Now, I was meant to be seen. Heard. Judged not for swordwork or strength, but poise and presence.

And I wasn’t sure I knew how to be that kind of person yet.

Still, I kept my back straight, folded my hands where they belonged, and followed Serenya’s lead when I needed to. Therrin, beside me, made it easier. He didn’t talk much, but when he did, it was only for me. "You’re doing fine. Just breathe." And somehow, that made the food taste warmer. I must have looked that way, because he seemed to sense my unease.

Midway through the meal, I caught part of a conversation taking place between Prince Eryndor, Lord Halric, and two of the prince’s knights, Sir Alric and Sir Cael. They were seated only a few places up the table from me, close enough that I could hear them comfortably. And besides, the men spoke freely. The ladies, not so much.

“I have not yet decided if it’s best that we depart tomorrow,” the prince said. “I thought that was the plan, but now... I’m reconsidering.”

Lord Halric nodded, his brow creased. “Perhaps that’s wise. With the unrest in the lowlands and the rumors from the eastern border, it may be safer to wait a day or two.”

Sir Alric leaned in. “I agree, Your Highness. We’ve seen signs, riders that don’t bear banners, strange tracks. The roads aren’t as quiet as they should be.”

Sir Cael gave a sharp nod of agreement. I even saw Therrin, seated beside me, dip his head once, solemn and certain.

I blinked, puzzled. What unrest? What rumors?

Apparently, everyone else at the table understood what this was about, everyone but us. The women. It hit me with quiet fury: they would never discuss such matters with us. Not truly.

Kaelira and I have become too delicate in their eyes. Too ornamental to be trusted with fear or strategy.

I wanted to ask. I wanted to know. But I dared not speak. So I looked at Kaelira.

She looked back at me, just as surprised. But unlike me, she was a princess. She could ask.

"Forgive my intrusion, Eryndor," Kaelira said, "but may I ask what exactly these rumors are? Should we be concerned?" I wondered how, in just a few days, she had become this ladylike. I feared I might appear the same from the outside. How strange. It must be the magic, or perhaps we naturally decided to adapt. I had heard on the way from Lady Maerel that my magic helps both of us adjust much faster than expected. I am a Stillmark.

Stillmarks can inflict calmness and adaptability in others, and sometimes in themselves. It is like someone is injecting calmness in us constantly.

Prince Eryndor turned his head slightly toward her and gave a polite smile, measured but warm. "There’s nothing to be alarmed over. Just reports from the border, likely exaggerations. Still, we’ll take precautions, because there are always those who might wish to jeopardize the peace. We are better to be alert. It’s my duty to ensure we travel safely, nothing more."

The way he said it offered no invitation for further questions, but it was not unkind.

Reassuring, without telling us anything at all.

Still, Kaelira tilted her head slightly. "So we wait a few days here?"Her brother was only a few years older than both of us, a couple of years older than Serenya. Kaelira, when she was also a prince, had always shown some respect for her brother. But now she spoke to him with more care, as if she needed to preserve the image of the perfect royal princess.

Noticing the worry beginning to edge her voice, Prince Eryndor reached across the table and gently took her hand. It was a quiet gesture, but full of meaning.

he said. "Don’t let yourself worry too much, Kaelira. We’ll stay a few days, and when we see that we have no reason for concern, we march."

From across the table, I could feel how Kaelira must have felt. Being the target is never easy, especially when you have become helpless as we are.

Lord Halric, reading the room, decided it was time to shift the tone. He raised his cup with a practiced smile. "But let us not trouble our fine company with such heavy talk, especially not the ladies."

The realization struck harder than I expected. We no longer had a place in the affairs of men; they would talk and decide among themselves, leaving us to follow quietly in the wake of their choices.

He turned slightly toward Kaelira and the rest of us. "Tomorrow, we’ll see to lighter matters. I’ve arranged a small garden luncheon for the afternoon. The weather should hold, and the view from the west terrace is quite lovely this time of year."

Just like that, the conversation was redirected to something more "suitable." But suitability is a subjective thing. Once, I would have preferred a topic of politics, war, or swordsmanship. Now, it wasn’t that I didn’t know such things. It was that I no longer felt welcome in those conversations. Not truly.
