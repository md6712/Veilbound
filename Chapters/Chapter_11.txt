Chapter 11

Mirna was helping me get ready. My legs were shaking.

Serenya sat on the edge of the bed, offering little comments of encouragement. “Don’t worry,”

she said, brushing my hair. “You’ll do fine. Just remember: shoulders back, spine straight.

Like you’ve always belonged.”

I wasn’t so sure.

Tonight was the formal reception, the first gathering of nobles, Council mages, and political envoys after the ritual. A celebration of peace. A performance of submission. A debut of the princess... and her mirror.

Me.

Serenya explained, in hushed tones, that the king would soon declare the date of our departure for Veylan. The guests present tonight would help finalize who would escort us.

Every noble eye would be watching—measuring how well I had adapted, how gracefully I wore the burden I’d been given.

And I could barely stay upright.

The maids—two of them now—began preparing my outfit. Serenya directed them with gentle authority. “Start with the shift,” she said, handing them a white linen chemise. “Then the stays.”

Mirna slipped the chemise over my head. The fabric brushed softly over my skin, raising goosebumps. Then came the stays—stiffened fabric laced tightly around my torso. Not as crushing as I feared, but still… confining.

Then came the petticoats. Layers of fabric were tied at my waist, giving volume to the dress to come. And then the gown itself—silken, pale blue, embroidered with silver thread at the cuffs and hem. A modest neckline. Flowing sleeves. A narrow waist.

Mirna gently helped arrange the skirts, fluffing the layers just right. Serenya stepped forward with a silver circlet. “You’ll need this,” she said. “Not a crown—but close.”

Once the dressing was done, they stepped back to admire their work. I stood on unsteady feet.

Serenya smiled. “You look beautiful.”

“I don’t feel like anything,” I murmured, blushing.

“Then fake it,” she said. “Until you do.”

The next lesson was walking. Mirna demonstrated—light steps, heel first, with the slightest sway of the hips. I copied her, stiffly at first, then a little better. They corrected my posture. Coached my hands. Showed me how to turn gracefully. When I stumbled, Serenya only chuckled and made me start again.

“You’re doing better than most court ladies your age,” she said. “Trust me.”

I tried to believe her.

All this time, I hadn’t seen Kaelen. She goes with Kaelira now. The princess.

Since the transformation, she’d remained secluded in her own chambers, under the same veil of guards and mages as me. But I felt her absence like a missing limb. We were bonded, after all.

I asked about her.

Serenya smiled. “You’ll see her soon. You’ll enter the main hall together.”

That made my stomach tighten.

I didn’t know whether I was afraid to see her—or aching to.

We practiced the curtsy next. I hated it. Bending knees, lowering my gaze, spreading the skirts just so. It felt humiliating. “This is degrading,” I muttered once.

Serenya raised an eyebrow. “No. It’s respect. Men kneel to kings. Women curtsy to queens.

You’re not beneath anyone—you’re simply not a boy anymore.”

I frowned. "But it means I can’t hold a sword anymore. I can’t defend the country anymore."

Serenya’s tone softened. "You’re wrong. You already have. You made the ultimate sacrifice.

That’s more than any blade could’ve done."

I looked away. "I hate being forced to behave like this."

"Like what?" she asked gently.

"Feminine. Graceful. With all this… too much softness."

She laughed—not mockingly, but with warmth. "In time, you’ll love your femininity. I’m sure of it."

Going back to curtsy. Still, I had to repeat the motion again and again until she was satisfied. Mirna helped me adjust my footing. “You’ll get used to it,” she whispered. “We all do.”

Mirna. My maid. My... confidant? It was strange, but in only a day, I already trusted her.

She didn’t flinch when I stumbled. She didn’t laugh when I cried. She just helped.

“Ready?” Serenya asked at last.

I didn’t answer.

But I nodded.

As we left the room, we noticed many guards lining the corridor. We walked in silence toward the princess’s chambers. Apparently, from there, we would all be escorted to the main hall.

The walk through that corridor was surreal. I had never felt so small, so helpless. I was walking among soldiers, men I used to stand beside. Now, I was just a girl. And I felt it.

Fortunately, none of them made a move. Their eyes were forward, posture perfect.

When we arrived, Serenya knocked gently. A maid opened the door and welcomed us in.

Inside stood the queen, radiant in a gown of deep plum velvet with intricate embroidery along the sleeves and bodice, her hair wound into a braided crown. My mother was beside her, dressed in sea green silk with golden pins in her hair. She looked elegant. Reserved. Strong.

It wasn’t strange to see her there—she had long been the queen’s companion at tea and council gatherings. But still, a pang of jealousy struck me. She had been here while I had been alone. Well, Serenya was there, but it wasn’t the same. She was my sister, not my mother. And though her presence meant the world, there were things only a mother could say, only a mother could do, to make this easier.

I had never looked at my mother as a role model. My father had always been the one I admired.

But today, I wanted her. It felt strange. From the two of them, I knew for certain—I wouldn’t want my father here, not when I was learning to curtsy or being laced into this dress.

The other noticeable change was how I suddenly paid attention to all the feminine things—like dresses, fabrics, gestures. I had never really looked at them before. Maybe because I was so deeply involved with them now. When you’re forced to wear it, you start to notice every stitch, every detail.

Then, behind the three women, was a girl sitting on a chair, in a beautiful pink and white dress - silk layered over sheer lace, with a pearl-threaded bodice and petal-soft sleeves.

She looked as though she’d been crying.

It is safe to say that as we entered, everyone was still for a moment. The first reaction came from my mother, who rushed to me.

She hugged me tightly, tears in her eyes. She told me how beautiful I looked, how proud she was. She gushed over me like I was her little girl.

I almost cried. It had become much harder not to cry these past few days.

Then it was the queen. I curtsied as she approached, unsure what else to do. I had been Kaelen’s friend, yes, but I had rarely come this close to the queen before.

She came close and took my hands in hers.

"I’m proud of you," she said. "And I’m happy my daughter has such loyal friends."

At the mention of daughter, I caught the faintest shift in Kaelira’s posture. A flinch, perhaps. I looked at her again.

I walked toward her. She rose from her chair. As we neared each other, something overtook us—an urge, sudden and strong—to embrace. And that’s exactly what we did.

I don’t know if it was because of the bond between us or because we were in the same shoes.

Maybe it was because we were such close friends. Or maybe it was just that, in this entire world, only the two of us truly understood what the other was going through.

From the outside, it must have looked like something out of a painting—two girls in their finest, one in blue, one in pink and white, holding each other in silence. So delicate. So graceful.

But none of that mattered. We didn’t care. We just needed each other.

We stood like that for a long while before finally sitting side by side on the edge of Kaelira’s bed. Yes, Kaelira, there is no way I call her Kaelen. She is far too delicate and beautiful to be called Kaelen.

We held hands for a moment.

Then Kaelira whispered, "Thank you, Rinya!"
