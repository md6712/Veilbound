Chapter 27

The road stretched quiet and pale beneath the morning light. Our maids rode with us in the

same cabin. My own, Mirna, sat near the small window, her hands folded in her lap as she kept

a watchful eye on everything outside. The princess’s trusted maid, Dina, rode beside her,

quiet and graceful, always ready to tend to her mistress but never speaking unless spoken to.

The mist clung low to the grass, curling around the wheels of the carriage as it rolled

forward. Inside, Kaelira sat across from me, her chin resting against her gloved hand, eyes

fixed on the window. For a long time, neither of us spoke.

Mirna broke the silence first. "It feels strange not to hear the rest of the company close

behind us," she said softly.

Kaelira nodded faintly, but it was clear she was restless. Finally, she let out a quiet sigh.

"I should have argued harder with Eryndor. We could have ridden with the others. Sitting here

feels wrong."

I looked toward her, half smiling. "I thought the same. I can still see the horses ahead

through the window. They look free. We look, well, trapped."

Kaelira gave a short laugh, though there was no real humor in it. "Do you remember when we

used to mock our sisters for complaining about the discomfort of travel? And now here we are,

wrapped in cloaks and cushions."

"I remember," I said, glancing out the window again. "At least they had a choice to complain.

We did not."

Kaelira leaned back, shaking her head. "I cannot believe Eryndor refused us. 'Appearances

must be kept,' he said. As though anyone would notice two riders among a dozen guards. It is

absurd."

"It is," I agreed. "And unfair. I never realized how strict these expectations were until

now. We used to walk freely through the yards, sparring with them, eating with them. And

now," I gestured to the closed walls around us, "now I cannot even see the sky without

permission."

Kaelira's expression softened, thoughtful. "I suppose that is the worst part, seeing how

different the world looks from inside this carriage. How limited it feels."

Outside, the road began to curve along the edge of a green field. Through the small window, I

caught sight of familiar faces riding beside us. Siren was closest, glancing our way for a

heartbeat before looking ahead again, his jaw set tight. A few lengths behind him was Joel,

whose quick look toward the carriage ended with him pretending to fix his reins. Neron passed

next, silent, eyes down.

Kaelira noticed my glance. "They do not quite know how to act around us, do they?"

"No," I said quietly. "I suppose they see something they cannot explain."

"They will learn," she murmured, though her voice carried more frustration than confidence.

We rode on in silence for a while. I searched the line of riders for another face, Therrin's,

but he was nowhere to be seen. When I asked Sir Tavren, who rode beside our window, he leaned

close enough to be heard over the clatter of hooves.

"He is leading the forward group," Tavren said. "They are checking the trail ahead. You will

see them when we stop to rest."

"Thank you," I said, and he nodded before riding on.

Kaelira exhaled sharply, turning back to me. "See? Even Therrin gets to lead. And here we

sit, like cargo."

I smiled faintly. "Maybe it is better this way. No one expects anything from us here."

"Maybe," she said, then added dryly, "but I would rather be expected to do something than

expected to sit still."

The carriage jolted slightly as it hit a patch of uneven ground, and we both braced against

the walls. When the motion steadied again, Kaelira laughed softly. "And they call this

comfort."

I could not help but laugh with her. "If this is comfort, I would rather have a saddle and a

sore back."

We shared a quiet smile then, the kind that carried the memory of who we had been before all

of this. The road outside shimmered in the sun, stretching endlessly ahead.

Around midday, the carriage slowed. Sir Alvis appeared at the window, his calm voice cutting

through the rattle of wheels.

"Your Highness," he said, "the men are checking the crossing ahead. Would you like to rest or

take some air before we continue?"

Kaelira looked at me and grinned. "We would like to feel like people again, Sir Alvis. A

short rest would be welcome."

He smiled, polite as ever. "As you wish. There is a stream just ahead. It is clear and safe

to drink from."

As the carriage came to a stop, Kaelira reached for the door handle. "Come on, Rinya," she

said. "Let us see the world again, even if it is only for a few minutes. My legs are sore; I

am not used to sitting still for so long.

Sir Alvis helped both of us down from the carriage. It felt almost foolish that we needed

their hands just to step down a few small stairs, but the gesture was kindly meant. The maids

hurried out soon after and began arranging a small rest spot beside the stream with the help

of a few guards.

Kaelira, however, waved them off. "We would rather walk a little," she said, and I agreed at

once. She knew my thoughts before I spoke them. Remember, we were linked, bound by the Veil

itself. I could sense what she wanted, and she could sense me as well.

We walked slowly along the riverbank. The air was cool, and the water glimmered beneath the

sun. Behind us, Joval and Nerran followed at a short distance, close enough to guard us but

far enough to seem polite. It felt, however, as if we were prisoners being watched.

I stopped and turned to them. "You can join the others for a while. We will be fine."

They hesitated, looking uncertain. Nerran spoke first. "My lady, we are ordered to follow

you. We do not know who else may travel this road."

I smiled slightly. "We are not going far. You can still see us from here. And please, do not

call me 'my lady.' It sounds strange coming from you."

They exchanged a glance but stayed where they were. I sighed and stepped closer. "I know this

must feel strange to you. Believe me, it feels strange to us."

For a moment, neither spoke. Then Nerran said quietly, "We understand. You were our friends,

like brothers once. We are still trying to make sense of it. But you are dear to us, my

lady."

I sighed and decided to ignore the words 'my lady.' I appear different now. I felt my chest

tighten. "We were good friends before. Can I trust you now?"

Both nodded at once. Joval’s voice was steady when he answered, "We are sworn to protect you

both. Nothing else comes first."

That was genuine enough. I believed them. Yet a small part of me remembered that Cale had

once sworn the same, and I trusted him least of all.

As we walked back toward the horses, we saw Therrin speaking with my uncle, Sir Alvis. My

heart beat strangely as I watched them. I was angry and uncertain why he had not come to say

a word to me. As if Kaelira could read my thoughts, she veered away and started a

conversation with Marek, who stood alone nearby. I could not hear their words, but poor Marek

seemed to be struggling to answer her.

When my uncle noticed me, he called my name and asked how I was. Their conversation stopped

at once. Therrin turned, saw me, and smiled.

"My lady," he said with a short bow, polite as ever.

I curtsied automatically, though I was thinking, Why would you do that, Therrin? We are in

the middle of a trip; there is no need for formality. And why call me that?

Sir Alvis excused himself politely, saying he had to speak with Sir Tavren, who was standing

with some of the guards. That left me alone with Therrin for the first time in days.

"Do not call me that," I said quietly. "Not you. We were best friends." I was tired of all

these formalities, and I wanted my friend to be just my friend again.

He tilted his head, a spark of humor lighting his eyes. "And I am not anymore? I thought I

had been promoted to bodyguard and occasional source of irritation."

"Do not joke with me, Therrin. Of course, you are still my friend."

"Good. Then perhaps I can risk calling you Lady Rinya? It sounds terribly refined."

"Therrin! No. Just Rinya."

He placed a hand over his heart and bowed slightly. "Forgive me, Rinya. I am only trying to

make peace before you decide to strike me with that glare of yours. "

I tried not to smile. "Suddenly you are back to your normal self."

He chuckled under his breath. "Otherwise you might think I have forgotten we were and are

still friends. Besides, someone has to lighten the air before you burn a hole through it with

that frown."

I crossed my arms. "Why did you not tell me you left for scouting? I was worried."

He raised a brow in surprise. "Worried? About me? Rinya, it was only scouting. No one hunts

scouts. They are all too busy chasing princesses and one very beautiful, very angry

Veilbound."

I rolled my eyes. "You are impossible. I was worried, and you are making light of it."

But wait! What did he just call me? Beautiful? In the midst of being both joyful and angry,

something shifted inside me, a strange flutter in my stomach I could not quite name. But

never mind. I hate when he turns my serious concerns into jokes.

His expression shifted then. The teasing edge faded from his voice, replaced by something

quieter and more sincere. "I am sorry for that," he said. "Truly. I did not mean to worry

you. You have enough burdens already, and I should not have added to them. But if it eases

your mind, I am back, safe and whole." He hesitated a moment, then added with a faint smile,

softer than before, "And I promise not to vanish without a word again, if only so I do not

have to face that look of yours twice."
