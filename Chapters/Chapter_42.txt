Chapter 42

All my attention had been on Kaelira, as I tried to steady her and keep her from breaking in front of them all, and because of that my back had turned slightly to the court, so I did not see the man who stepped forward when he entered the open space.

I heard him, though, and something in that voice stirred a recognition so immediate and so unsettling that it seemed impossible.

We turned together, almost without thinking, and there he stood.

Judew.

The shock struck me so sharply that I could not breathe, and for a moment the world itself seemed to pause, as the court fell into a silence so complete it felt as though time had stopped to witness what had just happened.

Around us, confusion spread in quiet waves, with many of our companions glancing at one another, uncertain, trying to place him and understand what they were seeing, while others simply stared.

But Kaelira and I knew him.

Not fully, not truly, but enough to understand that this changed everything.

I could not stop the questions from rising in my mind, what he was doing here, why he would say such a thing, and how any of this could possibly make sense.

He stood differently now, no longer the quiet traveler we had met on the road, but something far more defined, with a straighter posture, a presence that could not be ignored, and clothing that marked him clearly as someone who belonged within this court.

He looked as though he had always belonged here.

The silence stretched, tightening with every heartbeat, until at last the King moved, and to my surprise, he smiled, not the careful expression of a ruler, but something real.

King Valtar’s face opened with unmistakable joy. “My son. You have returned.”

A murmur rippled through the hall, breaking the stillness just enough to remind us all that we were not alone in witnessing this moment.

The King rose fully, his presence filling the space. “I did not think my messenger would find you. Yet here you stand. You have come back to us.”

Then he called out for celebration, and the single word rang through the chamber with authority.

He descended without hesitation and crossed the distance between them, drawing Judew into a firm embrace, holding him with a certainty that left no doubt.

“My son,” he repeated, more quietly this time.

Something shifted inside me at those words, something I had not expected to feel.

My son.

My father will never say those words to me again, and the realization struck harder than I was prepared for, settling deep and heavy within my chest.

Then my attention came back to Judew. He was not simply a traveler we had met by chance. He was of this house, bound to it by blood. A prince.

My gaze moved instinctively toward the Queen, and there I saw something entirely different from the King’s joy.

She was not pleased, nor relieved, but rigid and cold, watching him with an anger she did not bother to hide.

It did not take long for the truth to settle into place, because if he was the King’s son, then he was not hers.

The late Queen. Of course.

In that instant, the tension and the resentment that lingered in the room became clear, no longer hidden beneath formality.

Across the hall, Prince Jaelric stepped forward, his reaction slower and more measured, yet not without warmth, as he approached Judew and embraced him as well, though less tightly, as if unsure how much of himself to reveal before the watching court.

Prince Maedric, however, did not move at all.

He remained beside his mother, still and watchful, offering nothing.

The divide within the royal family revealed itself plainly in that moment.

And we stood at the very center of it when Judew turned toward the Queen. With a hesitation that did not suit the man we had met on the road, he inclined his head. “My Queen.” The words sounded respectful, but strained, as though they were unfamiliar. It was obvious, even to me, that there had never been warmth between them, and the Queen did not answer him at all.

He faced the court then, and the divide became unmistakable, some faces bright with relief and delight, others tight with shock, and many hardened with anger, so that it seemed a greater part of the court did not count him among their allies; one high-ranking lord stepped forward, bowed, and offered a polished welcome, speaking of his safe return after many years away, yet even that courtesy felt measured rather than heartfelt.

Questions rose in me all at once, why he had left, why he had returned now, why we had never heard his name, and most of all why he had hidden the truth from us, and as these thoughts pressed in, I felt Kaelira falter again beside me, her turmoil spilling through the Veil, and I tried to steady her with it, to quiet her breath, though the crowd and the weight of the hall made it harder with every passing moment.

At last, he moved, not toward the throne, but toward us, toward Kaelira, and when he reached her he took her hands with a gentleness that did not match the storm he had caused, bowed over them, and pressed a kiss to her knuckles before saying, “Princess Kaelira, before this court and before my father’s throne, will you grant me the honor of naming you my intended bride?”

The Queen surged to her feet before Kaelira could answer. “This is forbidden. This is nonsense. You abandoned your place, and now you return to ruin what remains.”

He did not yield. He turned to face her, steady and unflinching. “With respect, my Queen, I did not forfeit my blood when I left, nor did I surrender my name. By birth and by law, I am Aelyon of Veylan, son of the King, and no decree has stripped me of that.” He held her gaze. “I stand here not by your leave, but by my right.”

The Queen recoiled at that, shock flashing across her face before hardening into anger, as if she had expected the boy who left years ago and not the confident, seasoned man who had returned to stand before her.

No one spoke after that, not the ministers, not the princes, not even the King, and the silence held as he looked again to Kaelira and began, more formally now, “Princess Kaelira, will you grant me the honor...”

I stepped forward before he could finish, unable to hold back any longer, the words forcing their way out of me with a sharpness that cut through the silence. “You lied to us. We do not know who you are, not truly, so how do you expect...”

He did not recoil. He did not even hesitate. He held my gaze as if he had been waiting for that accusation.

“You are right, Lady Rinya,” he said. “I was not honest with you, and for that I owe you more than an apology. There were reasons, and I will answer for them, but not like this, not in fragments.”

He drew himself up then, not just straightening, but claiming the space around him, turning so that every eye in the court was forced upon him, as if he would no longer allow himself to be half-seen or misunderstood.

“Enough shadows.” His voice left no room for doubt or denial.

“Let there be no confusion about who stands before you.”

He paused, and in that breath, the entire court seemed to lean toward him.

“I am Aelyon of Veylan,” he declared. “Son of King Valtar. Prince by blood, by law, and by right,” and as the weight of his words settled over the hall, Kaelira’s strength finally gave way and she collapsed, and I felt the world tilt with her, a wave of dizziness rushing through me as I struggled to remain standing.
