Chapter 36

The moment we stepped out of the room, the air seemed to hold its breath.

A strange thrill curled low in my stomach, an echo of excitement sharpened by fear. It felt as if we were sneaking out of our own skins, wearing faces that were almost ours and yet not.

Therrin’s hand remained wrapped around mine, steady, warm, guiding me forward in a way that made my heartbeat flutter against my ribs.

Beside me, Kaelira moved stiffly at Judew’s side. Her eyes were lowered beneath her shawl, but I could feel her fear flickering through the Veilbond like quick sparks. I tightened my grip around Therrin’s arm. Not to appear convincing as a couple, but because I needed the anchor.

We slipped down the stairs carefully. Each board creaked like it wanted to betray us. Voices murmured from below, rough and low. Searching voices.

We reached the main room.

And there, near the entrance, three armed men stood. Blocking the door.

---

One of them raised his chin as we approached. “Hold there. The inn is under inspection.”

Judew stepped forward casually, as if nothing at all was amiss. “Morning to you. We are just passing through. Entertainers from Rivermarch.”

Rivermarch. A small town south of here. I had no idea Therrin or Judew even knew of it.

The guard narrowed his eyes. “Entertainers? At this hour?”

“Heading to the capital,” Judew replied smoothly. “The wedding, you understand. Plenty of coin to be earned when a prince takes a bride.”

That startled me. The wedding? I hadn’t expected them to prepare a story this complete.

The guard’s gaze slid to Therrin. “And what do you play?”

Therrin hesitated for only a fraction of a second. “Courstring.”

I blinked. Courstring? When had he ever—

But then I noticed the instrument strapped across his back: a small, polished, pear shaped thing with four strings, yet held like a traveling guitar. It was clearly borrowed from somewhere inside the inn.

The guard folded his arms. “Show me.”

Therrin stiffened almost imperceptibly. But he removed the little courstring, positioned it, and played.

And astonishingly, he played well.

Not masterfully, but enough. A solid, steady melody filled the air, soft and convincing.

On the other side, Judew lifted a small flute from his belt and joined him, the two melodies weaving together.

The guards exchanged glances.

Then another stepped forward. A man wrapped in a black cloak, clearly the leader. “And what about the girls?”

Therrin froze.

For the first time since morning, real fear flickered across his face. He hadn’t planned for this part.

My heart leapt painfully against my ribs. I looked at Kaelira. She looked back, lost, fragile, unsure.

And suddenly I knew.

“We sing,” I whispered.

Kaelira jolted softly. “What?”

“We sing,” I repeated. “Follow me.”

The black cloaked leader crossed his arms. “Then let us hear it.”

Therrin and Judew resumed their melody.

I closed my eyes, reached inward, and let my breath settle. I had never sung for anyone. Only in secret. Only when alone. But the music curled around me now, fitting into some instinctual place.

I opened my mouth.

And sang.

My voice slipped into the air like warm silver. Clear, bright, feminine in a way that startled even me. The guards stiffened. Therrin’s fingers faltered for half a beat.

Then another voice rose with mine.

Kaelira.

Perfectly in tune. Perfectly matched. As if we had shared songs our whole lives.

Maybe it was the Veilbond. Maybe something inside us mirrored itself the moment music touched it.

But together… our harmony shimmered.

And the room fell utterly still.

---

They let us go.

Not because they trusted us, I could feel that in their eyes, but because the music and our voices gave them nothing to hold us with. Suspicion lingered, but suspicion is not proof, and the cloak leader finally stepped aside with a curt motion.

We walked past them slowly, carefully, still holding onto our borrowed roles. Judew gave a polite nod, Therrin kept his arm linked with mine, and Kaelira stayed close to Judew with her head lowered beneath her shawl. Only when we stepped into the morning air did I feel the pressure ease from my chest.

The charade faded shortly after.

Kaelira’s magic flickered first, like a candle losing breath, and then our changed features softened back into our true ones. A faint shimmer passed over Therrin and Judew, the subtle illusions falling away.

But even when it ended, we didn’t let go.

I still held Therrin’s arm. Kaelira still held Judew’s. None of us spoke about it, but the closeness felt necessary. Reassuring. Somehow, their nearness steadied us more than distance ever could.

It was strange, realizing that. Strange to feel so small and yet so safe beside them, as if our bodies remembered something our minds had not accepted yet. Strange to walk with them and feel, for a moment, like men and women were different creatures entirely… and we no longer belonged to the one we used to.

We rounded a corner near the edge of the city.

And there they were.

Pattin and Sedrick stood waiting for us, holding the reins of our horses. When they spotted us, they waved us over frantically, relief closing the distance between us faster than our feet could.

“We thought they’d caught you,” Pattin said.

Sedrick shook his head quickly. “Come on, we need to ride before they circle back.”

We joined them, still close, still clinging quietly to the arms that had carried us through the danger.

Therrin and Judew spoke quickly, their voices low and firm. “We cannot ride separate,” Judew said. “You two cannot manage the pace we need.”

Therrin nodded. “We ride fast. Far faster than you can handle on your own. With the clothes you wear now, with the weight of the skirts and the saddle shift, it is unsafe.”

My cheeks warmed, but he was right. Even yesterday’s slow riding had been difficult. Today, shaken and exhausted, we would fall behind in moments.

Judew gestured toward Kaelira. “You ride with me.”

And naturally, Therrin looked at me. “And you with me.”

Before I could overthink it, Therrin stepped forward, his hands sure and strong as he guided me toward his horse. He lifted me with a practiced motion, placing me in the saddle ahead of him.

I settled uncertainly, but then he swung up behind me in one fluid movement, his presence wrapping around me like a protective wall. From each side, his arms came around to take the reins. His chest pressed lightly against my back.

I let out a breath I didn’t know I was holding.

Instinctively, I leaned into him. His warmth, his steadiness, the solid weight of his arms enclosing me — it was all grounding in a way I had never felt before. I placed my hands gently atop his, resting in the circle he formed around me.

He adjusted his grip on the reins. “Hold on,” he murmured. “I will not let you fall.”

Judew had already helped Kaelira onto his horse, sitting her in front of him in the same way.

She glanced back at me, wide eyed, but not frightened — simply overwhelmed.

Then Therrin nudged his horse forward.

We rode.

---

The wind struck our faces harder the moment we cleared the city outskirts. Therrin urged the horse into a faster pace, Judew matching beside us. The sudden speed pressed me back into him, my breath catching as his arms tightened so I would not slip.

Kaelira clung to Judew's forearm, her hair whipping behind her like a pale banner.

For a little while, it felt like we could outrun anything.

But the feeling did not last.

Hoofbeats thundered behind us.

Men shouting.

And then the whistle of an arrow slicing the air.

Therrin twisted sharply, one arm releasing the reins just long enough to deflect the arrow with the flat of a small travel-shield he had strapped to the saddle. Judew did the same on Kaelira's side, knocking another shot away with a quick motion.

"They are gaining," Judew called.

"Too many," Therrin answered through clenched teeth.

I could feel the strain beneath him. Even though we weighed far less than we once did, two riders still pushed the horse beyond comfort. Every hard stride trembled through the animal's body.

Another arrow came, then another. Pattin and Sedrick rode behind us, blocking what they could, but the horses were tiring.

My heart pounded. Their numbers grew clearer as the road curved.

Twenty. Perhaps more.

Armed. Skilled. Intent.

We would not outrun them.

Therrin slowed, breath sharp. “We stop here.”

Judew nodded once. “Fight, then flee.”

We veered off the path toward the edge of a sparse woodland where a massive stone lay half-buried, as if a chunk of the mountain had fallen ages ago.

Therrin leapt down first and lifted me off the saddle with surprising ease. Kaelira slid down with Judew’s help, her legs trembling.

Therrin guided us quickly to the shelter of the fallen stone. He placed us in the narrow space between rock and earth, partly hidden by jutting roots and low brush.

He crouched in front of me, his face inches from mine. “Stay here until we are done. Do not move, no matter what you hear.”

“I can help,” I whispered, the words breaking out before I could stop them. “I can fight.”

Therrin’s expression hardened instantly. “Do not be ridiculous,” he said, voice low but fierce. “Not in that body. You may still remember how to fight, but these men are too strong, too many, and I will not see anyone reach you.”

His hand cupped the side of my arm, warm and firm. “I will not let them touch you.”

My throat tightened. Kaelira grabbed his sleeve and whispered, “Please… come back.”

Judew stepped beside him, drawing his blade. “We will.”

And then we saw all four of them — Therrin, Judew, Pattin, Sedrick — turn toward the oncoming riders and run into the fight.

---

The fight began before I even realized the riders had fully closed the distance, and just before they clashed, Judew and his two companions slipped identical dark masks over their faces, hiding their features as if this were a practiced ritual for battles they did not intend to be recognized in, of course, because outcasts who dared fight guards or dark clothed mages in these lands were hunted without mercy.

Steel clashed against steel, the sound sharp and violent in the cold morning air. From our shelter beneath the fallen stone, Kaelira and I watched with our breaths held tight in our throats.

Judew reached the first enemy like a crashing wave. In that single heartbeat, I understood.

He was Ironmind. A strong one. His movements were clean, precise, unshakable. A man struck at him from behind, and without even turning his head, Judew lifted an arm to block, pivoted, and slammed his attacker to the ground. It was the same aura Prince Eryndor carried, that sense of unyielding focus. He could take on several men at once and not falter.

Pattin revealed himself next. Embercraft. Flames sparked along his forearms as he parried a blow, the fire licking up his blade. His swings were fierce and hot, crude but effective, burning back anyone who came too close.

Sedrick moved like wind. Literally. Windsway wrapped around him, soft and quick. He ducked under a sword, leapt over another, his light steps almost gliding across the dirt as if carried by the air itself. He struck fast, backed away faster.

All three were impressive.

But Therrin…

Therrin was different.

He did not glow with affinity. No flame, no wind, no shimmer of mind or magic. And yet he moved with a precision that made the hairs on my arms rise.

No pattern.

None.

One moment he fought like a disciplined soldier, the next like a street brawler, then suddenly like a trained duelist. A downward parry swept into an unexpected elbow, which rolled smoothly into a disarming twist I had never seen him use before.

He was brutally fast. Too fast.

Every strike his opponent made, he deflected, redirected, or punished. He never missed. His body flowed exactly where it needed to be every time, as if he could see attacks before they came.

I had watched Therrin fight before. Many times. He was always good. Better than us, better than most boys our age. Even older knights had trouble with him.

But this?

This was not the Therrin I knew.

This was something else entirely.

Kaelira whispered, her voice trembling, “How… how is he doing that?”

I could only shake my head. My heart pounded unevenly. Something inside me whispered danger, awe, confusion.

“He has no affinity,” I said softly, as if speaking too loud might shatter the moment. “At least… not one we ever saw.”

Yet he fought like a man carrying echoes of a dozen different styles, each one borrowed, sharpened, and executed perfectly.

The enemy pressed harder.

But our four held the line.

And I prayed they could hold it long enough to survive.

---

The longer the fight dragged on, the more wrong our estimate became.

At first it had seemed like twenty. Hard, but possible.

Then more riders emerged from the trees.

Then more came down the slope.

Then more stepped from behind the rocks.

Our breath caught as the truth settled in.

We had been wrong. Very wrong.

There were far more than twenty. Twice that. Maybe more. And at the ridge beyond them, ten dark cloaked figures stood watching, unmoving. They did not join the fight. They waited.

For what?

For our men to tire?

For the fight to crumble?

For the moment Kaelira and I were left undefended?

A cold shiver crawled down my spine.

Judew and Pattin fought back to back for a moment, both panting hard. Sedrick stumbled as his windsway flickered, nearly taking a blade to the shoulder before Judew pulled him clear.

Therrin cut down another attacker, but even he was slowing, his breaths sharpening, his movements losing a fraction of their impossible ease.

Their Veilflow was thinning. I could feel it, even from shelter.

We were losing.

My hands clenched in the dirt. “They cannot keep this up,” I whispered. “They’re too tired.”

Kaelira shook her head, eyes wide with fear. “What do we do? We cannot run. We—”

Her voice died as one of the black masked fighters lashed forward and managed to slice Judew’s mask open.

The cloth tore.

Judew’s face was revealed.

Everything stopped.

The guard who had struck him paled instantly. He stumbled back as if he had attacked a ghost.

His sword dropped from his fingers.

A murmur rippled through the attackers. Confusion. Recognition. Fear.

The man stammered, “It’s him. It’s—”

Before he could finish, the leader of the black cloaked watchers shouted, “It does not matter! Kill them all! That is the order from the prince!”

Kaelira gasped. “Which prince…?”

But I already knew.

Prince Jaelric.

Of course it was him.

He did not want a bride. He wanted us dead.

But then why did the guards fear Judew’s face?

Who was he?

The answer lurked between us like a living shadow, but there was no time to reach it.

Our men were faltering. Pattin’s Embercraft sputtered out. Sedrick dropped to a knee. Judew barely caught another blow. Even Therrin’s perfect rhythm broke as he took a slash across his forearm.

“We have to help,” I whispered, pushing forward.

Kaelira grabbed my sleeve. “No—”

“I cannot watch them die.”

But then Therrin’s voice echoed in my head as sharply as if he stood beside me.

*I will not see anyone reach you.*

And somehow… those words alone held me rooted. My whole body refused to move.

Hope flickered, dim and fading.

The battle was about to break.

Then I heard it. Hoofbeats. Many.

Charging down the path.

My heart collapsed. More fighters? Reinforcements for them? It would finish us.

But then something shifted in the air.

A familiar voice shouted. “On me!”

I looked up.

Sir Alvis burst from the trees, armored and fierce. Behind him rode Sir Tavren, Sir Mollian, and a dozen more knights. And among them—

Marek. Deren. Serin. Our comrades. Our friends.

The world flipped in an instant.

The doomed battle became a winnable one.

And for the first time since the attack began… Hope returned.
