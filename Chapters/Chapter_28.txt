Chapter 28

The break lasted about an hour before we were called to continue. The guards began packing away what little camp had been set, the sound of saddles tightening and horses stamping filling the air. When Kaelira and I returned to the carriage, the sunlight had grown warmer and brighter. The road stretched ahead, winding through low hills and long meadows that glimmered with fresh green.

As the carriage started to move again, Kaelira leaned back with a soft sigh. “At least it was a moment of peace,” she said. “Though I would have traded it for an hour on horseback.”

I smiled faintly. “Agreed. I almost envy the riders. They look so free.”

Kaelira glanced at me, a small smirk tugging at her lips. “Even Therrian looked more at ease today. Did you notice? He barely hesitated when we greeted him.”

“I did,” I said. “Maybe he only needed a few days to get used to everything. Perhaps the others will too.”

“Perhaps,” she murmured. “It must be strange for them, seeing us like this.”

I nodded. “And yet, it feels stranger for us. We spent years training beside them, fighting beside them, and now they act as if we are porcelain dolls.”

Kaelira’s voice lowered. “It is unfair. They are out there, free to laugh and ride, while we sit here, our gowns catching on every movement.”

“Unfair and ridiculous,” I said, tugging slightly at the fabric near my knees. “We used to speak about freedom as if it were something noble and distant. Now I see it is something as simple as being allowed to move.”

Kaelira looked thoughtful for a long moment. “When I was still Kaelen,” she said quietly, “I used to think women were treated gently out of respect. But now… I see it is more about control. About what they are not allowed to do.”

Her words settled heavily in the space between us. “I thought the same,” I said. “We were blind to it before. I suppose it takes living inside the walls to see how narrow they are.”

Dina looked up from her place near the door. “It is the way of things, my lady. The world was not made for fairness.”

Kaelira smiled kindly at her. “Perhaps not, Dina. But that does not mean we cannot see its cracks.”

Mirna shifted beside me. “Still, it is different when you’ve felt both sides of it. I think that changes a person.”

I met her eyes and nodded slowly. “Yes, it does.”

For a while, we fell silent, each lost in thought. The rhythm of the wheels on the road filled the cabin, a steady sound that almost lulled me to calm. I watched the passing landscape through the narrow window—trees, the occasional farm, the gleam of sunlight on distant water. Everything looked peaceful, deceptively so.

Kaelira broke the quiet again. “If I could, I’d ride ahead just to feel the wind again. Just for a moment.”

“So would I,” I said. “Though I doubt Eryndor would approve.”

“Then perhaps we will do it when no one is looking,” she said, half smiling.

I laughed softly. “You first, then. I will not be blamed for dragging the princess into rebellion.”

Kaelira grinned. “Oh, you will be blamed either way. That is the beauty of being Veilbound.

We are one, remember?”

I was about to answer when the carriage jolted suddenly. A sharp sound came from outside—shouts, the thud of hooves, and the unmistakable ring of steel. The laughter died in my throat.

Dina gasped. “What was that?”

Mirna leaned toward the small window, peering out. “Swords,” she whispered. “Someone’s drawn steel.”

Kaelira’s hand found mine, steady and sure. “Stay calm,” she said, though her voice trembled slightly.

Outside, the shouting grew louder. Horses screamed, and I heard Sir Alvis’s voice cut through the chaos, commanding the guards to formation. The sound of a clash followed. A strike, then another.

“An attack,” I whispered, my heart pounding.

The horses screamed again, closer this time, and the entire carriage shook as if struck. Dina clutched the seat for balance while Mirna reached for the latch, but I stopped her with a gesture. The sounds outside were chaos: men shouting orders, steel meeting steel, hooves striking the ground like thunder. Then came the hiss of arrows cutting the air.

The sounds outside the carriage grew louder until they filled every breath. Steel against steel. Shouts of command. The crackle of Veil magic flares through the air. I could picture it all in flashes — chaos and courage tangled together.

Sir Alvis’s voice rang clearly through the din, giving orders in that steady tone of his that always managed to cut through panic. He was fighting near the lead carriage, his affinity for Stonecall manifesting as a ripple through the ground. I could feel it even from where I sat — the earth itself seemed to hum under the weight of his will. Every strike he made sent shivers through the soil, knocking attackers from their feet. He must have been truly advanced in his mastery of Stonecall to move the very ground with such precision and force, each strike showing how deeply he understood the language of the earth.

Therrin’s voice was louder still, sharp and focused. Yet something about his movements was different this time. He fought in a way I had never seen before, faster and more precise than ever. Each turn of his blade and sweep of his arm brought down one or two masked attackers as though he anticipated their every move. It was beyond the Therrin I remembered — sharper, more deliberate. How was this possible? He must have trained relentlessly, or perhaps there was something new within him that I could not yet understand.

Joval and Nerran fought nearby, close enough that I could hear their shouted warnings. Joval’s hands glowed faintly red — Embercraft. Each motion left a trail of smoke as he struck out, using short bursts of flame to blind the enemy and ignite their spears. Nerran was quieter, relying on focus and training alone; every movement of his blade was deliberate, unshaken, as if he were made of the same steel he wielded.

Arrows struck the side of the carriage, splintering wood. Dina whimpered and covered her head, while Mirna muttered a prayer beneath her breath. Kaelira and I exchanged a look — neither of us dared open the door again.

I pressed my palm against the wall, feeling the trembling of the ground. The sounds of magic and violence outside made my stomach twist. I wanted to move, to help them, but I knew stepping outside would only make me a target. I hated the helplessness of it. The knowledge that, in this body, I might not even be able to lift a sword properly burned through me like shame.

I glanced down at the small blade tucked beside the seat. My old habits stirred — muscle memory from years of training. I could still fight if I had to. The sword looked heavier than I remembered, but perhaps my body would remember even if my strength did not.

Kaelira reached for me, her eyes steady. “If they reach us, we fight together.”

I nodded, though fear clung to my chest like a weight. My thoughts slipped into darker places — what would happen if we were taken? What might they do to us if the guards fell and the carriage was breached? I pushed the thought aside, but it lingered like a shadow.

A sudden cry broke through the noise. A man’s shout, raw and pained. I froze. The voice was familiar.

I turned toward the window just as Mirna pulled the curtain aside. Outside, through the chaos of dust and movement, I saw Joval stumble. His sword fell from his hand, his arm slick with blood. He dropped to one knee, clutching the wound as another blow narrowly missed him.

“Joval!” I cried before I could stop myself.

Kaelira caught my arm and pulled me back from the window. “Stay down,” she hissed. But I could not look away.

The man who struck him wore a mask, his blade curved and darkened with Veil-forged steel. He moved like someone trained — not a bandit, not a wanderer. Whoever they were, they were organized.

Joval tried to rise, his jaw clenched in pain, but another masked figure closed in. Therrin appeared then. The double-kick knocked both attackers backward as he reached Joval’s side.

Even from where I sat, I could feel the force of his anger.

The fighting was far from over, but for a moment, seeing him there, standing firm against the tide, I felt something I hadn’t since the first scream — hope.

Kaelira’s face hardened, her calm cracking only for an instant. “We need to be ready,” she said. “If they reach the carriage... ”

I cut her off, listening. A voice shouted her name outside, muffled by the wood. “Keep her safe!” it was Sir Alvis’s voice, sharp with urgency.

Before we could move, the door burst open and Therrin appeared, pale but determined. “Stay inside. Do not open the door for anyone but me.”

He shut it again before either of us could speak. The horses lurched, the carriage jolted forward, and we realized they were trying to move us away from the fight.
