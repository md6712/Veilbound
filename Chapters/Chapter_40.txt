Chapter 40

I did not realize how tired I was until the doors closed behind us.

Not the kind of tired that settles in the limbs after travel,but something deeper, quieter.

The kind that seeps into the bones when too many things have changed too quickly, and the world refuses to slow down long enough for you to understand who you’ve become inside it.

Mirna insisted on the bath.

I tried to protest, half-heartedly, but she had already begun drawing the water, steam rising soft and pale against the carved stone walls. It felt strange, being tended to like this. Not unpleasant. Just… unfamiliar.

No. That wasn’t true.

It was becoming familiar. And that was the unsettling part.

The water was warm, too warm at first, and I lowered myself slowly, breath catching as it wrapped around me. My body still didn’t feel entirely like mine in moments like these. Not wrong. Not foreign. Just… newly understood. Like reading a language I had always known but never spoken aloud.

Mirna washed my hair gently, her fingers careful, practiced.

“You should rest after,” she murmured. “Tomorrow will be… long.”

She didn’t say dangerous. Or frightening. She didn’t need to.

I nodded, closing my eyes.

----------

I must have slept.

Not deeply. Not peacefully. But enough that when I woke, the room had shifted into that quiet stillness that only exists at the edge of night, when even the castle seems to hold its breath.

Mirna had dimmed the lamps. The air felt cooler.

For a moment, I didn’t remember where I was.

Then it came back.

The ritual. The court. The journey ahead.

And her.

Kaelira.

I sat up slowly, pushing the covers aside. My reflection caught briefly in the polished metal mirror across the room, long hair, softened features, still mine, and not mine at all.

I hesitated only a moment before standing.

“I’m going to see her,” I whispered.

It helped that our chambers were secretly connected. The door was hidden behind a narrow panel near the wardrobe, something I might never have noticed if it hadn’t been shown to us earlier. I pressed against it and felt it give with a soft click.

I did not need to fully dress. A simple nightgown was enough, the linen light against my skin as I slipped through the passage. Before stepping in, I paused and gave a quiet tap against the frame, more out of instinct than necessity.

Dina looked up at once.

For a brief moment, surprise crossed her face, then it softened as she recognized me.

“My lady,” she said quietly, as if reading my face. “She’s awake.”

She stepped aside without hesitation. Few would be allowed to enter like this, unannounced and unguarded, but I was her Veilbound. No one stopped me. No one questioned it.

Kaelira sat near the window, her hair unbound, pale strands falling freely over her shoulders. She looked… smaller, somehow. Not in form, but in the way she held herself, as if the weight of tomorrow had pressed inward, drawing her quiet.

When she saw me, something in her expression shifted.

Relief.

“You couldn’t sleep either?” she asked.

I shook my head, stepping closer. “No.”

A pause. Then, softer “I thought you might not.”

Dina, ever perceptive, rose quietly. “I’ll prepare the brushes,” she said, already moving. I didn’t realize what she meant until she placed one into my hand.

I froze. Kaelira turned, shifting slightly so I could reach her hair. My fingers hesitated at first, unsure, then began to move. Slowly. Carefully. It was not the first time, but certainly the first with others watching.

The strands were softer than I expected. Silk-like. Familiar, in a way that startled me. We said nothing for a while. Only the quiet rhythm of the brush moving through her hair filled the room. It should have been awkward. It wasn’t. That was the strange part.

“For fairness,” she said.

I turned, sitting beside her.

Her touch was gentler than mine had been. Slower. More careful.

As if she feared breaking something.

“Rinya,” she said after a while. The name still caught me sometimes. But less than before.

“Yes?”

“… Are you afraid?”

The question lingered between us. I considered lying. It would have been easy. But there was no point. She didn't need to ask. She already knew.

“Yes,” I said simply.

Her hand paused slightly in my hair.

“I feel guilty,” she said softly. “You should not have to suffer. It should have been only me. But I am happy to have you. You make it much easier for me. Thank you.”

A breath left me, something lighter than I expected.

We sat in that shared truth for a moment.

“Tomorrow,” she said quietly.

“Yes.”

“We will stand before their court. Their king. Their queen.” Her voice wavered slightly. “And him.”

I knew who she meant.

Jaelric.

I felt the tremor before she even spoke further, the tightness in her chest, the quiet dread that sat beneath her composure.

“They will look at me,” she continued. “Not as I was. Not as I am. But as something… made.”

She set the brush down gently.

“They will look,” I said, “because they do not understand. And what they do not understand, they judge.”

She gave a faint, humorless smile. “That is not comforting.”

“No,” I admitted. “But it is honest.”

She turned slightly toward me, her eyes searching mine. “And what of him?”

I did not need to ask which prince she meant.

“I do not know,” I said. “But I saw enough.”

Her shoulders tensed. “He did not even look at us.”

“That may be the worst of it,” I replied softly. “Not hatred. Not anger. Just… indifference.”

Silence settled again. “He will choose,” she whispered.

“Yes.”

“And I will have to accept it.”

I felt that truth cut through both of us at once. “Yes.”

Her hand tightened slightly in my hair before loosening again.

“I am afraid I will fail,” she said. “Not as a princess. Not in court.” She hesitated. “But as… this.”

I reached for her hand. “You won’t.”

“You cannot know that.”

“I do,” I said quietly. “Because you are still you. And I will be there.”

She looked at me then, really looked.

“And that makes it easier,” she admitted.

“It makes it bearable,” I corrected.

A small breath escaped her, something between a laugh and a sigh.

“Then tomorrow,” she said, “we face it together.”

“Together,” I echoed.

And for the first time since we had entered this city, the word felt strong enough to hold.
