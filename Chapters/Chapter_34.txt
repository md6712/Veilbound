Chapter 34

We chose the first inn that did not reek of stale ale or sweat. A narrow place tucked between a bakery and a cloth merchant, its lantern-lit windows glowing a warm amber against the darkening sky. Therrin opened the door first, stepping in to judge the room before allowing us inside. Only when he gave a small nod did Kaelira and I follow.

The inn was crowded but not rowdy. Merchants finishing their meals, travelers rubbing the dust from their boots, a few locals laughing over cheap wine. It smelled of roasting meat, woodsmoke, and something earthy simmering in a cauldron.

We found an empty table near the wall, half hidden behind a wooden beam. Therrin placed himself where he could watch both the door and the stairs. Judew sat across from us. Pattin and Sedrick followed reluctantly, still looking down after the shame of earlier.

A serving woman approached, wiping her hands on her apron. "What will it be?"

"Soup," I said, before Therrin could speak. "Something warm."

Falcrest was known for its river trade, and the innkeeper’s specialty reflected that. We were served steaming bowls of herb-bone broth, a thick, hearty soup of riverfish stock, barley, chopped root vegetables, wild herbs, and shreds of smoked meat. It was the kind of meal meant to restore strength to travelers who had walked far.

As the bowls were set down, I kept my eyes on Pattin and Sedrick. They shifted uncomfortably under my stare.

Judew noticed.

He cleared his throat and spoke before I did. "They are fools," he said. "They laugh because they have never been asked to carry anything of weight. I apologize for the insult, though it was not mine."

Pattin and Sedrick murmured weakly, "We are sorry," before falling silent again.

Judew leaned in slightly. "And you must not let that wretched play settle too deeply in you. Common folk do not know the truth. They reach for the easiest cruelty and call it wit. That is not the whole of Veylan."

His words were curious. Too careful. Too eager to soothe.

I studied him, suspicious, not knowing why his concern bothered me so much. His softness felt almost artificial. "Why would you care so much about how we feel? Or how Veylan receives us?"

Judew paused, then answered slowly. "Because I saw what it did to you, and it should not have had that right. That is all."

Something in his voice felt heavy. Personal. But he did not elaborate.

We ate in silence for a time. The soup warmed my throat and settled my nerves, though the ache in my heart remained.

At the table beside us, two men were drinking heavily, their speech already growing slurred.

Their argument rose above the hum of the room.

"Jaelric is the true crown prince! Mighty as the mountains! The gods touched his blood!" one insisted, slamming his cup down so hard ale splashed across the table.

The other snorted. "Jaelric is an arrogant prince who never leaves his gilded cage. Rumors say he is vile and quick-tempered, and he treats his closest friends and guards like dirt.

Maedric is better. Smarter. People like him. He talks to the small folk. He should be crowned." He waved his cup as if delivering a royal decree.

"Nonsense," the first man barked. "Maedric is weak. Soft. And he would trip over his own cloak on a battlefield. He cannot even swing a sword without shaking."

The second man leaned in conspiratorially, breath thick with drink. "Oh, when Jaelric marries that Elarian thing, he will be a laughingstock in all the courts. Imagine it, the mighty crown prince forced to bed a creature who was a man a year ago. And with how stupid he is, he might not even notice the difference. The ugly princess will probably be used every night, whether she wants it or not!"

The first man choked on his laughter. "Ha. Better Maedric than Jaelric. If someone must suffer, let the canny second prince bear the burden. Or perhaps the king will simply force Maedric to marry her. He is better suited anyway. The crown prince should wed a real woman, someone who can bear many children and make the kingdom prosper."

The second added loudly, "Maybe they will keep her behind curtains so no one faints at the sight. Gods, can you imagine waking up to that face every morning? I'd rather cut my own eyes out!"

Cheers and crude laughter followed, each louder and crueler than before.

Their laughter stung sharper than the play. Kaelira stared into her bowl, hands trembling again.

Pattin suddenly stood from our table. His face was red, whether from shame or anger I could not tell. Judew reached out quickly, trying to grasp at his sleeve as if to stop him, but Pattin had already reached their table. He strode toward the drunk men. "You there," he snapped, sharper than I had ever heard from him. "Mind your tongues. You do not speak of the crown prince, or of his betrothed, in that fashion."

One of the drunks glared blearily. "Who are *you* to tell us anything? Be gone, traveler.

Mind your own business."

Pattin stiffened, and for a moment I saw something pass through him, something like the start of a confession, as if he wanted to declare something of himself. But he swallowed it down.

He looked instead toward the side of the room, where two city guards stood near the staircase.

"Very well," he said loudly. "Then perhaps the guards should hear you say it. These men insult the prince and spread filth about his future bride." I sensed a jolt from Kaelira's side at the mention of the future bride, a sharp tremor that ran through her as if the words had struck something deep. Through the Veilbond I felt her fear, raw and cold, the grief of being stripped of her station as a prince and now bound to become the bride of a vile Veylan heir she did not choose.

The guards turned.

The two drunk men paled instantly. "No, no! We said nothing of the sort! We were just talking! Nothing wrong here!"

The guards approached their table, and the men shrank in their seats like scolded children, insisting innocence.

Therrin rose at once. "Enough. We go upstairs now. All of us."

His tone left no room for argument, and neither of us even tried. Perhaps before all of this, Kaelira and I would have protested, insisted on staying, on choosing for ourselves. But after being mocked, torn apart by that cruel play, and then overhearing the filth those drunk men spewed, something inside us had collapsed. We no longer had the strength to resist. We needed someone, anyone, to take control so we would not fall apart.

Therrin had stepped into that role without hesitation. And we simply followed.

We left the common room behind and climbed the narrow staircase, the noise fading behind us.

Step by step, the tension slowly eased from my chest. Only when we reached the small corridor where our rooms waited did I finally breathe again.

Once inside the privacy of our shared chamber, Kaelira and I paused. We looked at each other, neither of us speaking, both of us holding everything in by the thinnest threads. Then suddenly, we moved at the same time and embraced each other in a tight, desperate hug.

The moment our arms wrapped around each other, the dam inside us broke.

We sobbed, loudly, helplessly, without the slightest control. It was unlike anything we had ever done before. Not during our childhood, not during the trials, not even in the days leading up to the ritual. This was raw, wounded grief, born from humiliation and fear and exhaustion.

We stumbled backward together until we reached the bed, and then we sank onto it, still holding each other as if letting go would shatter us completely.

Our tears soaked each other’s shoulders. Our breaths came in ragged bursts. Every time one of us began to quiet, the other would tremble and set us both off again.

We cried until our throats burned and our chests ached. Until it felt like there was nothing left inside us but empty space and quiet shaking.

Only when the tears finally ran out did our bodies begin to calm. Her breathing steadied first. She pressed her face against my collarbone and clutched the fabric at my waist.

Without thinking, we lay down together, still wrapped in each other’s arms.

That night, she curled into me as the small spoon, fragile and warm, and I held her close, promising silently that I would not let the world break her.

Only then did sleep find us.
