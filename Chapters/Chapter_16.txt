Chapter 16

The day before our departure, we were summoned to the main hall. The king had called for everyone involved in the journey to Veylan. It was to be the final assembly before we left.

Kaelira came to fetch me herself.

Earlier that morning, her maid had visited our chambers with a quiet message: the princess wished for us to enter the hall together. I felt the same. Being with her made everything easier. The world outside us felt sharp and wrong, but when we stood side by side, something aligned.

It was still excruciating to face those who had known us before. They had once seen us as proud young men, strong and destined for command. Now they looked on, confused, awkward, uncertain, as if unsure what we were now. Girls in gowns. Symbols in silk. Delicate things to be married off or displayed.

She needed support.

I needed support.

We needed each other. Yesterday, when we touched, when she held my hand, I felt a stillness I hadn’t known in weeks. I wanted that again. I needed it when I stepped into that room.

I had been ready for some time, waiting quietly. Mirna had helped me into my outfit, a long, cornflower-blue gown with silver trim that brushed the floor with every step. The bodice was structured, soft against my ribs, and laced tight enough to make me stand taller than I felt.

The sleeves hugged my arms in fitted velvet, and the neckline sat modestly high, but even so, I felt exposed. The fabric was light, elegant, too soft, too telling.

I waited for the princess to come to me. I would have gone to her myself, but Mirna had insisted, gently, firmly, that Kaelira wished to come here first. So I stayed, watching the light shift on the wall, heart fluttering for reasons I didn’t fully understand.

When she arrived, Mirna let them in without ceremony. Kaelira stepped through the doorway in a rose-gold gown embroidered with glints of glass-thread that caught the light with every movement. The fabric hugged her waist and then flowed outward in gentle waves, delicate as breath. Her hair had been swept into a half-braid, adorned with tiny rose-shaped pins that shimmered faintly under the morning sun. She looked as overwhelmed as I felt.

We embraced without words. The contact was immediate and necessary. It felt like a balm, or a drug. Like something inside us knew it needed this to breathe.

When we pulled apart, she didn’t let go of my hands.

"Rinya, thank you," she said.

I knew what she meant. We hadn’t spoken much since the transformation, not out loud. But there was no need. We understood one another now in ways no words could match. Our bond was more than magical, it was emotional, spiritual, and intimate.

I just nodded.

She smiled faintly. "Ready?"

Again, I nodded.

We left my room together.

Outside, a knight waited just as he had the day before, Sir Mollian. Loyal and precise. He bowed deeply. I curtsied, though every time I did, I felt the reminder of what I had become.

Compared to him, I was so small. So breakable. He stood tall in steel and leather, and I in silk and quiet breath. He was strength and certainty. I was... not.

Together, Kaelira and I walked down the corridor toward the grand hall. The sound of our skirts brushing the stone floor echoed strangely in the silence between us, layers of silk whispering like unfamiliar voices. It was an odd, fluttering sound, one that would've been laughable a few weeks ago. But now, it barely registered. Among all the other strange new things, it was perhaps the least of our worries.

When we arrived, the guards opened the great doors.

Inside, the room was alive with motion. Council mages, generals, diplomats, and knights circled the massive central table, its surface covered in a map of the realm. Colored markers showed routes, escorts, campsites, and risks.

But the moment they saw us, they stopped.

A hush fell like a veil. Dozens of eyes turned. And I held tighter to Kaelira’s hand.

To everyone’s surprise, the king stepped forward to greet his daughter. I dropped into a deep curtsy, the motion tugging at my tightly laced bodice. From the corner of my eye, I could see my former companions, those who had once trained beside me, standing tall at the side of the map like statues in armor. Why is it that I must curtsy in this delicate dress while they stand like warriors, dressed in heavy fabric and steel, part of the plan rather than merely escorted by it?

The king stopped before Kaelira, looking her over from head to toe. I could feel her stiffen beside me, mortified under his gaze.

Then he nodded solemnly. "You are so beautiful, my daughter. You are the jewel of this kingdom. And you so much resemble your late sister."

I had never thought of that. The king’s bond with the late princess had been unshakable. Her death had shattered him for months. Now he had a daughter again, and I saw it, in his eyes, a shine just short of tears. He wanted to say more, I could feel it. But not here. Not in front of everyone. How do I know that? Is this what Stillmark does, let me feel emotions beneath the surface?

I sensed Kaelira’s inner conflict. She wanted to reach for him, to hug her father. Something inside her yearned for it. But she was afraid, afraid of how it would look, how it would feel, after being a prince.

After a long pause, the king gently reached out, took her hand, and drew her forward. Then, with quiet dignity, he leaned in and kissed her forehead.

Lightly.

Fatherly.

The king then escorted his daughter to the side of the map table. As they passed, everyone bowed or curtsied to the princess, everyone except for the queen and the two princes, who offered her quiet, approving smiles.

I tagged along, a step behind, trying to make myself small.

Then the king turned and addressed the hall. "General Maeren," he said, nodding toward my father, "the floor is yours. Share the plan."

My father stepped forward, straight-backed and unshaken, as he always had been.

"There are two columns in this escort," he began, gesturing to the map. "The primary will carry the princess and her Veilbound, Lady Rinya, along the eastern trade road. It is the better-paved, more visible route. We will not hide. The convoy must be seen."

He placed markers, wooden sigils carved in the shapes of our royal crest, along the winding path.

"The core of the procession will include four carriages: one for the princess and Lady Rinya, one for noble support, and two for provisions and healers. Surrounding them will be an honor guard comprised of the seven knights selected by the crown." He looked toward the young men who had once been my companions. "Their role is protection, but also presentation. They are to be visible. Veylan watches even now."

He moved his hand to the edge of the map. "Prince Eryndor will command the rear vanguard. His unit, seasoned knights, will monitor the terrain, scout ahead, and intervene if the front column is blocked."

There was a pause as he moved a second set of pieces.

"A secondary party, three days behind, will carry false banners. A distraction, should any saboteurs attempt to intercept us, they may strike at shadows."

He looked up at the gathered room. "The greatest threat will be during the border crossing.

There, we are subject to Veylan’s terrain, and Veylan’s politics. The Council insists we make the crossing during daylight, with visible ceremonial presence, to avoid misinterpretation."

He tapped one final marker near the Veylan border. "If anything happens, this is the place.

Our magic defenses will be strongest then, with three Veilcast mages traveling in rotation, supported by Mage Halren as Veilwarden."

A murmur passed through the court.

Then the king nodded slowly. "Thank you, General. That will be all for now."

Silence returned. And all eyes turned again to Kaelira.

And then, to me.

I wasn’t sure why. Perhaps because I stood beside her. Or because I was part of this story now, not as a knight or a noble’s son, but as something they didn’t know how to define.
