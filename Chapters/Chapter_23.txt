Chapter 23

The morning began with another ordeal—dressing. Once, as a boy, it took me minutes to prepare: a shirt, trousers, boots, and I was done. Now, every step felt like a drawn-out ritual. Layers of linen, lacing stays, smoothing skirts, arranging hair, choosing the right shoes, then the finishing touches: ribbons, pins, and jewelry. Each piece pressed somewhere, the stays holding my ribs tight, the fabric heavy and close around my legs, the sleeves brushing my arms yet tugging when I moved. My maids, including Mirna, worked with steady care, dressing me in a sky-blue gown of soft silk with a fitted bodice embroidered in silver thread, the skirt falling in layered folds to brush the floor. Sheer sleeves clung lightly to my arms. A ribbon in my hair matched the gown, and delicate pearl drops hung from my ears, ensuring I looked as refined as the company I was about to keep.

Later, as we prepared to depart for the terrace, I found myself speaking quietly with Mirna while she adjusted the fall of my skirts.

“I wish I could wear something less…feminine,” I admitted under my breath. “At least something like the travel clothes, still proper enough, but looser, so I could walk easier and breathe easier, without my legs feeling trapped with every step.”

Mirna glanced up, hiding a smile. “It would be easier to walk, my lady. But you’d have every eye wondering why you’d chosen it.”

“I’d rather walk easily than have every stitch and ribbon in place,” I muttered. “When I was a boy, it took minutes to get dressed. Now it feels like an entire campaign before I’m ready.”

She smoothed the fabric over my hips, her fingers quick but gentle. “The truth is, my lady, people notice the difference. A less restrictive skirt might make you more comfortable, but you’d be the only one wearing it. And that, too, would draw notice.”

I sighed. “So either I suffer the gowns, or I suffer the gossip?”

Mirna’s lips twitched again. “Exactly, my lady. But between you and me, if I could choose, I’d let you wear whatever made you move like yourself.”

Her words warmed me, though they didn’t make the skirt any looser.

By the time I stepped onto the terrace, the promised tea party awaited. The table was set in the open air, shaded by silk canopies, the gardens stretching below us. From here, we could see the men in the yard, some deep in discussion, others training.

Two of our companions, Joval, whose Embercraft affinity flared in quick bursts of heat and flame, and Marek, wielding his Windsway with sharp currents of air, were in mock combat, weaving their magic into playful strikes and counters, teasing each other with flickers of fire and gusts of wind. Nearby, Deren and Serin stood by one of the horses in the yard, their heads bent together in quiet conversation, the animal shifting its weight patiently between them. Further off, Sir Tavren was speaking with Prince Eryndor, Lord Halric, and flanked by Sir Alvis and Sir Alric, their postures alert yet carrying the ease of seasoned soldiers, as if their conversation wove effortlessly between matters of duty and the small practicalities of travel. Therrin, however, was conspicuously absent from the yard, his whereabouts unknown.

The ladies were already gathered, dressed in their finery. Serenya in pale gold, Kaelira in rose silk, Lady Elsin in layered ivory lace, Lady Maerel in deep violet with a silver sash.

Our host’s wife, Lady Anire, wore soft lavender, and her daughter, Evelyne, a gown of cream embroidered with pale green vines. It was such a colorful gathering, a striking contrast to the men beyond, who all wore either dark blue or dark gray. I took my place among the former group, skirts settling reluctantly around me.

As I entered, I noticed that the princess had already begun to look uneasy among the women; the conversation had turned to nothing but men, their habits, their virtues, their faults.

“My younger brother still sends you roses every month?” Lady Anire asked Lady Maerel with a knowing smile. “He’s far too fond of you, I think. I’ve half a mind to hide the gardener’s shears so he can’t keep raiding his own gardens.”

Lady Maerel returned the smile with a faint arch of her brow. “Fond enough that I heard Lord Bennett wanted to join this very party, but His Majesty disapproved. Perhaps he feared your brother would charm every lady at the table before the tea cooled.”

That earned a ripple of laughter from the group, Lady Anire chuckling behind her cup, Evelyne giggling outright, even Elsin hiding a smile behind her hand. Kaelira and I exchanged a brief glance, each of us forcing polite smiles that didn’t quite reach our eyes.

“I swear Sir Tavren has the most gallant posture,” Evelyne added, glancing toward the yard.

She was unmarried, and her eyes lingered perhaps a touch too long.

Her mother, Lady Anire, gave a soft laugh. “Too old for you, my dear. We must find you a husband nearer your age, perhaps one of the younger lords would do.”

That earned another round of laughter from the table. Kaelira and I, however, exchanged another startled glance. Which of our friends were they speaking of? Was this a serious proposal or just jest?

Evelyne tilted her head toward the yard. “The one lifting stones is handsome,” she said with unabashed cheer, clearly meaning Deren, whose Stonecall affinity was on display as he toyed with a block of granite near the training yard. That only made the ladies laugh harder, leaving Kaelira and me smiling faintly, unsure whether to join in or retreat into our teacups.

“But soldiers’ hands can be far too rough,” Serenya said knowingly, her betrothal giving her words a certain weight.

“Better rough than idle,” Elsin countered with a sly smile. Then, with the faintest flicker of irony, she added, “At least a plant has the decency to grow quietly, I can understand them far better than I can an idle man.” That drew a louder round of laughter. Given her Greentongue affinity, the jest carried an extra sting, after all, she truly could understand plants far better than most men, just like Serenya.

Then the topic shifted to affinities.

Elsin leaned back with a shrug. “I like my Greentongue, don’t get me wrong, but sometimes I wish I had something more…useful. Even Watersoul, or Beastmark, would do. At least then I could do more than coax roses and brambles.”

Watersoul is the most common feminine affinity, and most girls with an affinity are Watersoul. Beastmark, though neutral, is less common, and even then it appears more often in boys than in girls. Those with Beastmark often develop an affinity toward certain groups of animals, which differs between the sexes, girls more often connect with birds or small cats, while boys tend toward larger creatures. Some scholars believe this is due to the size and strength of the animal needing to match the bearer’s own body.

“I’m Watersoul,” Evelyne chimed in, “and I could use something better as well. Most feminine affinities—like Watersoul and Greentongue—are pretty but useless. The masculine ones are the ones that can win wars or truly change the world.”

Lady Anire set down her cup with a quiet clink. “Girls, you mustn’t underestimate your power,” she said firmly. “Besides, the masculine affinities would contradict your very souls.

We have all heard of those born with the wrong affinity, and almost all of them went mad, or became dangerous.”

I found myself wondering, with a strange chill, if Lady Anire’s words applied to me. If I had stayed a boy, would I have died when my true affinity surfaced? Or lost my mind like those she warned about? Or would I have somehow developed a masculine one instead, avoiding all of this entirely?

“What’s the use of being able to project calm?” I thought. “If someone attacks, being calm won’t stop them. I need strength.” Kaelira glanced toward me, her eyes betraying the same conflicted agreement.

Lady Maerel’s eyes met mine, and for a moment it felt as though she had plucked the thought straight from my mind. “Many say, for example, that a Stillmark, rare as it is, is useless,”

she began, her tone patient but certain. “But they are wrong.”

She went on to explain. “A Stillmark can steady an army before a battle, prevent riots before they spark, and anchor dangerous rituals that would otherwise fail. Heartsense healers mend wounds of the heart and mind as surely as any surgeon treats the body. A Greentongue can keep whole cities fed in times of famine or grow medicines no healer could craft alone. Watersoul can save lives in droughts, guide ships through storms, and even turn the tide in sieges.

Mirrorbind can unite two people so completely their combined will can sway courts or end blood feuds.”

Her gaze softened as she sipped her tea. “The world may praise the flash and fury of war, but without the quiet strengths, there would be nothing left worth saving.” She paused, then allowed herself a wry smile. “Of course, I’ll grant there’s some exaggeration in what I’ve said. A single Greentongue can coax one plant at a time, not entire fields in an instant. And to truly steady an army, you’d need a host of Stillmarks working together. But the point stands: these gifts matter, even if they lack the spectacle of fire and steel.”

All the girls listened closely, leaning in with genuine interest. I had heard that Lady Maerel was as skilled as any Veilwarden, though she did not hold the title officially, and her words carried the quiet authority of someone who could have worn that rank if she wished.

The conversation moved on to other topics, many of them distinctly feminine, centering on jewelry, gowns, and the latest court fashions. There was gossip as well, whispered and laughing, about who wore what to which feast and whose taste had faltered. Kaelira and I stayed mostly silent through it all, our polite smiles fixed in place as we let the tide of talk wash past us. I kept one eye on the yard beyond the terrace, and I suspected Kaelira did the same. The contrast between the polite, lilting chatter around our table and the sharp, physical life of the training yard was so stark that no one could have denied it.

After not so long, we were interrupted by Lord Halric and Prince Eryndor, who approached the terrace with warm smiles and an invitation. They asked if we might join them for a walk through the fields and woods surrounding their magnificent castle.

“I’m not certain these gowns are suited for a walk,” Lady Maerel said, glancing at the delicate fabrics and long hems. “We may need something more practical.”

Lord Halric smiled in reassurance. “The path is well-maintained and flat, my lady. You should have no trouble at all in your current attire.”

For a fleeting moment, I hoped we might be allowed to change. Despite the tedious process of redressing, it would have been worth it to be free of the tight, attention-drawing gown.

At first, my hope faded when Lord Halric assured us the path would be fine. But Lady Anire unexpectedly shook her head. “No, of course we should change our attire. These gowns are fine in the garden but would be ruined in the mud. Let us change.”

With that, we were escorted back to our rooms, where the maids set about helping us into more suitable gowns for the walk. As I stood still for laces and pins once again, I decided the freedom of the new dress hardly made up for the hassle.

Nearly an hour later, we rejoined the men in the yard, where they approached one by one to offer their hands in the formal fashion. Prince Eryndor stepped forward first, bowing slightly as he presented his arm to Lady Anire, who accepted with practiced grace. Lord Halric followed, offering a courteous bow before extending his arm to the princess. Kaelira’s acceptance came with visible reluctance, her chin lifting slightly as if to remind herself of the role she now played.

The first son of Lord Halric approached Serenya with a warm smile, and she placed her hand lightly on his sleeve. Joval, Evelyne’s cousin and my old friend, strode over to Elsin with easy confidence, and she met him with a teasing smile. Evelyne herself brightened when Deren appeared before her; she took his arm without hesitation, clearly pleased, and I couldn’t help but wonder who had planted the idea in Deren’s mind.

Lady Maerel was next, greeting Lord Alvis with a nod before they set off side by side, their conversation flowing at once.

Not that I wanted someone to lead me, but I was the only one left. I stood waiting, scanning the familiar faces. Therrin was nowhere to be found—where was he? No one came forward until Cale, with his characteristic boldness, stepped ahead. He offered his arm with a smirk I disliked instantly.

“Looks like you’ve been left to me, Lady Rinya,” he murmured, the words wrapped in a strange, smirking tone that made it unclear whether he was mocking me or savoring the moment, voice low enough for only me to hear.

“How unfortunate for us both,” I replied evenly, keeping my tone polite enough to pass as playful.

I hated the idea of taking it, yet to keep face before everyone, I placed my hand lightly on his sleeve and forced my expression into polite neutrality.
