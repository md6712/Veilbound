Chapter 24

We set out from the castle grounds, the path ahead bright with late-morning light. The spring air was fresh, carrying a faint sweetness from the nearby orchards, but the walk began on a note of discomfort for me that had little to do with the weather.

I glanced at Cale beside me. Once, we had been nearly the same height, both of us still filling out the frames of boys. He had been a touch broader in the shoulders, but not by much. Now, after my transformation, the difference felt enormous. He seemed to tower over me, his stride longer, his shadow stretching ahead like that of a giant. And Cale was not even the most imposing of my friends.

He started with small talk that curdled almost immediately.

"You look beautiful today," he said, voice dripping with smugness. "Pity you didn’t paint your face like your sister, then you might have been truly stunning." Mirna had suggested I wear a touch of paint before we left, but I had resisted with all my will.

I felt my shoulders stiffen. I replied flatly, "I am sure you can find a less patronizing way to say that, especially since all of this was supposed to be for you, not me."

He only chuckled and leaned closer. "You know, you were always meant to be the girl. I heard you are a Stillmark. That is for women, through and through."

"All that is important is that you are now the Veilbound," he said with a sinister edge to his voice, "and a beautiful one. I could even dream about you."

I gave him a sharp look, holding his gaze until the smirk faltered. "Then wake up, Cale, because that fantasy will never be real. Not now, not ever."

Before I could roll my eyes again, he smirked. "You are even more beautiful than your sister." The words were a calculated insult wrapped in flattery, and I felt my composure strain. Cale had long shown interest in Serenya, though she had always been beyond his reach, older than he and already courted by the prince. Now that same covetous look was turned on me, and it unsettled me all the more because I remembered the coarse and vivid things he had once said about the women he desired, Serenya among them.

"That is no compliment, Cale. It diminishes us both, and I cannot recall ever truly counting you as a friend."

"Even then, you would still be mine," he said without pause. "You may not accept it now, but I know you. You crave it as well."

I slowed my step, though I kept within reach of the others. As the last lady in the line, I dared not let the gap widen, least of all with Cale at my side. "Not in this life. Nor in any other. I will never marry a man. My life had one purpose, and I have already fulfilled it. Do not humiliate yourself further." That was the truth of it. I could not, in any form, imagine myself with a man.

"You do not understand," he said with the tone of one delivering a lesson. "Without a husband, a woman is nothing. You cannot own property, nor live without a man’s protection. I can give you all of that. You will be my wife, and in time, you will see it serves your best interest. I will make certain of it."

I met his gaze with a cold glare. "That will happen only if I lose all sense. Your advances are beyond rude, Cale, and it is no wonder every woman you have pursued has rejected you." It struck me then, with a bitter edge, that I might indeed be powerless in this society without a man, but having one in my life was unthinkable. I would find another way in time. For now, there was no chance I would yield, and certainly not to Cale.

He laughed again, as though I had spoken in jest, but the silence I let settle between us was heavy enough that even he seemed to feel its weight.

As soon as we arrived at the edge of the cliff, the beauty of the scene stole my breath. I do not know why, but I felt I could appreciate such beauty more than I once did, the vivid flowers along the path, the bright scatter of colors across the grass, and the soft, constant murmur of flowing water.

Before us, a small river spilled over the cliff in a graceful waterfall, tumbling into a clear pond below. From there, the water rushed on and vanished over a second cliff, where it plunged again into a wide, shimmering lake. To the right, dense woods stood in watchful silence; to the left, the view stretched back toward the castle, its towers rising proud against the sky.

As the lord spoke, explaining the history of this place, I saw Kaelira moving quickly toward me. Her eyes searched my face as she asked what had happened, why I looked unsettled. In that moment it was confirmed, we could feel each other’s emotions. She had sensed it, the depth of our bond undeniable.

I told her what Cale had said. She was as shocked as I had been, though she simply took my hand to steady me. That should have been my role for her, yet here she was, lending me her calm. Then her gaze slid to Cale, sharp as a dagger, while he still wore that infuriating smirk. We both knew that if his father and mine ever set their minds on such a marriage, the king would not object and my say in the matter would be minimal. That thought filled me with a quiet, cold fear.

Then it struck me. Since my transformation, I had felt flickers of emotions from Kaelira, but I had never been able to link them to a clear cause. Now I understood. She was afraid: afraid of what awaited her with her intended, the prince of Veylan.

"I am sorry, Kaelira. I should have realized… you are also afraid of Prince Jaelric," I said, speaking of the prince of Veylan.

She gave a faint, composed smile and pressed my hand more firmly. "It is all right. I am indeed afraid of him, yet it is my duty to serve this land. You, however, had far more to lose for a cause that should have been mine alone to shoulder."

By then, the rest of our party had drifted across the cliffside. The view was broad and striking: to one side, the prince and the lord conversed in measured tones; to the other, Elsin and Evelyne stood with Sir Mollian and Sir Alric in quiet discourse. I remained with the Princess until Lady Maerel approached with a glimmer of unspoken understanding.

"I could lend Sir Alvis to you for the return walk, my dear," she said with courteous warmth.

"How very kind of you," I replied.

Her gaze lingered. "I sense you are uneasy with the young lord. Has he caused you offence?"

"It is of no consequence," I replied after a pause, allowing the weight of my words to settle.

"Even so, I would suggest you walk back in the company of your uncle," she said, indicating Sir Alvis.

"He is not my uncle," I corrected gently, although I have always regarded him as one. "He is my mother’s distant cousin."

Lady Maerel’s smile deepened. "I am aware, child. I am one of your mother’s oldest friends, and she holds him as dear as a brother." There was, I felt, more history there than I knew.

Lady Maerel clearly shared bonds with both Sir Alvis and my mother, but I chose not to press the matter.

Some of the maids and several of Lord Halric’s household had arranged a small camp for us ladies on a pre-built wooden stage beside the cliff. We were served light refreshments there and lingered for a while, our earlier conversation (mostly the talk of ladies) continuing as the men wandered off to explore the trails and edges of the forest. Some of them, such as Joval and Deren, even returned to their playful testing of fire and stone magic, though Lord Halric was quick to warn Joval when his flame leapt too near the trees.

Beyond that, little of note occurred, save for the weight of Cale’s gaze on me from time to time. It was a new kind of unease, the feeling that someone’s eyes alone could wound you.

Kaelira quietly took my hand to calm me, yet even her touch could not dispel it.

At last, the time came to return for luncheon. As promised, Sir Alvis approached and offered his arm, leaning close to whisper, “I have been ordered to walk my dear niece back to the castle, an order I dare not disobey.” His tone held the light humor of a man who valued Lady Maerel’s counsel far too much to refuse it.
