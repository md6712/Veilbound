Judew, no, Aelyon, standing in the center of it all and saying his name like a challenge.

By dawn, my thoughts felt like thread pulled too tight. Mirna helped me dress in a pale green gown with silver stitching at the cuffs, simple by court standards, but formal enough that no one could accuse me of disrespect. My hands still shook while she fastened the clasps.

"Lady Rinya," she said, keeping her voice low, "a summons came before sunrise."

I looked up. "From whom?"

"His Majesty. For you and Princess Kaelira."

My stomach tightened.

"Where?"

"The King's private council chamber." She hesitated, then added, "Prince Aelyon is expected."

Of course he was.

I thanked her, then crossed the corridor to Kaelira's rooms with my pulse beating too fast in my throat. The guard admitted me without question.

Kaelira stood near the window when I entered. She wore blue today, deep and quiet, with her hair pinned more carefully than usual. She turned as soon as I stepped inside, and the bond between us stirred at once.

Anxious.

Braced.

Trying not to show either.

"You were summoned too," I said.

She nodded. "A few moments ago."

"How are you feeling?"

She gave me a look that was almost a smile. "Like someone asked me to stand on a narrow ledge and smile while doing it."

I let out a breath that might have been a laugh if either of us had room for one.

"That sounds familiar."

For a moment we only looked at each other.

Then she reached for my hand and squeezed it once.

"Do not let me agree to anything because I am afraid," she said.

I closed my fingers around hers.

"Do not let me do the same."

We went together.

The private council chamber sat behind carved oak doors and two ranks of silent guards. Inside, the room was smaller than the throne hall but no less deliberate. A long table of dark wood filled the center. Maps and wax-sealed rolls were stacked at one end. Morning light entered through narrow windows and cut the chamber into bands of gold and shadow.

King Valtar stood near the head of the table with Lord Pattin at his side. Queen Seralyth sat already, gloved hands folded, expression composed enough to pass for calm unless one watched too closely.

Aelyon stood opposite her.

He turned when we entered, and for a moment I saw the man from the road again, not the prince who had split a court in half with a single claim. Then the look was gone, replaced by careful formality.

"Princess Kaelira. Lady Rinya." He bowed.

Kaelira inclined her head. I did the same, though mine was stiffer.

The King gestured toward the seats nearest him.

"Sit. We will speak plainly this morning."

I hoped that would be true.

When we were seated, Valtar did not waste words.

"Yesterday was disorder," he said. "Today will be order."

His gaze settled on Aelyon.

"You have returned after years of silence. You have named yourself before the court. You have made a public claim on our Elarian bride." He paused. "You will now explain yourself to all parties concerned."

Aelyon did not argue. He stepped forward until he stood between both sides of the table, where none of us could ignore him.

"I left because remaining here meant becoming a weapon in disputes I did not choose," he said. "After my mother died, every faction in this palace tried to claim me or erase me. I was too young to resist either. So I disappeared before someone decided my usefulness outweighed my life."

Queen Seralyth's mouth tightened, though she did not interrupt.

"I took another name," he continued. "Judew was safer than Aelyon. Under that name, I learned who still spoke to me for myself and who only bowed to blood."

He looked directly at Kaelira then.

"When I joined your escort on the road, I did not expect to care. I expected to observe, return, and play whatever role was required." His voice lowered slightly. "I was wrong."

The chamber felt very still.

"By the time we reached Veylan," he said, "I understood what this pact had cost you. What it had cost both of you. I saw how the court treated Princess Kaelira yesterday. I saw what my brothers intended. I decided I would not stand aside and watch her handed to men who considered cruelty a form of sport."

That line landed like stone dropped in water. Even Lord Pattin shifted.

Queen Seralyth spoke at last, her tone polished and cold.

"You speak as if righteousness excuses defiance. You humiliated this house in front of its own court."

Aelyon met her gaze.

"No, my Queen. The humiliation was already underway before I spoke."

The air thinned.

Valtar lifted a hand before the reply could turn into open conflict.

"Enough." He turned to us. "You have heard him. Now you may answer."

Kaelira sat very straight, but I felt the tremor inside her through the bond.

"I am grateful for what you did in the hall," she said to Aelyon. "You protected me when I had almost no strength left."

She paused, choosing each word with care.

"But gratitude is not consent."

No one moved.

"I crossed half the world because kings and councils decided my life for me," she said. "I cannot begin a marriage with another decision made over my head, even if the man making it is kinder than the others."

I felt something like pride rise through my fear.

Aelyon bowed his head once.

"That is fair."

Kaelira held his gaze.

"Then hear me clearly. If this is to proceed, it proceeds with terms."

Queen Seralyth gave a short, incredulous breath, but Kaelira did not look at her.

"First," Kaelira said, "there will be no rushed wedding to satisfy gossip. I will not be paraded from collapse to altar."

The King's brows drew together.

"The pact demands timeliness," he said.

"It demands fulfillment," Kaelira replied, still courteous, still steady. "Not haste at any cost."

I nearly forgot to breathe.

She went on.

"Second, my companion remains with me in all formal negotiations and in all private meetings related to this marriage. Lady Rinya is Veilbound to me. If I am expected to stand in this court, she stands beside me."

I saw the Queen's fingers press into her gloves.

"Third," Kaelira said, "Prince Aelyon will answer what he withheld from us. Not to satisfy curiosity, but because trust without truth is only a prettier form of obedience."

Silence followed.

Then Valtar looked to Aelyon.

"Can you accept these terms?"

"Yes," he said without hesitation.

Seralyth turned to the King.

"This is indulgence. She is here under treaty obligation, not as an equal negotiator."

Valtar's expression hardened.

"She is my future daughter by law and the vessel of the peace this realm requires. If she collapses again under our roof, we gain nothing from speed."

He looked back at Kaelira.

"How long do you ask?"

Kaelira considered.

"Twelve days."

Pattin spoke for the first time. "That will strain preparation schedules, Your Majesty, but it is manageable if orders are sent today."

Valtar nodded once.

"Then twelve days."

Seralyth rose.

"You would rewrite protocol for a foreign girl who arrived yesterday."

Kaelira turned to her with perfect formality.

"I arrived weeks ago, my Queen. Yesterday was only when your court chose to look at me."

For the first time since entering the chamber, I saw Seralyth lose control of her face entirely.

The King stood, ending the exchange by force of presence.

"This matter is decided. Pattin, draft the revised ceremony order and circulate it by midday."

"At once, sire."

"Princess Kaelira," Valtar said, "you and Lady Rinya will be granted access to the inner gardens during these twelve days without prior request. You are to be afforded privacy and proper escort when needed."

His gaze shifted to me.

"Lady Rinya, your role is now formally recognized in this process. No one will bar your presence at treaty meetings concerning her marriage."

The words should have felt like victory.

Instead, they felt like another chain fitted more comfortably than the last.

I bowed anyway.

"Thank you, Your Majesty."

The audience broke.

Seralyth left first, skirts cutting a hard line through the doorway. Pattin followed with his tablets and sealed inks. Valtar spoke briefly to Aelyon in a low tone I could not hear, then departed through a side passage.

That left the three of us alone in the thinning morning light.

No throne. No court.

Just truth waiting where protocol had been.

Aelyon looked at me before Kaelira, and I understood why.

"You asked yesterday how I expected trust after lying," he said. "You were right to ask."

I folded my arms, more to steady myself than to challenge him.

"Then answer now."

He exhaled slowly.

"My first reason was survival. My second was proof. I needed to know whether either of you would speak to a nameless traveler the way you spoke to princes and nobles." A tired edge entered his voice. "Most people do not."

I could not deny it entirely.

"And the third reason?" I asked.

His expression changed, some of the guarded court polish falling away.

"Cowardice," he said. "If I told you who I was, everything between us would have become political in a single moment. I wanted, for a little while, to be seen without the crown attached."

Kaelira watched him quietly.

"And now?"

"Now there is no without." He gave a short, humorless smile. "Now there is only what I am, and whether you can endure it."

I studied him, searching for the easy charm he used on the road, the careful half-truths, the places where a practiced liar leaves space to retreat.

What I found was exhaustion and resolve, set side by side.

It did not make me trust him. But it made him legible.

"You do not get to be our rescue and our secret at the same time," I said.

"I know."

"If you hide something that can hurt her, and I learn it from someone else, I will stand against you in front of the whole court."

His jaw tightened, then he nodded.

"Understood."

Kaelira stepped closer to both of us, as if physically narrowing the distance might make the next words less heavy.

"I cannot promise affection," she told him. "Not yet. I can promise honesty, if you return it."

Aelyon bowed, not theatrically this time, only once, deeply.

"Then honesty it is."

We left the chamber together, though not side by side.

In the corridor beyond, servants were already moving faster than before, carrying bolts of cloth, silver inventories, floral charts, lists of invited houses. The palace had accepted the new date and immediately begun dressing the decision in gold.

As Kaelira and I turned toward the inner court, the Veil between us settled into something steadier than fear.

Not peace.

But shape.

For the first time since entering Veylan, we had asked for terms and heard yes.

That should have comforted me.

Yet when I looked back once over my shoulder, I saw Queen Seralyth at the far end of the gallery, half-hidden behind a carved pillar, watching us go with a stillness that felt colder than anger.

Twelve days, I thought.

In this palace, twelve days was enough time for a wedding.

It was also enough time for someone to decide peace had become inconvenient.
