Chapter 37

# Chapter 37

The battle did not last much longer.

Once Sir Alvis and the rest of our knights crashed into the fray, the attackers began to falter. Those who had seemed so confident a moment ago were suddenly scrambling, stumbling, and then running. Even the dark cloaked figures, the ones who had waited on the ridge, turned sharply and retreated into the trees.

Within minutes, the final clangs of steel faded. Victory settled over the clearing like a sudden breath of warm air.

Kaelira and I stepped cautiously from our hiding place beneath the fallen stone. The moment we were in the open, everything seemed brighter, louder. Relief burst in shouts around us.

Knights embraced one another. Pattin and Sedrick leaned against each other, exhausted but grinning.

And Therrin,

Therrin looked stunned.

Not because he doubted the win, but because help had come at all. His eyes stayed fixed on the armored figures dismounting nearby, as if he needed to make sure they were real. When Sir Alvis strode toward us, Therrin let out a shaking breath he’d clearly been holding for far too long.

I felt it, relief, pride, and something gentler flickering beneath.

He had kept us safe until help arrived.

That mattered more to him than the victory.

Sir Alvis reached us at once. He nearly scooped me up like a child, stopping only at the last second, his hands hovering awkwardly in the air. “Are you hurt? Are you well?”

“I… I am fine,” I said, unsure whether my voice was truly steady.

He turned, bowed deeply to Kaelira, and repeated the same question. She dipped her head with quiet dignity. “We are unhurt. Thank you.”

Around us, the rest of the party began celebrating, checking bodies, and making sure no wounded enemy remained. Deren stomped on a fallen attacker’s weapon for extra measure. Marek laughed sharply, tension spilling out of him all at once. Serin leaned on his spear, breathing hard but smiling.

Therrin joined us then. Sir Alvis grasped his forearm firmly. “Your courage kept them alive.

Elaria is in your debt.”

Therrin just nodded, jaw tight, eyes bright with relief and exhaustion.

Then Therrin frowned. “Where is Prince Eryndor? Where is the rest of the caravan?”

Sir Alvis exhaled slowly. “Once Nerron delivered the news, we sent out search riders at once, though Prince Eryndor remained with the main caravan for safety. This morning, we heard rumors from travelers leaving the inn. We followed the trail and found you.”

Sir Alvis exhaled heavily. “Good thing we did… otherwise,” He stopped himself.

Otherwise, we would be dead. The air grew heavier for a heartbeat.

Then Sir Alvis turned his attention toward the masked fighters who had helped us. “Those three. Who are they?”

Only then did I remember.

The masks and the moment Judew’s face had been revealed.

I stepped forward, heart pounding. “You are hiding something,” I said, sharper than I intended.

Judew lifted his head. His expression barely shifted, but something wary flickered in his eyes.

Before he could answer, Therrin stepped in front of me, protective, steady. “Easy,” he murmured. “These gentlemen saved our lives. Without them, none of us would be standing.”

He was right.

But the truth still hung in the air. “The fighters knew you,” I said softly. “They feared you. Who are you really?”

Judew hesitated for only a moment.

Before he could speak, Sedrick stepped forward quickly. “We need to leave,” he said urgently.

“They saw your face. It isn’t safe for us here anymore. We should go now.”

Judew nodded once, a decision closing over him. He did not answer my question. Instead, he turned and walked toward Kaelira.

When he spoke, his voice carried a warmth I had not expected.

“My lady,” he said, bowing his head gently, “it has been an honor to stand in your presence.

I only wish I could remain at your side to see you safely to your destination. But I fear our paths must part here.”

Kaelira’s eyes widened in surprise. She looked almost… disappointed.

Judew smiled faintly. "Take heart. You are not alone any longer. And I pray our paths cross again soon."

He bowed deeper, then took Kaelira's hand with surprising gentleness. He lifted it to his lips and pressed a soft kiss to her knuckles. Kaelira froze, breath catching, her blush blooming in full.

"Farewell, my fair lady," he murmured against her skin.

Then he released her, turned with his mask half torn, blood along his cheek, and walked back toward his waiting companions.

Kaelira and I watched him go, the wind tugging at our hair, the aftermath of battle humming around us. Through the Veilbond, I felt a tremor of what she felt: confusion, a flicker of admiration she did not fully understand, a strange sadness at his leaving, and beneath it all a fragile, quivering relief that we were still alive. And then I noticed her cheeks warming with a shy blush, a soft flutter in her stomach that echoed faintly into mine, delicate and breath-light, as if her emotions brushed gently along my own. Her feelings mingled with mine like warm currents, until I could not tell where hers ended and mine began.

A dozen questions burned in my chest.

But he gave us no answers.

And our journey was far from over.

---

As Judew and his companions disappeared into the trees, a lingering silence followed them.

Their departure left not just relief behind, but a question that hovered in the air like mist. Who were they truly? What name, what past, what danger had made the guards pale at the sight of Judew’s face?

But as quickly as the thought formed, it began to feel unimportant compared to the urgency of the moment.

Our knights were already gathering the surviving horses, checking tack and reins, preparing to move. The danger had passed, but not far enough. We were still too close to the place where Veylan’s hired blades had nearly taken us.

Sir Tavren approached and offered his hand. “We should not remain. The prince’s caravan is half a day ahead. If we ride promptly, we will reach them before nightfall.”

Therrin nodded and moved to my side, his presence steady and grounding. “Come,” he said softly. “We ride with the others. It is safer now.”

Kaelira and I exchanged a look. The Veilbond stirred faintly between us, a mix of exhaustion, fear, and the smallest flicker of renewed hope. Together we stepped forward toward the waiting horses.

Therrin lifted me into the saddle with a gentleness that made my chest warm, and Sir Alvis helped Kaelira mount behind him. The knights moved with practiced urgency, forming a protective ring around us as the company prepared to depart.

We had survived.

And now, at last, we were returning to the main caravan, back to Prince Eryndor, back to the others, back to safety.

For the moment, that was enough.

---

The march back toward the main caravan was strangely quiet. After everything we had escaped, every rustle of leaves or distant bird call made my heart jump. Yet nothing happened. No shadows emerged from the trees, no arrows whistled past us. For the first time in days, the world felt still.

I breathed in slowly. The calm eased something inside me, though fear lingered beneath it like a fading bruise.

As we crested a low hill, shapes appeared ahead: wagons, banners, dozens of horses. The caravan.

My breath tightened with relief.

Prince Eryndor spotted us long before we reached them. He raised an arm sharply, signaling the entire column to halt. The caravan slowed, wheels grinding to a stop.

By the time we approached, Eryndor was already striding toward us.

He reached Kaelira first, lifting her down from Sir Alvis’s horse as if she were made of spun glass. “Sister,” he said, voice rough with worry, “I should never have let you depart. The plan… it must have been leaked. Someone knew you’d be on that smaller road.”

Kaelira lowered her eyes. “We are safe now.”

Meanwhile, Sir Alric came to my side and helped me dismount. The moment my feet touched the ground, I saw them,

My sister, Serenya.

Lady Elsin.

Lady Maerel.

They rushed toward us with tears already streaking their faces.

Serenya performed the quickest curtsey of her life to the princess, but the very next second she threw her arms around me, hugging me so tightly it forced the air from my lungs.

“Rinya! Rinya, by the gods, are you hurt? Are you whole?”

I tried to answer, but she held me too tightly. Lady Maerel embraced Kaelira gently, whispering soothing words, and Lady Elsin wrapped her arms around both of us in turn.

It was chaotic, messy, overwhelming, and perfect.

In the midst of it all, voices murmured anxiously. The attack. The leak. Someone among us had betrayed the route.

I lifted my head, scanning the gathered faces. And then I saw him.

Cale.

Standing a short distance away with that same smirk tugging at his lips. Not joy, not relief, something else. Something unpleasant.

Relief washed through me for surviving the ambush, but the sight of him tightened my stomach.

For days, I had not needed to deal with him.

Now… I was safe from the attackers. But not from him.

---

“Oh my gods, you need to change, and you need to do it fast.”

Serenya’s voice snapped the moment she pulled back from hugging me. Lady Elsin and Lady Maerel echoed her shock as they took in the state of our dresses.

And they were right.

Kaelira and I looked down.

Our disguise gowns, once passable, were now torn, dust-covered, stained with earth and sweat, and even streaked with dried blood, none of which we had noticed in the frantic escape. The thrill and terror of the fight had pulled us past the point of caring for appearances.

But the ladies around us cared. Deeply.

“In a few hours, we will reach the capital,” Lady Maerel said, voice low but stern. “You cannot arrive looking like this. Both of you, come.”

Before we could protest, the three women and a small flood of maids surrounded us, guiding us, no, dragging us, toward two waiting carriages lined up behind the main wagons.

“Inside, quickly,” Mirna urged, pulling open one of the doors.

All the curtains were already drawn tight, sealing us away from the men outside. Privacy.

Blessed privacy.

Kaelira and I were ushered in, and the maids followed us like a practiced storm.

Hands were everywhere in seconds, loosening laces, untying belts, lifting layers of cloth.

Our dresses, stiff with dirt and travel, were slid off our shoulders. I had not realized how filthy I felt until cool air touched my skin.

The maids exchanged clucks of disapproval.

“Look at this hem, completely ruined.”

“Did they roll down a ravine?”

“Dust in their hair, gods preserve us,”

Kaelira’s cheeks were a soft shade of pink as she murmured, “We… had a difficult morning.”

Serenya gave a gentle laugh. “Clearly. But you will arrive in the capital as the Princess of Elaria and her Veilbound lady in waiting, not escaped bandits. Let us make you clean.”

And so began the whirlwind.

It was almost funny.

There we were, standing in nothing but our underthings, almost bare, while two maids attended each of us, and Kaelira and I looked at one another as if this were the most normal thing in the world. A week ago, we would have died of embarrassment. Now… it barely registered.

“Ouch,” Kaelira hissed as Dina dabbed at a smear of dried blood on her thigh. A thin scratch, likely from brambles or a glancing blade, revealed itself beneath the grime.

“You did not even feel this?” Dina said.

Kaelira shook her head. “There was too much happening.”

Soon enough, the scrubbing slowed. We were not bathed, but we were clean enough, fresh water, travel towels, and determined maids worked small miracles.

Then came the dressing.

The moment soft fabrics brushed my skin, something inside me reacted. A shiver. A bloom of sensation across my shoulders, down my arms. I had almost forgotten how different fine dresses felt compared to the coarse commonfolk gown I had worn the last two days.

I missed that freedom of the commonfolk gowns; the looseness, the ease, the way it let me move and breathe. But the silk and linen gliding over my skin sent tiny bursts of warmth through me. Every piece fastened, every ribbon tied, every fold smoothed awakened some new awareness.

I swallowed lightly.

Kaelira caught my eye, cheeks warming, and I knew she felt the same.
