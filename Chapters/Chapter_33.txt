Chapter 33

We reached Falcrest by late afternoon, the sky already deepening into shades of rose and gray. Lanterns were being lit one by one along the main street, each flame flickering to life as though waking from a long sleep. The place shimmered with color and music and laughter, far brighter and livelier than Weddelreach.

This was a city that breathed celebration.

Therrin muttered something under his breath about too many people, too much noise, too little caution. But even he could not fully dampen the energy around us. Smoke from street vendors drifted through the air, mixed with the smell of pastries and roasted nuts. Children darted through crowds, faces painted with bright pigments.

We were not riding anymore. Therrin had insisted we walk the last stretch into the city to avoid drawing attention, so our horses trailed behind us on loose reins. It made us blend in better with the evening crowds. Two young women walking beside three armed men was far less remarkable than the sight of us arriving on horseback.

It did not take long before we saw the gathering.

Just beyond the city’s central square, a dense crowd had formed around a raised wooden platform. Laughter rolled outward in waves. Someone was performing.

Therrin frowned immediately. “We should keep moving. No reason to linger.”

But Kaelira had already pulled her horse to a stop. I, too, found myself staring. Something in the laughter drew us in, a kind of loud, careless cruelty that made my stomach twist.

“I want to see,” Kaelira whispered.

“Kaelira…” Therrin warned.

But she was already moving closer to the crowd. And so I followed.

Our three suspicious companions exchanged uneasy looks, but they did not interfere. They knew better than to draw attention.

The four of us, Kaelira, Therrin, Judew, and I, slipped closer until we stood at the back of the crowd.

On stage, a troupe of brightly dressed performers moved with exaggerated gestures. At the center stood a girl, a small, round, rosy-cheeked actress with a golden wig tied in clumsy braids. She was wearing a gown far too tight, stuffed with padding until she appeared swollen and awkward.

Her character was obvious.

“Behold!” cried the narrator, a tall man with a painted mustache. “The new Princess Kaelira of Elaria!”

The crowd roared with laughter as the padded girl curtsied clumsily, nearly falling over.

Kaelira went still beside me.

My heart dropped.

“They are mocking you,” I whispered.

Her hand squeezed mine. Hard.

“And after such a tragedy,” the narrator continued dramatically, “after the death of the real princess, the king scraped together what he could! And therefore, here she is!”

More laughter.

The little actress waddled forward, fluttering her lashes and patting her padded chest. “Oh, mighty prince of Veylan,” she said in a squeaky falsetto, “will you accept poor me as your bride?”

The laughter doubled.

A man portraying the Veylan crown prince entered with heavy, stomping steps. He wore thick muscles drawn on his sleeves and a crude metal crown. He puffed out his chest and raised his chin grandly.

“Never!” he bellowed. “I, Crown Prince Jaelric of Veylan, will not marry a creature who was once a man!”

The crowd howled so loudly the wooden stage shook.

Another actor stepped forward from behind the fake king, dressed in a too-large military coat as if drowning in it. “Father,” he squeaked, “I beg you, do not make me wed her!”

The narrator announced with flourish, “Behold, Prince Maedric, second son of Veylan, condemned by his own father to take the ugly creature in marriage!”

The crowd roared at the boy’s exaggerated trembling and teary-eyed pleading.

Behind us, I heard two short bursts of laughter. Pattin and Sedrick. They quickly stifled themselves when Judew shot them a look sharp enough to cut stone. Both fell silent, shifting uncomfortably before slipping away into the edges of the crowd, unable to keep watching.

Onstage, the play only grew crueler.

The fake king stomped forward again. "Enough! My sons must honor the alliance! But alas, our mighty Crown Prince Jaelric cannot stomach the sight of her without fainting!" The actor portraying Jaelric dramatically collapsed backward, waving his arms like a dying bird, which sent another round of laughter through the audience.

The narrator shouted, "Fear not! The princess has talents beyond measure!" He gestured grandly.

The padded Kaelira waddled to a cauldron placed center-stage and began stirring it with exaggerated effort, grunting as if the spoon weighed more than her. "Look! I can cook! I can cook!" she repeated in a shrill voice.

A performer dressed as a kitchen servant appeared, sniffed the pot, gagged loudly, and collapsed on the ground. "Poison! She made poison again!" he wailed, kicking dramatically.

The crowd erupted.

Next came a scene meant to mimic diplomacy: the fake princess attempted a curtsy to a fake ambassador but tripped, tumbling down the steps and crashing into him. The ambassador fell onto a pile of props, sending fruit and plates flying.

"She destroys everything she touches!" shouted a voice from the audience, adding to the chorus of laughter.

Kaelira flinched beside me.

And then the worst part began.

Beside the fat girl portraying Kaelira, another actress stumbled onto stage, a lanky woman with a tangled brown wig, painted freckles, and a tattered dress. Padding had been shoved into the wrong places, making her look shapeless and absurd.

“And here,” declared the narrator gleefully, “is the Veilbound companion!”

My breath froze.

The actress tripped immediately, falling face-first into the dirt. The crowd erupted again.

“She is so stupid she cannot even walk!” shouted one man.

“Fit only for the castle guards!” another laughed. “All of them!”

The actress on stage flopped onto her back and thrust her legs upward crudely. “Take turns, boys!” she hollered.

Laughter. Cheers. Whistles.

Kaelira’s hand in mine began to shake.

Heat rushed to my face, my chest, my throat. I could not breathe. My vision blurred at the edges.

Therrin stepped forward, jaw tight. “We are leaving,” he said.

But neither Kaelira nor I moved.

We were frozen.

Watching ourselves turned into a joke.

A cruel, vulgar story twisted for entertainment.

“We should go,” Therrin repeated.

Kaelira took a trembling breath. “I… just… a little longer.”

I nodded weakly. My feet refused to move.

On stage, the fake princess sobbed loudly and pitched herself at the fake prince’s feet, wailing, “Please, mighty prince, take me! I can cook! I can clean! I can be anything you want!”

The actor brushed her away with a broomstick. “Begone, you useless creature!” he shouted.

The audience laughed so hard someone fell over.

Kaelira whispered, “They hate us.”

“No,” I said. “They do not know us.”

“But they think they do.”

There was no reply I could give.

At last, Therrin gripped both our arms and drew us back. "Enough," he said. "This place is poison. We are finding an inn and staying out of sight." What struck me was not the movement itself, but the change it revealed.

A week ago, he would never have been able to pull us so easily. Now, he wrapped his hands fully around our arms without effort. We had become slender, delicate, far too easy to guide. The strength, the muscle, the solidity we once had were simply gone. And with him so close, the height difference felt sharper than ever, as though we had shrunk into smaller versions of ourselves. I could only imagine how tiny we must seem next to Judew, who towered even above Therrin.

Only when we turned away did I feel air return to my lungs. The laughter behind us continued as if nothing in the world mattered more.

We walked in silence through the torchlit streets, the sounds of mockery fading but not leaving us. Not truly.

For the first time since we left home, Kaelira looked small. And perhaps I had not truly registered it before. But after nearly being violated, and after watching that cruel play made of us, the truth finally settled in my bones. We were so very far from safety, and maybe now more than ever, I should have realized it.
