Chapter 4

The duels were held the next day. There wasn’t much time to prepare. The Council didn’t want this to drag on. The ritual had to begin soon.

The first fight was between Therrin and Cale.

They were given time to select their weapons and armor. Cale chose a long-handled spear with a narrow blade and light leather armor for speed. Therrin, unsurprisingly, picked a sword. He always did. He wore heavier mail than Cale, more protection, less movement, but I knew from experience, he didn’t need to move much to win.

We stood along the edge of the stone ring, watching. Everyone silent.

In truth, I wanted Therrin to win. Not just because he was my friend, though that mattered.

The sooner he was out, the better, for both of us. I didn’t want to face him in the second round. But there was another reason, one I didn’t like to admit: I didn’t like Cale. I didn’t know why. There was something about him that felt... off. I’d trained with him, spoken with him, but I never got the sense he was fighting for the same reasons we were.

The bell rang.

Cale moved first, fast, darting in and jabbing low with his spear. Therrin stepped back smoothly, turned the blade aside, and countered with a hard strike toward Cale’s side. Cale dodged, but not fast enough. The tip of Therrin’s sword caught the edge of his armor and tore the leather clean across.

They circled again.

Therrin pressed forward. Cale danced around him, using reach to his advantage. He was clever.

But Therrin was solid. Patient. When Cale lunged again, Therrin let the spear pass, closed the gap, and brought the pommel of his sword up into Cale’s ribs.

Cale staggered.

Therrin didn’t give him the chance to recover. He swept Cale’s legs out from under him, dropped a knee to his chest, and pressed the blade lightly to his throat.

The bell rang again.

Cale coughed, then nodded. "Yield."

The match was over sooner than everyone expected.

Therrin had won.

The second fight was mine.

Nerran stood across from me in the center of the ring. He looked eager, focused, maybe even a little overconfident. He always had something to prove. I didn’t. I just wanted to survive.

We were both given the same choices: weapon and armor. Nerran took a broad-bladed axe and strapped on heavy vambraces. I chose twin short swords, lighter, faster, familiar. My armor was simpler than his, more suited for speed than brute force.

The crowd around us quieted.

The bell rang.

He came at me like a storm, straightforward, hard, fast. His axe cleaved the air with raw strength, and I barely got my blades up in time to deflect the first hit. The force of it sent a jolt through my arms.

I stepped back. Sidestepped. Let him overreach. He was powerful, yes, but power meant nothing if it couldn’t land.

He swung again, wide and reckless. I ducked under it and sliced a shallow cut along his side.

The crowd murmured, but I didn’t pause to look.

Nerran roared and charged. I dodged. Let him waste his strength.

But he adapted quickly. On his third pass, he feinted left and hooked his foot behind my ankle. I hit the ground hard, and he was on me in a second.

I rolled, bringing a blade up to keep him back. It cut his arm, not deep, but enough.

We both scrambled up. Breathing hard now.

The fight became slower, tighter. More measured. Each of us circling, searching.

I parried another blow and kicked him in the knee. He staggered. I struck again, low, then high. He blocked, barely. But I could see it, he was wearing me down.

I misjudged the next swing. His axe caught my side, even through the armor. Pain flared. I dropped one blade.

He rushed forward, and I tried to step aside, but my balance faltered. He caught me in the chest with the flat of his weapon and knocked me to the ground.

The bell rang. My blade was gone. His axe hovered over me.

"Yield," I said.

I closed my eyes for a second, chest heaving. Nerran offered me a hand, and I took it.

The match was over.

I had lost.

There was a strange look in Nerran’s eyes as he helped me up. As if he wanted to say sorry but didn’t know how, or maybe wasn’t allowed to. It wasn’t smugness. It wasn’t pride. Just a quiet apology, sitting unspoken between us.

-----------------------------

Then came the last fight.

Cale and me.

From watching his match with Therrin, I could tell he wasn’t in top form. I’d never trained with him seriously, not more than a few light rounds. We weren’t close. But I remembered the way he moved in the last fight, cautious, slow. I had a chance.

And yet... the pressure was worse than anything I had felt before. My chest was tight with dread. This wasn’t just a duel. This was the end of the path for one of us.

My father stood by the edge of the training hall. General Maeren. His face was stone, but I could feel his support. Beside him stood my younger brother. He never came to things like this. He rarely showed interest in what I did. His presence unsettled me more than it comforted.

My mother and sister had wanted to attend, but my father said no. He told them it was a private matter. He wasn’t wrong. These duels were kept from the court and public. Only a handful of people even knew they were happening. The risk of sabotage, especially from Veylan, was too great. Other kingdoms might even encourage it. War between Elaria and Veylan would serve many interests.

I tried not to dwell on that.

Therrin gave me a few final words before I stepped in. "Don't overthink it," he said. "He's fast, but you're sharper when you're calm. Fight your fight, not his."

We entered the ring.

This time, I chose a different weapon, one I hadn’t trained with as much, but something I hoped would give me an edge: a curved blade and a buckler. Cale stuck with what he knew: his spear.

The bell rang.

We moved slowly at first. Testing. Feeling. My heartbeat was loud in my ears.

Cale lunged. I caught the spear on my shield and knocked it aside, countering with a quick slash. He stepped back.

Again. Thrust. Block. Step.

The fight dragged. Longer than either of us expected. Neither one of us gaining the upper hand for long.

I could feel the edge slowly shifting in my favor. He was slower than the first round. I landed a solid hit to his shoulder. Another to his thigh.

Then a hard strike with the flat of my blade against his ribs. He sat down hard in the ring, one hand clutched to his side.

I thought it was over.

But then something changed.

He looked up at me, sweat dripping down his face, eyes hollow, and then suddenly sprang forward with renewed speed.

His attacks came faster. Sharper. I blocked and dodged, but I was on the defensive.

He pushed me back. Again and again. I stumbled.

My arms ached. My shield arm went numb. He struck low, then high, a feint that nearly disarmed me.

He was winning now.

But I refused to lose.

I closed my eyes for half a breath. Focused. Dug deep.

And when I opened them, I waited. Deflected. Stepped inside. Let him exhaust himself.

His breathing became ragged. His steps uncertain.

He raised his spear for another lunge, and then collapsed.

Just fell.

I rushed to him, but the fight was over. The bell rang. He didn’t rise.

I had won.

I didn’t believe it. I had won.

I was about to win, then almost lost, but in the end... I had won.
